    Section 4.2 Signed Borel measures on \(\mathbb{R}\) and functions of bounded variation

    In Section~\ref{sec: borel on R} we studied the Borel measures on closed intervals in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) In particular, we showed that if \(\mu\) is a Borel measure on closed interval \([a,b]\) then the cumulative distribution function for \(\mu\) is non-negative, increasing, and right-continuous, Theorem 3.3.6 Conversely, if \(g\) is a non-negative, increasing, right-continuous function on \([a,b]\) then \(g\) uniquely determines a Borel measure on \([a,b]\) whose cumulative distribution function is \(g\text{,}\) Corollary 3.3.10. In this section we want to study the relationship between signed Borel measures on closed intervals and functionso on \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We begin with a definition to formalize our language.
    🔗

    Definition 4.2.1.

    Let \(X\) be a topological space and let \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) be the \(\sigma\)-algebra of Borel sets of \(X\text{.}\) A signed measure \(\nu\) on \((X,\mathcal{B}(X))\) is a signed Borel measure if \(|\nu(K)|<\infty\) for all compact sets \(K \subseteq X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 4.2.2.

    If \(\nu\) is a signed Borel measure on \((X,\mathcal{B}(X))\) and \(X\) is compact, then \(|\nu(X)|< \infty\text{.}\) It follows that \(|\nu|(X) < \infty\text{.}\) In particular, if \(\nu\) is a signed Borel measure on the closed interval \([a,b]\) then \(\nu\) is of bounded variation.
    🔗
    🔗
    When studying Borel measures on \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) the functions that arose were increasing. For signed measures, the functions that we will need to study are functions of bounded variation. We define the necessary terms now.
    🔗

    Definition 4.2.3.

    Let \(f \colon [a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be a function. If \(P = \{a= x_0 < \ldots < x_n =b\}\) is a partition of \([a,b]\) the variation of \(f\) over \(P\) is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V(f,P) = \sum_{k=1}^n |f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})|. \end{equation*}
    The total variation of \(f\) is defined as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} TV(f) = \sup \{ V(f,P) \colon P \text{ a partition of }[a,b]\}. \end{equation*}
    When \(TV(f)<\infty\) then \(f\) is of bounded variation.
    🔗
    🔗
    The next example, whilst simple, is very important.
    🔗

    Example 4.2.4.

    If \(f \colon [a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is monotone (i.e. \(f\) is increasing or decreasing), then \(f\) is of bounded variation. Indeed, if \(f\) is increasing and \(P = \{a= x_0 < \ldots < x_n = b\}\) is any partition of \([a,b]\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V(f,P) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty |f(x_i) -f(x_{i-1})| = f(b) - f(a). \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(TV(f) = f(b)-f(a)\text{.}\) If \(f\) is decreasing then we can show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} TV(f) = f(a) - f(b). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Intuitively, the total variation of a function \(f\) can be seen as a measure of the oscillation of \(f\text{:}\) the more oscillation the higher the value of \(TV(f)\text{.}\) Monotone functions, as in Example 4.2.4 do not oscillate and so have bounded variation. To find examples of functions which are not of bounded variation we look at functions with a lot of oscillation.
    🔗

    Example 4.2.5.

    Define \(f\) on \([0,1]\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases} \sin(\frac{1}{x}) & x \neq 0 \\ 0 & x = 0.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Then \(f\) is not of bounded variation. See Exercise 4.3.10.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.2.6.

    Define \(g\) on \([0,1]\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = \begin{cases} x\sin(\frac{1}{x}) & x \neq 0 \\ 0 & x = 0.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Then \(g\) is not of bounded variation. See Exercise 4.3.11.
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that Example 4.2.6 shows that there are continuous functions which are not of bounded variation, see Exercise 4.3.11. However the continuous functions which are of bounded variation are bountiful, as the next proposition shows.
    🔗

    Proposition 4.2.7.

    Let \([a,b]\) be a closed interval in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) If \(f,g \colon [a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) are functions of bounded variation then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \alpha f + \beta g \end{equation*}
    is of bounded variation for all \(\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(P = \{a=x_0 < \ldots < x_n =b\}\) be a partition of \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} V(f+g, P) &= \sum_{k=1}^n |f(x_i) + g(x_i) - f(x_{i-1}) - g(x_{i-1})| \\ &\leq \sum_{k=1}^n|f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})| + \sum_{k=1}^n|g(x_i) - g(x_{i-1})|. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} TV(f+g) \leq TV(f) + TV(g) < \infty. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(f+g\) is of bounded variation.
    🔗
    Take any scalar \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} V(\alpha f,P) = \sum_{k=1}^n|(\alpha f)(x_i) - (\alpha f)(x_{i-1})| & = |\alpha|V(f,P)|. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(TV(\alpha f, P) = |\alpha|TV(f,P) < \infty\text{.}\) The result follows.
    🔗
    🔗
    Knowing that increasing functions have bounded variation (Example 4.2.4) and that the functions of bounded variation form a vector space (Proposition 4.2.7), gives many functions which are of bounded variation. Examples include the sine function (Exercise 4.3.12), the cosine function (Exercise 4.3.13), and polynomials (Exercise 4.3.14).
    🔗
    The following lemma gives a partial relationship between Borel signed measures and functions of bounded variation.
    🔗

    Lemma 4.2.8.

    Let \(\nu\) be a Borel signed measure on the closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Define a function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \nu([a,x]). \end{equation*}
    Then \(f\) is the difference of two increasing functions right-continuous. In particular, \(f\) is right-continuous and of bounded variation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(\nu = \nu_+ - \nu_-\) be the Jordan decomposition of \(\nu\text{.}\) Let \(f_1\) be the cumulative distribution of \(\nu_+\text{,}\) and let \(f_2\) be the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_-\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f(x) &= \nu([a,x]) \\ &= \nu_+([a,x]) - \nu_-([a,x])\\ &= f_1(x) - f_2(x). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    By Theorem 3.3.6, \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are increasing, right-continuous functions. By Example 4.2.4 \(f_1\) and \(f_2\) are of bounded variation, and so \(f\) is of bounded variation by Proposition 4.2.7.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 4.2.9.

    An alternate way to prove \(f(x) = \nu([a,x])\) is of bounded variation in Lemma 4.2.8 would be to invoke Proposition 4.1.13 directly.
    🔗
    🔗
    To complete the picture of the relationship between functions of bounded variation and Borel measures of bounded variation, we need another description of functions of functions of bounded variation.
    🔗
    Let \(f\) be a function of bounded variation on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then, for each \(x \in [a,b]\text{,}\) the restriction of \(f\) to \([a,x]\) is also of bounded variation. Define the function \(T_f\) on \([a,b]\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T_f(x) = TV(f|_{[a,x]}) \end{equation*}
    We call \(T_f\) the function of total variation for \(f\). It is straightforward to see that \(T_f\) is a non-negative, increasing function.
    🔗

    Theorem 4.2.10. Jordan Decomposition Theorem.

    Let \(f\) be a function of bounded variation on the interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then there are increasing functions \(h,g\) on \([a,b]\) such that \(f = g -h\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take \(a\leq c < d \leq b\text{.}\) Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} TV(f|_{[c,d]}) \geq |f(c) - f(d)|. \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} (T_f + f)(d) - (T_f + f)(c) &= f(d) - f(c) + TV(f|_{[c,d]})\\ &\geq f(d) - f(c) + |f(d) - f(c)| \\ & \geq 0, \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} (T_f - f)(d) - (T_f - f)(c) &= TV(f|_{[c,d]}) - f(d) + f(c) \\ &\geq |f(d) - f(c)| - f(d) + f(c) \\ &\geq 0. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence, the functions \(T_f + f\) and \(T_f - f\) are increasing functions. Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g = \frac{1}{2}(T_f + f) \text{ and } h = \frac{1}{2}(T_f - f). \end{equation*}
    Then \(f = g - h\text{,}\) and so \(f\) is the difference of two increasing functions.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 4.2.11.

    Let \(f\) be a function of bounded variation. Let \(g = \frac{1}{2}(T_f + f)\) and \(h = \frac{1}{2}(T_f - f)\text{.}\) Then \(f = g - h\) is called the Jordan decomposition of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 4.2.12.

    Note that for a function of bounded variation
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} TV(f) &= T_f(b) = \frac{1}{2}(T_f + f)(b) + \frac{1}{2}(T_f - f)(b) \\ &= TV(\frac{1}{2}(T_f + f)) + TV(\frac{1}{2}(T_f - f)). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus, the total variation of \(f\) is given by the sum of the total variations of the Jordan decomposition of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Recall that for a signed measure \(\nu\text{,}\) the total variation of \(\nu\) is given by \(|\nu|(X) = \nu_+(X) + \nu_-(X)\text{,}\) where \(\nu = \nu_+ - \nu_-\) is the Jordan decomposition of \(\nu\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 4.2.13.

    Let \(f\) be a function of bounded variation on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) If \(f\) is a right-continuous function then \(T_f\) is right-continuous. In particular, there are right-continuous increasing functions \(g, h\) such that \(f = g- h\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose that \(f\) is right-continuous. Take \(x_0 \in [a,b)\text{.}\) Let \(\alpha = \lim_{x \rightarrow x_0^+} T_f(x) - T_f(x_0)\text{.}\) Since \(T_f\) is increasing, \(\alpha \geq 0\text{.}\) We want to show that \(\alpha = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Fix \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) By the right-continuity of \(f\) and definition of \(\lim_{x\rightarrow x_0^+}T_f(x)\text{,}\) there exists \(\delta > 0\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f(x_0+h) - f(x_0)|< \varepsilon \end{equation*}
    and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T_f(x_0+h) - \lim_{x\rightarrow x_0^+}T_f(x) < \varepsilon \end{equation*}
    when \(0<h<\delta\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Fix \(h\) with \(0<h<\delta\text{.}\) Choose a partition
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P = \{x_0 < x_1 < \ldots < x_n = x_0 + h\} \end{equation*}
    so that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \sum_{k=1}^n|f(x_k) - f(x_{k-1})| &\geq \frac{3}{4}TV(f|_{[x_0,x_0+h]})\\ &= \frac{3}{4}(T_f(x_0+h) - T_f(x_0))\\ &\geq \frac{3}{4}(\lim_{x\rightarrow x_0^+}T_f(x) - T_f(x_0))\\ &\geq \frac{3}{4}\alpha. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \sum_{k=2}^n |f(x_k)-f(x_{k-1})| &\geq \frac{3}{4}\alpha - |f(x_1) - f(x_0)|\\ &> \frac{3}{4}\alpha - \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Now take a partition of \([x_0,x_1]\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} Q = \{x_0 =t_0 < t_1 < \ldots < t_m=x_1\} \end{equation*}
    such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \sum_{k=1}^m|f(x_k) - f(x_{k-1})| \geq \frac{3}{4}TV(f|_{[x_0,x_1]}). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \sum_{k=1}^m|f(x_k) - f(x_{k-1})| &\geq \frac{3}{4}TV(f|_{[x_0,x_1]})\\ &= \frac{3}{4}(T_f(x_1) - T_f(x_0)) \\ &\geq \frac{3}{4}(\lim_{x\rightarrow x_0^+}T_f(x) - T_f(x_0)) \\ & = \frac{3}{4}\alpha. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Noting that \(P \cup Q\) is a partition of \([x_0,x_0+h]\text{,}\) we have that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \alpha + \varepsilon &> \left(\lim_{x\rightarrow x_0^+}T_f(x) - T_f(x_0)\right) + \left(T_f(x_0+h) - \lim_{x\rightarrow x_0^+}T_f(x)\right)\\ &= T_f(x_0 + h) - T_f(x_0) \\ &\geq \sum_{k=1}^m|f(t_k) - f(t_{k-1})| + \sum_{j=2}^n|f(x_j) - f(x_{j-1}| \\ &> \frac{3}{4}\alpha + \frac{3}{4}\alpha - \varepsilon\\ &= \frac{3}{2}\alpha - \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\frac{\alpha}{2} > 2 \varepsilon\text{,}\) or \(\alpha < 4 \varepsilon\text{.}\) As this holds for all \(\varepsilon>0\text{,}\) it follows that \(\alpha = 0\) and so \(T_f\) is right-continuous.
    🔗
    The Jordan decomposition of \(f\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f = \frac{1}{2}(T_f + f) - \frac{1}{2}(T_f - f) \end{equation*}
    shows that \(f\) is the difference of two increasing, right-continuous functions.

    Theorem 4.2.14.

    Let \(\mu\) be a signed Borel measure on \([a,b]\) be a signed Borel measure on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f(x) = \nu([a,x])\) is a right-continuous function of bounded variation.
    🔗
    Conversely, if \(f\) is a right-continuous function of bounded variation on \([a,b]\text{,}\) then there is a unique Borel signed measure \(\mu\) on \([a,b]\) such that \(\mu([a,x]) = f(x)\) for all \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The first part of the theorem is dealt with in Lemma 4.2.8.
    🔗
    Let \(f\) be a right-continuous function of bounded variation on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Let \(f = g - h\) be the Jordan decomposition of \(f\text{.}\) By Proposition 4.2.13, \(g\) and \(h\) are right-continuous. By Corollary 3.3.10, there are Borel measures \(\nu_1\) and \(\nu_2\) on \([a,b]\) so that \(g\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_1\) and \(h\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_2\text{.}\) Let \(\nu = \nu_1 - \nu_2\text{.}\) Then \(\nu\) is a signed Borel measure on \([a,b]\text{,}\) and for any \(x \in [a,b]\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \nu([a,x]) &= \nu_1([a,x]) - \nu_2([a,x]) \\ &= g(x) - h(x) \\ &= f(x).\qedhere \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Theorem 4.2.14 gives a correspondence between right-continuous functions of bounded variation, and signed Borel measures on \([a,b]\text{.}\) We have a Jordan decomposition for functions of bounded variation, and we have a Jordan decomposition for signed measures. We will now show that the correspondence given in Theorem 4.2.14 sends the Jordan decomposition for functions to the Jordan decomposition for the signed measures, and vice versa.
    🔗

    Proposition 4.2.15.

    Let \(f\) be a right-continuous function of bounded variation on the closed interval \([a,b]\text{,}\) and let \(\nu\) be the signed Borel measure satisfying \(\nu([a,x]) = f(x)\) from Theorem 4.2.14. Let \(f = f_1 - f_2\) be the Jordan decomposition for \(f\) and \(\nu = \nu_+ - \nu_-\) be the Jordan decomposition for \(\nu\text{.}\) Then \(f_1\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_+\) and \(f_2\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_-\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Conversely, if \(\nu\) is a signed measure on \([a,b]\) with Jordan decomposition \(\nu = \nu_+- \nu_-\text{.}\) If \(f_1\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_+\) and \(f_2\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_-\text{,}\) then, up to the addition of a constant to \(f_1\) and \(f_2\text{,}\) \(f = f_1 -f_2\) is the Jordan decomposition of the function \(f(x) = \nu([a,x])\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In both cases, we get that the total variation of \(\nu\) is equal to the total variation of \(f\text{,}\) i.e. \(|\nu|([a,x]) = TV(f)\text{,}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(f\) be a right-continuous function of bounded variation and let \(\nu\) be the signed Borel measure satisfying \(\nu([a,x]) = f(x)\) (which exists by Theorem 4.2.14). Let \(f = f_1 - f_2\) be the Jordan decomposition of \(f\text{,}\) and \(\nu = \nu_+ - \nu_-\) be the Jordan decomposition of \(\nu\text{.}\) By Corollary 3.3.10, there are Borel measures \(\nu_1\) and \(\nu_2\) on \([a,b]\) so that \(\nu_1((c,d]) = f_1(d) -f_1(c)\) and \(\nu_2((c,d]) = f_2(d) -f_2(c)\) for intervals \((c,d] \subseteq [a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note that \(\nu = \nu_1 - \nu_2\) and so, by Proposition 4.1.14,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} |\nu|([a,b]) &\leq \nu_1([a,b]) + \nu_2([a,b]) \\ &= f_1(b) - f_1(a) + f_2(b) - f_2(a) \\ &= TV(f). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Let \(P\) be a partition of \([a,b]\text{,}\) by Proposition 4.1.13,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} V(f,P)\leq |\nu|([a,b]). \end{equation*}
    Thus \(TV(f) \leq |\nu([a,b])\text{.}\) Hence \(TV(f) = |\nu|([a,b])\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(\nu\) be a signed Borel measure on \([a,b]\text{,}\) and define \(f(x) = \nu([a,x])\) for \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is right-continuous and of bounded variation by Theorem 4.2.14, Let \(\nu = \nu_+ - \nu_-\) be the Jordan decomposition of \(\nu\text{.}\) Define functions \(f^+1\) and \(f^-\) on \([a,b]\) by \(f^+(x) = \nu_+([a,x])\) and \(f^-(x) = \nu_-([a,x])\text{.}\) By Theorem 3.3.6, \(f^+\) and \(f^-\) are increasing, right continuous functions. Since \(\nu = \nu_+ - \nu_-\text{,}\) it follows that \(f = f^+ - f^-\text{.}\) Let \(f_1 = \frac{1}{2}(T_f + f)\) and \(f_2 = \frac{1}{2}(T_f - f)\) be the Jordan decomposition of \(f\text{.}\) By Corollary 3.3.10, there are Borel measures \(\nu_1\) and \(\nu_2\) whose cumulative distribution functions are \(f_1\) and \(f_2\text{,}\) respectively.
    🔗
    Note that \(\nu = \nu_1 - \nu_2\text{.}\) By Proposition 4.1.14 for each \(x \in [a,b]\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} |\nu|([a,x]) &\leq \nu_1([a,x]) + \nu_2([a,x]) \\ &= (f_1(x) - f_1(a)) + (f_2(x) - f_2(a)) \\ &= \frac{1}{2}[T_f(x) + f(x) - T_f(a) - f(a) + T_f(x) - f(x) - T_f(a) + f(a)]\\ &= T_f(x). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    By Proposition 4.1.13, \(T_f(x) \leq |\nu|([a,x])\) for all \(x \in X\text{,}\) and thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T_f(x) = |\nu|([a,x]). \end{equation*}
    We have now,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} f(x) + T_f(x) &= f^+(x) - f^-(x) + |\nu|([a,x]) \\ &= f^+(x) - f^-(x) + (f^+(x) - f^+(a)) + (f^-(x) - f^-(a))\\ &= 2f^+(x) - f^-(a) - f^+(a) \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^+ = \frac{1}{2}(T_f + f) + C, \end{equation*}
    where \(C = \frac{1}{2}(f^+(a) + f^-(a))\text{.}\) It follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^- = \frac{1}{2}(T_f -f) + C.\qedhere \end{equation*}
