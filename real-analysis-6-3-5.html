    Section 11.3 Locally compact Hausdorff spaces

    As all continuous functions on \([a,b]\) are necessarily bounded, \(C([a,b])= C_b([a,b])\) and \(C([a,b])\) is a Banach space with the \(\sup\)-norm, see Example Example 10.1.10. In Section Section 11.4 we will describe the dual space of \(C([a,b])\text{.}\) We will do this by provingt the (more general) Riesz-Markov-Kakutani Theorem. We begin with some topological preliminaries.
    🔗
    In this section we will review some topological facts. Most will be presented without proof. Details can be found in [6] or [12] (and umpteen more texts).
    🔗
    Let \(X\) be a topological space. If \(x \in X\text{,}\) a set \(A \subseteq X\) is a neighborhood of \(x\) if \(x \in A\text{,}\) and there is an open set \(U \subseteq X\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x \in U \subseteq A. \end{equation*}
    Take \(K \subseteq X\text{.}\) A collection of open sets \(\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}\) is an open cover of \(K\) if \(K \subseteq \bigcup_{\alpha \in A}\text{.}\) The set \(K\) is compact if given any open cover \(\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}\) of \(K\text{,}\) there is a finite subcover. That is, there are \(U_1,\ldots,U_n \in \{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}\) such that \(K \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^n U_k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The following result gives a useful alternative characterization of compactness.
    🔗

    Proposition 11.3.1.

    Let \(X\) be a topological space. The following are equivalent:
    🔗
    1. \(X\) is compact;
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(\{F_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}\) is a collection of closed sets satisfying \(\bigcap_{k=1}^nF_k \neq \emptyset\) for any \(F_1,\ldots,F_n \in \{F_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A}\text{,}\) then
      \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{\alpha\in A}F_\alpha \neq \emptyset. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Given a collection of topology spaces \(\{X_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in A}\) we can form the Cartesian product \(Y = \prod_{\alpha \in A}X_\alpha\text{.}\) The product topology on \(Y\) is the topology generated by the sets \(\prod_{\alpha \in A}U_\alpha\) where, for each \(\alpha \in A\text{,}\) \(U_\alpha \subseteq X_\alpha\) is an open set, and \(U_\alpha = X_\alpha\) for all but finitely many \(\alpha\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 11.3.2. Tychonoff’s Theorem.

    Let \(\{X_\alpha\}_{\alpha\in A}\) be a collection of topological spaces, let \(Y = \prod_{\alpha \in A}X_\alpha\) with the product topology. If \(X_\alpha\) is compact for all \(\alpha \in A\text{,}\) then \(Y\) is compact.
    🔗
    🔗
    Tychonoff’s Theorem is a non-trivial result. In fact, it can be shown that Tychonoff’s Theorem is equivalent to the axiom of choice.
    🔗
    Recall that \(X\) is Hausdorff if given \(x,y\in X\) with \(x \neq y\text{,}\) there exist open sets \(U,V \subseteq X\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x \in U,\ y \in V,\ \text{ and }U \cap V = \emptyset. \end{equation*}
    Compact Hausdorff spaces are very nice, but if we restrict to compact topological spaces we lose too many important examples. We instead restrict to locally compact spaces:
    🔗

    Definition 11.3.3.

    Let \(X\) be a topological space. The space \(X\) is locally compact if every point \(x\in X\) has a compact neighborhood.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.3.4.

    Every compact space is locally compact.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.3.5.

    The real line \(\mathbb{R}\) is locally compact and Hausdorff. Indeed, for any \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) the set \([x-1,x+1]\) is a compact neighborhood of \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) More generally, \(\mathbb{R}^n\) is locally compact and Hausdorff. Indeed, if \(x \in \mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) then the neighborhood \(K = \{y \in \mathbb{R}^n \colon \|x-y\|_2\leq 1\}\) is compact by the Heine-Borel theorem.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.3.6.

    Every metric space is Hausdorff. However, not every metric space is locally compact. In particular, a Banach space \(V\) is locally compact if and only if \(V\) is finite dimensional.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.3.7.

    Let \(X\) be a topological space with the discrete topology, i.e. every subset of \(X\) is open. Then \(X\) is Hausdorff and locally compact. Indeed, if \(x \in X\text{,}\) then \(\{x\}\) is a compact neighborhood of \(x\text{.}\) If \(X\) has an infinite cardinality, then \(X\) is locally compact, but not compact.
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(X\) be a locally compact Hausdorff space. We denote by \(C(X)\) the space of all continuous real-valued functions on \(X\text{.}\) We denote by \(C_b(X)\) the space of all bounded continuous real-valued functions on \(X\text{.}\) The space \(C_b(X)\) is a normed-vector space with the \(\sup\)-norm
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|f\|_\infty = \sup_{x\in X} |f(x)|. \end{equation*}
    A function \(f \in C(X)\) vanishes at infinity if, for all \(\varepsilon>0\text{,}\) there is a compact set \(K\text{,}\) such that \(|f(x)|<\varepsilon\) for all \(x \in X \backslash K\text{.}\) Denote by \(C_0(X)\) the space of all continuous functions on \(X\) which vanish at infinity.
    🔗
    A function \(f \in C(X)\) is compactly supported, or of compact support, if there is a compact set \(K \subseteq X\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in X \colon h(x) \neq 0\} \subseteq K. \end{equation*}
    When \(f\) is compactly supported, we call the closure of \(\{x \in X \colon h(x) \neq 0\}\) in \(X\text{,}\) the compact support of \(f\). We denote by \(C_c(X)\) the space of all continuous compactly supported functions on \(X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    These definitions are consistent with the definitions in Example Example 10.1.10, Example Example 10.1.11, and Example Example 10.1.12. We have that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C_c(X) \subseteq C_0(X) \subseteq C_c(X). \end{equation*}
    Both \(C_b(X)\) and \(C_0(X)\) are Banach spaces with the \(\sup\)-norm. In general, \(C_c(X)\) is not a Banach space. The smallest Banach subspace of \(C_b(X)\) containing \(C_c(X)\) is \(C_0(X)\text{.}\) That is, \(C_0(X)\) is the closure of \(C_c(X)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    When we work with the real line, we can often find continuous functions that take desired values on certain sets. We have, for example, does this with piecewise linear functions many times throughout these notes. When working with a more general topological space, it is not clear that we can always find these nice continuous functions. The following proposition shows that for locally compact Hausdorff spaces, we can, at times, find continuous functions to do what we want.
    🔗

    Proposition 11.3.8. Urysohn’s Lemma.

    Let \(X\) be a locally compact Hausdorff space. Let \(K \subseteq X\) be compact and \(U \subseteq X\) be open such that \(K \subseteq U \subseteq X\text{.}\) There exists a continuous, compactly supported function \(f \colon X \rightarrow [0,1]\) such that \(f(x) = 1\) for all \(x \in K\) and \(f(x) = 0\) for all \(x \in X \backslash U\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    What we have stated here is actually just a special case of Urysohn’s Lemma. When working in a metric space, the next example shows that the proof of Urysohn’s Lemma is straightforward.
    🔗

    Example 11.3.9.

    Suppose \(X\) is a metric space with metric \(d\) (not necessarily locally compact), and that \(K \subseteq U \subseteq X\) with \(K\) closed and \(U\) open. For any closed set \(F \subseteq X\text{,}\) let \(d_F\) be the function on \(X\) defined by \(d_F(x) = \inf\{d(x,y)\colon y \in F\}.\) For each closed \(F\text{,}\) it can be shown that \(d_F\) is a continuous function. Now, define a function \(f\) on \(X\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{d_{X\backslash U}(x)}{d_{X\backslash U}(x) + d_K(x)}. \end{equation*}
    Then \(f\) is a continuous function from \(X\) to \([0,1]\) satisfying \(f(x) = 1\) for \(x \in K\text{,}\) and \(f(x) = 0\) for \(x \in X\backslash U\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 11.3.10.

    Let \(X\) be a locally compact Hausdorff space, let \(K \subseteq X\) be a compact subset, and let \(\{U_1,\ldots,U_n\}\) be an open cover for \(K\text{.}\) A collection of continuous functions \(h_1,\ldots,h_n\) from \(X\) to \([0,1]\) are partition of unity for \(K\) subordinate to \(U_1,\ldots,U_n\) if
    🔗
    1. \(h_i \in C_c(X)\) for \(1\leq i \leq n\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\sum_{i=1}^n h_i(x) = 1\) for all \(x \in K\text{;}\) and
      🔗
      🔗
    3. the compact support of \(h_i\) is a subset of \(U_i\) for \(1\leq i \leq n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    The following result is an application of Urysohn’s Lemma.
    🔗

    Proposition 11.3.11.

    Let \(X\) be a locally compact Hausdorff space, \(K \subseteq X\) be compact, and \(\{U_1,\ldots,U_n\}\) be an open cover of \(K\text{.}\) There is a partition of unity for \(K\) subordinate to \(U_1,\ldots,U_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(X\) be a locally compact Hausdorff space. We denote by \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) the Borel sets in \(X\text{.}\) That is, \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) is the smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra of subsets of \(X\) containing all the open subsets of \(X\text{.}\) Recall that a measure \(\mu\) on the measurable space \((X,\mathcal{B}(X))\) is a Borel measure if \(\mu(K)<\infty\) for all compact sets \(K\subseteq X\text{.}\) We will be primarily interested in a special type of Borel measure.
    🔗

    Definition 11.3.12.

    Let \(X\) be a locally compact Hausdorff space and let \(\mu\) be a Borel measure on \((X,\mathcal{B}(X))\text{.}\) Take \(E \in \mathcal{B}(X)\text{.}\) The measure \(\mu\) is outer regular on \(E\) if
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu(E) = \inf\{\mu(U) \colon E \subseteq U,\ U \text{ open}\}, \end{equation*}
    and inner regular on \(E\) if
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu(E) = \sup\{\mu(K) \colon K \subseteq E,\ K \text{ compact}\}. \end{equation*}
    The measure \(\mu\) is a Radon measure if \(\mu\) is outer regular on all Borel sets, and inner regular on all open sets.
    🔗
    A signed Borel measure \(\nu\) on \((X,\mathcal{B}(X))\) is a Radon signed measure if the measure of total variation \(|\nu|\) is a Radon measure.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.3.13.

    Let \(m\) be the Lebesgue measure on the Borel subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) By Theorem Theorem 5.1.8, \(m\) is a Radon measure.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.3.14.

    Let \(\mu\) be a Borel measure on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) By Lemma Lemma 9.3.10, \(\mu\) is outer regular on all Borel sets. By Exercise Exercise 11.5.12, \(\mu\) is inner regular on open subsets \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) and hence \(\mu\) is a Radon measure.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 11.3.15.

    Let \(X\) be a locally compact Hausdorff space, let \(x \in X\) be any pointy, and let \(\delta_x\) be the Dirac measure at \(x\) on the Borel sets \(\mathcal{B}(X)\text{.}\) Then \(\delta_x\) is a Radon measure.
