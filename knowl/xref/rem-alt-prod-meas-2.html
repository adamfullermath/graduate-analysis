Remark 1.1.14.

We note in Remark 1.1.4, that some people only define the product measure to act on the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the measurable rectangles. Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces, let \(\mu \times \nu\) be the product measure on \(X\times Y\text{,}\) and let \(\pi\) be the restriction of \(\mu\times \nu\) be the restriction of \(\mu \times \nu\) to the \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{F}\) generated by the measurable rectangles. The analogue of Fubini’s Theorem

for \(\pi\) states: if \(f\) is integrable with respect to \(\pi\text{,}\) then the \(x\)-sections \(f_x\) are integrable with respect to \(\nu\) for all \(x \in X\text{,}\) and
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_{X\times Y}{f}\,d{\pi} &= \int_{X}{\left(\intd{Y}\,d{f(x,y)}{\nu(y)}\right)}{\mu(x)}\\ &= \int_{Y}{\left(\intd{X}\,d{f(x,y)}{\mu(x)}\right)}{\nu(y)}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
The key differences between Fubini’s Theorem for \(\mu \times \nu\) (our version) and for \(\pi\) are
  1. we do not need to assume that \(\nu\) or \(\mu\) is a complete measure to prove Fubini’s Theorem for \(\mu\times\nu\text{;}\)
    🔗
  2. we have that \(f_x\) is \(\nu\)-integrable for all \(x\in X\text{,}\) not just for almost all \(x \in X\text{,}\) in Fubini’s Theorem for \(\pi\text{;}\) and
    🔗
  3. we are assuming that the measure spaces are \(\sigma\)-finite in Fubini’s Theorem for \(\pi\text{.}\)
    🔗
In practice, concerns over these differences rarely matter. For ‘nice’ functions and ‘nice’ \(\sigma\)-algebras one usually has that
\begin{equation*} \int_{X\times Y}{f}\,d{(\mu\times\nu)} = \int_{X\times Y}{f}\,d{\pi}. \end{equation*}
