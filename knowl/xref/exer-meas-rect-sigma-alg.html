Exercise 4.

Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces and let \(\mathcal{F}\) be the \(\sigma\)-algebra of subsets of \(X \times Y\) generated by the measurable rectangles. In this exercise we will prove, for all \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{,}\) that the map \(x \mapsto \nu(E_x)\) is measurable and
\begin{equation} (\mu\times \nu)(E) = \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu.\tag{1.3} \end{equation}
We will do this over several parts.
🔗
For \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{,}\) define a map \(\Phi_E\) on \(X\text{,}\) by \(\Phi_E(x)= \nu(E_x)\text{.}\) Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be all the measurable rectangles in \(X \times Y\text{;}\) let \(\mathcal{S}'\) be all finite unions of disjoint sets in \(\mathcal{S}\text{;}\) let \(\mathcal{C}\) be the smallest collection of subsets containing \(\mathcal{S}\) which is closed under countable unions and countable intersections; and let \(\mathcal{G}\) be the collection of sets \(E \in \mathcal{F}\) for which \(\Phi_E\) is measurable and (1.3) holds.
  1. Note that if \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{,}\) then the \(x\)-section \(E_x\) is measurable for all \(x \in X\text{.}\) Hence \(\Phi_E\) is defined on all \(x \in X\text{.}\) (HINT: This is already done in Exercise 1.4.3).
    🔗
  2. Show that \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{G}\text{.}\) (HINT: This is already done in Lemma 1.1.6 when \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{.}\) What needs to change in the argument if \((\mu\times\nu)(E)=\infty\text{?}\)).
    🔗
  3. Show that \(\mathcal{G}\) is closed under countable unions.
    🔗
Until we state otherwise, we will now assume that \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) are finite measure spaces.
🔗
  1. Show that \(\mathcal{G}\) is closed under countable intersections.
    🔗
  2. Conclude that \(\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{G}\text{.}\)
    🔗
For \(E \in \mathcal{C}\) let
\begin{equation*} \mathcal{C}(E) = \{ F \in \mathcal{C} \colon E \cap F,\, E\backslash F,\, F \backslash E \in \mathcal{C}\}. \end{equation*}
  1. Show that \(\mathcal{S}' \subseteq \mathcal{C}\text{.}\)
    🔗
  2. Show that for each \(E \in \mathcal{C}\text{,}\) \(\mathcal{C}(E)\) is closed under countable unions and countable intersections.
    🔗
  3. Show that if \(E \in \mathcal{S}'\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{S}' \subseteq \mathcal{C}(E)\) (HINT: Recall Lemma II.2.3.13). Conclude that \(\mathcal{C}(E) = \mathcal{C}\text{.}\)
    🔗
  4. Show that if \(F \in \mathcal{C}\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{C}(F)= \mathcal{C}\text{.}\) (Consider the symmetry in definition of \(\mathcal{C}(E)\) and part~\ref{part: meas rect sigma-alg}.)
    🔗
    🔗
  5. Show that \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed under complements, finite unions, and finite intersections.
    🔗
    🔗
  6. Show that \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed under countable unions. Conclude that \(\mathcal{C}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.
    🔗
    🔗
  7. Conclude that \(\mathcal{C} = \mathcal{F}\text{,}\) and hence \(\mathcal{G} = \mathcal{F}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
  8. Assume now that \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) are \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces. Show that \(\mathcal{G} = \mathcal{F}\text{.}\)
