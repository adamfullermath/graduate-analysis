Lemma 1.1.9.

Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be measure spaces with \(\nu\) a complete measure. If \(E\subseteq X\times Y\) is \((\mu\times\nu)\)-measurable with \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{,}\) then the \(x\)-sections \(E_x\) are \(\nu\)-meaurable for almost all \(x\in X\text{,}\) and the function \(x \mapsto \nu(E_x)\) is a \(\mu\)-measurable function. Further,
\begin{equation*} (\mu\times\nu)(E) = \int_{X} \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x). \end{equation*}
🔗
🔗

Proof.

Since \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{,}\) by Proposition II.2.3.15 there is an \(\mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\)-set \(A\subseteq X\times Y\) such that \(E \subseteq A\) and \((\mu\times\nu)(E) = (\mu\times\nu)(A)\text{.}\) Thus \((\mu\times\nu)(A\backslash E)=0\) by the excision property.
🔗
By Lemma 1.1.6, \(A_x\) is measurable for all \(x\in X\text{,}\) and \((A\backslash E)_x\) is measurable for almost all \(x\in X\text{.}\) For each \(x\in X\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} E_x = A_x \backslash (A\backslash E)_x. \end{equation*}
Thus, for almost all \(x\in X\text{,}\) \(E_x\) is \(\nu\)-measurable and
\begin{equation*} \nu(A_x) = \nu(E_x) + \nu((A\backslash E)_x)= \nu(E_x). \end{equation*}
Applying Lemma 1.1.6 again, we have
\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} (\mu\times \nu)(E) &= (\mu\times \nu)(A)\\ &= \int_X\nu(A_x)\,d\mu \\ &= \int_X\nu(E_x)\,d\mu. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
