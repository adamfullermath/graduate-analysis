Example 1.3.3.

For \(n\geq 2\text{,}\) let \(f_n\) be the function on \([0,1]\) given by
🔗
\begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases}n^2 x &x \in[0,\frac{1}{n}],\\ -n^2x+2n &x\in[\frac{1}{n},\frac{2}{n}], \\ 0 & x\in[\frac{2}{n},1].\end{cases} \end{equation*}
🔗
The sequence \((f_n)_n\) converges pointwise to \(0\text{.}\) Also
🔗
\begin{equation*} \int_{[0,1]}f_n\,dm = \frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{2}{n}\cdot{n}=1 \end{equation*}
and so each \(f_n \in L^1([0,1],m)\) with \(\|f_n\|_1 = 1\text{.}\) Hence \((f_n)_{n\geq 2}\) does not converge to \(0\) in \(1\)-norm.
🔗
Note that, the sequence \((f_n)_{n\geq 2}\) can not converge to anything in \(1\)-norm. Indeed, if \((f_n)_{n\geq 2}\) converges in \(1\)-norm to some \(f \in L^1([0,1],m)\text{,}\) then by Corollary 1.3.2, a subsequence would converge pointwise a.e. to \(f\text{.}\) Since \((f_n)_{n\geq 2}\) converges pointwise to \(0\text{,}\) any subsequence converges pointwise to \(0\text{,}\) and so we must have \(f=0\text{.}\)
