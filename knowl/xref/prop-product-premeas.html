Proposition 1.1.2.

Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be measure spaces; let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the measurable rectangles in \(X\times Y\text{;}\) and let \(\mu \times \nu\) be the extended real-valued function on \(\mathcal{S}\) defined by
\begin{equation*} (\mu\times\nu)(A\times B) = \mu(A)\nu(B) \end{equation*}
for all \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) and \(B \in \mathcal{N}\text{.}\) Then \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring, and \(\mu \times \nu\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\)
🔗
🔗

Proof.

Recall, from Exercise II.2.4.14, that the collection \(\mathcal{S}\) of all measurable rectangles in \(X \times Y\) forms a semiring of sets.
🔗
Note that \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{S}\text{,}\) and \((\mu\times\nu)(\emptyset)=\mu(\emptyset)\nu(\emptyset)=0\text{.}\) To show \(\mu\times \nu\) is a premeasure it remains to show that \(\mu\times\nu\) is finitely additive and countably monotone.
🔗
Suppose \(A \times B\) is a measurable rectangle such that
\begin{equation*} A\times B = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k \times B_k, \end{equation*}
where \(\{A_k \times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are disjoint measurable rectangles. For a fixed \(x \in A\text{,}\) we have
\begin{equation*} B = \bigcup_{k \colon x \in A_k} B_k. \end{equation*}
Note that, if \(x \in A_{k_1}\) and \(x \in A_{k_2}\text{,}\) then since \(A_{k_1}\times B_{k_1}\) and \(A_{k_2}\times B_{k_2}\) are disjoint, \(B_{k_1}\) and \(B_{k_2}\) are disjoint. Thus, the collection \(\{B_k\}_{\{k \colon x \in A_k\}}\) are a disjoint collection of measurable sets is in \(Y\text{,}\) and hence
\begin{equation*} \nu(B) = \sum_{k \colon x \in A_k} \nu(B_k) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu(B_k)\chi_{A_k}(x). \end{equation*}
Hence, for any \(x \in X\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \nu(B) \chi_A(x) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu(B_k)\chi_{A_k}(x). \end{equation*}
Now, by the series version of the Monotone Convergence Theorem, Corollary II.6.1.18,
\begin{equation*} \mu(A)\nu(B) = \int_X \nu(B) \chi_A \,d\mu= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \int_X\nu(B_k)\chi_{A_k}\,d\mu = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(A_k)\nu(B_k). \end{equation*}
Hence, \(\mu\times \nu\) is countably additive, and therefore finitely additive.
🔗
Note that, for measurable rectangles \(A\times B\) and \(C \times D\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} A \times B \subseteq C \times D \end{equation*}
if and only if
\begin{equation*} A \subseteq C \text{ and } B \subseteq D. \end{equation*}
Thus, if \(A \times B \subseteq C \times D\text{,}\) then
\begin{equation*} (\mu\times \nu)(A\times B) \leq (\mu\times \nu)(C\times D). \end{equation*}
Thus, \(\mu\times \nu\) is monotonic.
🔗
Let \(A\times B\) be a measurable rectangle and \(\{A_k\times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) be measurable rectangles, such that
\begin{equation*} A\times B \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k \times B_k. \end{equation*}
Since the measurable rectangles \(\mathcal{S}\) are a semiring, and \(\mu\times \nu\) is finitely additive, we can assume that the sets \(\{A_k \times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are disjoint. Thus
\begin{align*} (\mu\times\nu)(A\times B)\amp= (\mu\times\nu)\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (A\times B)\cap (A_k \times B_k) \right) \\ \amp= \sum_{k=1}^\infty (\mu\times\nu)\left((A\times B)\cap (A_k \times B_k )\right)\\ \amp\leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty (\mu\times\nu)(A_k \times B_k). \end{align*}
Hence \(\mu\times \nu\) is countably monotone, and therefore \(\mu\times \nu\) is a premeasure on the measurable rectangles.
