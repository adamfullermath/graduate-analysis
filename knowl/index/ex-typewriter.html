Example 10.3.5.

For each \(n\geq 1\text{,}\) let
🔗
\begin{equation*} f_n = \chi_{\left[\frac{k}{2^m},\frac{k+1}{2^m}\right]}, \end{equation*}
where \(m\geq 0\) and \(0\leq k < 2^m\) satisfy
🔗
\begin{equation*} n = 2^m +k. \end{equation*}
These functions are sometimes called the typewriter functions or dancing functions. Explicitly, we have
🔗
\begin{equation*} f_1 = \chi_{[0,1]},\ f_2 = \chi_{[0,1/2]},\ f_3=\chi_{[1/2,1]}, f_4 = \chi_{[0,1/4]}, f_5 =\chi_{[1/4,1/2]},\ldots \end{equation*}
If \(n = 2^m +k\) as above,
🔗
🔗
\begin{equation*} \int_{[0,1]}f_n\,dm = \frac{1}{2^m}. \end{equation*}
Thus \(f_n \in L^1([0,1],m)\) and \((f_n)_n\) converges to \(0\) in \(1\)-norm. However, \((f_n)_n\) does not converge pointwise (nor pointwise a.e.) to any function. Indeed, for any \(x \in [0,1]\) there are infinitely many \(n\) such that \(f_n(x) = 0\) and infinitely many \(n\) such that \(f_n(x) =1\text{.}\)
