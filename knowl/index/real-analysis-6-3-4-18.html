Remark 2.2.5.

Theorem 2.2.4 shows that in order for the dual of \(L^1(X,\mu)\) to be (isometrically isomorphic to) \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{,}\) it is sufficient that \(\mu\) be \(\sigma\)-finite. But is it necessary that \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite? The proof relies on two key steps: first we show in Lemma 2.2.2, that if \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, then the map \(g \mapsto T_g\) is a norm preserving map from \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\) to \((L^1(X,\mu))^*\text{;}\) then in Theorem 2.2.4 we show that the map is surjective.
🔗
You will notice, that the proof of Lemma 2.2.2 does not fully rely on the fact that \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite. Indeed, the property we use is that if \(\mu(A)>0\text{,}\) then there is a \(B \subseteq A\) with \(0<\mu(B)<\infty\text{.}\) Measure spaces with this property are called semi-finite. Whilst all \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces are semi-finite, not all semi-finite measure spaces are \(\sigma\)-finite. E.g., if \(X\) is an uncountable set and \(c\) is the counting measure on all the subsets of \(X\text{,}\) then \((X,P(X),c)\) is semi-finite but not \(\sigma\)-finite.
🔗
The proof of surjectivity in Theorem 2.2.4 also does not tell the whole story. In order for the map \(g \mapsto T_g\) to be surjective, a necessary and sufficient condition is that the measure space is localizable. Other than to say that being localizable is a stronger condition than being semi-finite, but a weaker condition than being \(\sigma\)-finite, we will say no more on the topic. We refer the interested reader to the treatise of Fremlin [7].
🔗
In lieu of a more in-depth discussion on the dual of \(L^1(X,\mu)\) when \(\mu\) is not \(\sigma\)-finite, we refer the reader to Exercise 2.5.3 and Exercise 2.5.4. In Exercise 2.5.3, an example is given of a measure space which is not \(\sigma\)-finite, but the dual of \(L^1(X,\mu)\) is \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{.}\) In Exercise 2.5.4, an example is given where the dual of \(L^1(X,\mu)\) is not \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{.}\)
