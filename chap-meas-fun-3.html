    Section 7.1 Measurable functions

    We first need to decide on what class of functions it makes sense to consider. These will be called measurable functions. Given a set \(X\text{,}\) a function \(f\) from \(X\) to \(\mathbb{R}\) is called a real-valued function; a function from \(f\) to the extended reals \(\mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty, \infty\}\) extended real line is an extended real-valued function. We will be interested in extended real-valued functions. The following theorem is necessary before the definition of a measurable function.
    🔗

    Theorem 7.1.1.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space. Let \(E \in \mathcal{F}\) and let \(f\) be an extended real-valued function with domain \(E\text{.}\) Then the following are equivalent:
    🔗
    1. for all \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) \(\{x \in E \colon f(x) > \alpha \} \in \mathcal{M}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. for all \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) \(\{x \in E \colon f(x) \geq \alpha \} \in \mathcal{M}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. for all \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) \(\{x \in E \colon f(x) < \alpha \} \in \mathcal{M}\text{;}\) and
      🔗
      🔗
    4. for all \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) \(\{x \in E \colon f(x) \leq \alpha \} \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in E \colon f(x) > \alpha \}^c = \{x \in E \colon f(x) \leq \alpha \}, \end{equation*}
    and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in E \colon f(x) < \alpha \}^c = \{x \in E \colon f(x) \geq \alpha \}. \end{equation*}
    As the \(\sigma\)-algebra is closed under taking complements, (i) is equivalent to (iv), and (ii) is equivalent to (iii).
    🔗
    Assume that (i) holds and take any \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) For each \(n \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) the set \(\left\{x \in E \colon f(x) > \alpha - \frac{1}{n}\right\}\) is in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) As a countable intersection of measurable sets is measurable it follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in E \colon f(x) \geq \alpha\} = \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty\left\{x \in E \colon f(x) > \alpha - \frac{1}{n}\right\} \end{equation*}
    is measurable. Hence (ii) holds.
    🔗
    Finally, assume (ii) holds and take any \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) For each \(n \in \mathbb{N}\text{,}\) the set \(\left\{x \in E \colon f(x) \geq \alpha + \frac{1}{n}\right\}\) is in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) As a countable union of measurable sets is measurable it follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in E \colon f(x) > \alpha\} = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty\left\{x \in E \colon f(x) \geq \alpha + \frac{1}{n}\right\} \end{equation*}
    is measurable. Hence (i) holds.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 7.1.2.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space, \(E \in \mathcal{M}\text{,}\) and \(f \colon E \rightarrow [-\infty,\infty]\) be an extended real-valued function. Then \(f\) is a measurable function with respect to \(\mu\) if \(f\) satisfies any of the equivalent conditions from Theorem Theorem 7.1.1. If there is no chance of confusion on what the \(\sigma\)-algebra is, we will simply say that \(f\) is a measurable function.
    🔗
    If \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) is a measure space, and \(f\) is measurable with respect to \(\mathcal{F}\) we also say \(f\) is measurable with respect to \(\mu\text{,}\) or \(\mu\)-measurable.
    🔗
    If \(f\) is a measurable function with domain \(E\text{,}\) and there is a measurable set \(F \subseteq E\) such that \(\mu(F)<\infty\) and \(f(x) = 0\) for all \(x \in E \backslash F\text{,}\) then \(f\) is finitely supported.
    🔗
    🔗
    Over the next several results we will examples of functions which are measurable. We begin with a simple example. Let \(X\) be a set and let \(A \subseteq X\text{.}\) We define the characteristic function of \(A\), \(\chi_A\text{,}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \chi_A(x) = \begin{cases}1 &\text{if }x\in A\\0& \text{if }x \notin A. \end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Characteristic functions will play an important role in our study of measurable functions. The proof of the following proposition is left as an exercise, Exercise Exercise 7.3.3.
    🔗

    Proposition 7.1.3.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space, and take \(A \subseteq X\text{.}\) The characteristic function \(\chi_A\) is a measurable function if and only if \(A\) is a measurable set.
    🔗
    🔗
    We now give an alternative characterization of a measurable real-valued function.
    🔗

    Proposition 7.1.4.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space.
    🔗
    If \(f\) is an extended real-valued measurable function on \(X\text{,}\) then \(f^{-1}(U)\) is measurable for all open \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(f\) is a real-valued function and \(f^{-1}(U)\) is measurable for all open \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then \(f\) is a measurable function.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(f\) be a measurable extended real-valued function on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then \(f^{-1}((-\infty,b))\) and \(f^{-1}((a,\infty))\) are measurable sets for all \(a,b \in \mathbb{R}.\) Thus, if \(a<b\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^{-1}((a,b)) = f^{-1}((a,\infty)) \cap f^{-1}((-\infty,b)) \end{equation*}
    is the intersection of two measurable sets, and hence measurable. That is, if \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is an open interval, then \(f^{-1}(I)\) is measurable. If \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is open, then \(U = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k\) where \(\{I_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are disjoint open intervals. Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^{-1}(U) = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty f^{-1}(I_k) \end{equation*}
    is a countable union of measurable sets, and thus is measurable.
    🔗
    If \(f\) is a real-valued function on \(X\) an \(f^{-1}(U)\) is measurable for open \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) then for all \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in X \colon f(x) > \alpha \} = f^{-1}((\alpha,\infty)) \end{equation*}
    is measurable. Hence \(f\) is a measurable function.
    🔗
    🔗
    Proposition Proposition 7.1.4 immediately gives the following corollary telling us that (in the right settings) continuous functions are measurable functions.
    🔗

    Corollary 7.1.5.

    Let \(X\) be a topological space, let \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) be the \(\sigma\)-algebra of Borel sets in \(X\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal{F}\) be a \(\sigma\)-algebra with \(\mathcal{B}(X) \subseteq \mathcal{F}\text{.}\) Let \(f \colon X \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be a continuous function. Then \(f\) is measurable with respect to \(\mathcal{F}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Since \(f\) is continuous, \(f^{-1}(U)\) is open in \(X\) for all open sets \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Hence, by Proposition Proposition 7.1.4, if \(U\) is an open set in \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f^{-1}(U) \in \mathcal{B}(X) \subseteq \mathcal{F}. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(f\) is measurable with respect to \(\mathcal{F}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Recall that two functions \(f\) and \(g\) on a measure space \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) are equal almost everywhere if there is a measurable \(E \subseteq X\) with \(\mu(E) = 0\) so that that \(f(x) = g(x)\) for all \(x \in X \backslash E\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Proposition 7.1.6.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) be a complete measure space. Let \(f,g\) be functions from a measurable set \(E\) to \(\mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty,\infty\}\text{.}\) If \(f = g\) almost everywhere and \(f\) is a measurable function, then \(g\) is a measurable function.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(E \subseteq X\) be a measurable set with \(\mu(F) = 0\) such that \(f(x) = g(x)\) for all \(x \in X \backslash F\text{.}\) Fix any \(c >0\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \{x \in E \colon g(x) > c \} &= \{x \in E \cap F \colon g(x) > c \} \cup \{x \in E \backslash F \colon g(x) > c\}\\ &= \{x \in E \cap F \colon g(x) > c \} \cup \{x \in E \backslash F \colon f(x) > c\}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in E \backslash F \colon f(x) > c \} = \{x \in E \colon f(x) > c \} \cap F^c \end{equation*}
    is the intersection of two measurable sets, and thus is measurable. Further
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in E \cap F \colon g(x) > c \} \subseteq F \end{equation*}
    thus \(\{x \in E \cap F \colon g(x) > c \}\) is measurable, since \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) is complete. Thus \(\{x \in E \colon g(x) > c \}\) is the union of two measurable sets, and hence is measurable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 7.1.7.

    The completeness of the measure space \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) is necessary in Proposition Proposition 7.1.6, see Example Exercise 7.3.4.
    🔗
    🔗
    The following theorem gives some simple ways of creating measurable functions from measurable functions: by linear combinations and products. Before we start we need to do some mild bookkeeping. If \(f\) and \(g\) are measurable functions, with the same measurable domain \(F\text{,}\) we want to consider their sum, \(f+g\text{.}\) However, if there is an \(x \in F\) such that \(f(x) = \infty\) and \(g(x) = -\infty\text{,}\) then \(f(x) + g(x)\) is undefined. One way to circumvent this issue would be to only consider real-valued measurable functions. However, there will be times when considering functions with infinite values is either unavoidable or desirable.
    🔗
    Instead, we will focus on measurable that are finite almost everywhere. Let \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) be a measure space. A function \(f\) on \(F \subseteq X\) is finite almost everywhere, if there is a \(E\in \mathcal{F}\) with \(\mu(E) = 0\) such that \(|f(x)|<\infty\) for all \(x \in F \backslash E\text{.}\) If \(f\) and \(g\) are two measurable functions with common domain \(F\text{,}\) and \(f\) and \(g\) are finite almost everywhere, then there is a measurable set \(E_1\) such that \(|f(x)|<\infty\) for all \(x \in F \backslash E_1\text{,}\) and there is a set \(E_2\) such that \(|g(x)|<\infty\) for all \(x \in F \backslash E_2\text{.}\) Note that \(\mu(E_1 \cup E_2) = 0\text{.}\) We can thus, define \(f+g\) on \(F \backslash (E_1 \cup E_2)\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x) \text{ for }x \in F \backslash(E_1 \cup E_2). \end{equation*}
    For \(x \in E_1 \cup E_2\) we will assign arbitrary values to \((f+g)(x).\) We thus are not uniquely defining the function \(f+g\text{,}\) we are instead defining \(f+g\) up the equivalence class of equality almost everywhere. That ``equal almost everywhere" forms an equivalence class in functions is covered by Exercise Exercise 7.3.6.
    🔗

    Theorem 7.1.8.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) be a measure space. Let \(f\) and \(g\) be measurable functions with the same domain \(E \in \mathcal{F}\) which are finite almost everywhere. Then
    🔗
    1. for all \(\alpha,\beta \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) there is a \(E_0 \subseteq E\) with \(\mu(E\backslash E_0) =0\) such that \(\alpha f + \beta g\) is measurable on \(E_0\text{;}\) and
      🔗
      🔗
    2. there is a \(E_0 \subseteq E\) with \(\mu(E\backslash E_0) =0\text{,}\) \(fg\) is measurable on \(E_0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    If \(f\) or \(g\) are real-valued, or if \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) is complete, then we can take \(E_0 = E\) in both (i) and (ii).
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    That \(\alpha f\) is a measurable function for \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\) when \(f\) is a measurable function is left to Example Exercise 7.3.7.
    🔗
    Let \(f\) and \(g\) be measurable functions on a measurable set \(E\text{,}\) where both \(f\) and \(g\) are finite almost everywhere. Then there is a measurable set \(E_0 \subseteq E\) such that \(\mu(E \backslash E_0) = 0\) and \(f\) and \(g\) are finite on \(E_0\text{.}\) Fix \(c \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) We want to show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \{ x\in E_0 \colon f(x) + g(x) < c \} \end{equation*}
    is a measurable set. Take \(x_0 \in A\text{.}\) Then \(f(x_0) + g(x_0) < q\text{.}\) By the density of the rationals \(\mathbb{Q}\) in \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) there is a \(q \in \mathbb{Q}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x_0) + g(x_0) < q < c. \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x_0 \in \{x \in E_0 \colon f(x) < q\} \cap \{x \in E_0 \colon g(x) < c - q\}. \end{equation*}
    Conversely, if \(x_0 \in E_0\) and there is \(q \in \mathbb{Q}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} x_0 \in \{x \in E_0 \colon f(x) < q\} \cap \{x \in E_0 \colon g(x) < c - q\}, \end{equation*}
    then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x_0) + g(x_0) < q + (c-q) = c. \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \bigcup_{q\in \mathbb{Q}}[\{x \in E_0 \colon f(x) < q\} \cap \{x \in E_0 \colon g(x) < c - q\}]. \end{equation*}
    Since \(f\) and \(g\) are measurable the sets \(\{x \in E \colon f(x) < q\}\) and \(\{x \in E \colon g(x) < c - q\}\) are measurable for every \(q \in \mathbb{Q}\text{.}\) Hence \(A\) is a measurable set. It follows that \(f+g\) is a measurable function on \(E_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} fg = \frac{1}{2}(f^2 +g^2 - (f-g)^2). \end{equation*}
    Thus, to show that the product of two measurable functions is measurable, it suffices to show that the square of a measurable function is measurable. Let \(f\) be a measurable on \(E\) and take \(c \geq 0\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in E \colon f(x)^2 > c \} = \{x \in E \colon f(x) > \sqrt{c}\} \cup \{x \in E \colon f(x) < -\sqrt{c}\} \end{equation*}
    is a measurable set. If \(c<0\) then \(\{x \in E \colon f(x)^2 > c \}= \emptyset\) is also measurable. Thus \(f^2\) is a measurable function.
    🔗
    If \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) is a complete measure space we can take \(E_0 = E\) in the above cases, by Proposition Proposition 7.1.6.
    🔗
    🔗
    We now introduce an important class of measurable functions: simple functions.
    🔗

    Definition 7.1.9.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space, and let \(\varphi\) be a real-valued measurable function on \(X\text{.}\) The function \(\varphi\) is simple if \(\varphi(X)\) is a finite set.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 7.1.10.

    Let \(\varphi\) be a simple function on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\) and let \(\varphi(X) = \{a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_n\}\text{.}\) For each \(i = 1,\ldots,n\) let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E_i = \{x \in X \colon \varphi(x) = a_i\}. \end{equation*}
    By Exercise Exercise 7.3.1, each set \(E_i\) is a measurable set. Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \varphi = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \chi_{E_i}. \end{equation*}
    Conversely, if \(F_1,F_2,\ldots, F_m\) are measurable sets, and \(b_1,\ldots,b_m\) are real numbers then if
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \psi = \sum_{k=1}^m b_k \chi_{F_k}, \end{equation*}
    \(\psi\) is a measurable function by Theorem Theorem 7.1.8. Further, as \(\psi\) takes finitely many values
     1 
    Note the sets \(\{F_k\}_{k=1}^m\) are not necessarily disjoint, so \(\psi\) may take values other than the values \(b_1,b_2,\ldots,b_m\text{.}\)
    , \(\psi\) is a simple function.
    🔗
    🔗
    The following few results give further ways of constructing measurable functions from measurable functions.
    🔗

    Proposition 7.1.11.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) be a complete measure space and let \(f_1,\ldots,f_n\) be measurable functions on \(X\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    1. \(\max\{f_1,\ldots,f_n\}\) is measurable; and
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\min\{f_1,\ldots,f_n\}\) is measurable.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(f_1,\ldots,f_n\) be measurable functions, and let \(f = \max\{f_1,\ldots,f_n\}.\) Take any \(c \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x \in X\colon f(x) >c\} = \bigcup_{k=1}^n \{x \in \mathcal{X} \colon f_k(x) > c\}. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\{x \in X\colon f(x) >c\}\) is a measurable set, and hence \(f\) is a measurale function.
    🔗
    Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \min\{f_1,\ldots,f_n\} = -\max\{-f_1,-f_2,\ldots,-f_n\}. \end{equation*}
    Hence, \(\min\{f_1,\ldots,f_n\}\) is a measurable function.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 7.1.12.

    For a measurable function \(f\) on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{,}\) \(f_+ = \max\{f,0\}\) is the positive part of \(f\) and \(f_-\) is the negative part of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that, Proposition Proposition 7.1.11 shows that if \(f\) is a measurable function, then \(f_+\) and \(f_-\) is a measurable function. Further, note that \(f = f_+ - f_-\text{.}\) That is, \(f\) is the difference of two non-negative measurable functions. This decomposition, \(f = f_+ - f_-\) will be used frequently when studying integration.
    🔗

    Corollary 7.1.13.

    Let \(f\) be a measurable function on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\) The the function \(|f|\) is measurable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Note that \(|f| = f_+ + f_-.\) Both \(f_+\) and \(f_-\) are measurable, by Proposition Proposition 7.1.11. As \(f_+\) and \(f_-\) are both non-negative functions, \(f_+(x) + f_-(x)\) is defined (possibly infinite) for all \(x \in X\text{.}\) It follows from the same argument as in Theorem Theorem 7.1.8, that \(f_+ + f_-\) is a measurable function.
    🔗
    🔗
    The question of whether the composition of measurable functions is measurable is complicated. In general, the answer is no, Example Example 7.1.14 However, there are circumstances where measurability of a composition is guaranteed, Proposition Proposition 7.1.15.
    🔗

    Example 7.1.14.

    In the proof of Lemma Lemma 5.2.7, we prove the existence of a non-Lebesgue measurable set \(B \subseteq [0,2]\) and a bijective continuous function \(f\colon [0,2] \rightarrow [0,1]\) such that \(f(B)\) is Lebesgue measurable. By a simple adjustment, we can assume that \(f(B) \subseteq (0,1).\) We extend \(f\) to a continuous function on all of \(\mathbb{R}\) by setting \(f(x) = 1\) for \(x \geq 2\) and \(f(x) = 0\) for \(x \leq 0\text{.}\) Let \(A = f(B)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Consider the measure space \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{M})\text{,}\) where \(\mathcal{M}\) is all Lebesgue measurable sets in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) The function \(\chi_A\) is a measurable function since \(A\) is measurable, Exercise Exercise 7.3.3, and \(f\) is measurable since it is continuous, Proposition Corollary 7.1.5. However the function \(g = \chi_A \circ f\) is not measurable. Indeed
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \{x \in \mathbb{R} \colon g(x) > 0 \} &= \{x \in \mathbb{R} \colon \chi_A(f(x)) >0 \} \\ &= \{x \in \mathbb{R} \colon f(x) \in A\} \\ &= B, \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    is not Lebesgue measurable, and hence \(g\) is not a measurable function.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 7.1.15.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space, let \(f\) be a real-valued measurable function on \(X\text{,}\) and let \(\varphi \colon \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be a continuous function. Then \(\varphi \circ f\) is continuous.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take any open set \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) and let \(V = \varphi^{-1}(U)\text{.}\) Then \(V\) is an open subset of \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) since \(\varphi\) is continuous. By, Proposition Proposition 7.1.4, \(f^{-1}(V)\) is a measurable set. Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (f\circ \varphi)^{-1}(U) = f^{-1}(V) \end{equation*}
    is measurable for all open sets \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Hence \(f \circ \varphi\) is a measurable function, by Proposition Proposition 7.1.4.

    Remark 7.1.16.

    We have only considered extended real-valued measurable functions. This is what we will be interested in for the majority of these notes. In more generality, if \((X,\mathcal{M})\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N})\) are two measurable spaces, a function \(f \colon X \rightarrow Y\) is measurable with respect to \(\mathcal{M}\) and \(\mathcal{N}\) if \(f^{-1}(E) \in \mathcal{M}\) for all \(E \in \mathcal{N}\text{.}\) This definition is consistent with Definition Definition 7.1.2 when \(Y = \mathbb{R}\cup\{-\infty,\infty\}\) and \(\mathcal{N} = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty,\infty\})\text{,}\) the Borel sets in the extended real line. See Exercise Exercise 4.4.5 for more on \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty,\infty\})\text{.}\) This fact almost follows from Proposition Proposition 7.1.4. The full details are left to Exercise Exercise 7.3.12.
    🔗
    When we study measurable functions from \(\mathbb{R}\) to \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) we study measurable functions from with respect to \(\mathcal{M}\) and \(\mathcal{B}\text{,}\) \(\mathcal{M}\) is the Lebesgue measurable sets on \(\mathbb{R}\) and \(\mathcal{B}\) is the Borel sets on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) I.e. we study measurable functions from the measure space \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{M},m)\) to the measure space \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{B},m)\text{.}\) The lopsidedness of this naturally raises questions. Firstly, the advantage of the domain being \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{M},m)\) is that \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{M},m)\) is a complete measure space. See, e.g., Proposition Proposition 7.1.6 for an advantage of having a complete measure space in the domain.
    🔗
    One reason for taking the Borel sets in the range, instead of all Lebesgue measurable sets, is the following. As described in the proof of Lemma Lemma 5.2.7 and Example Example 7.1.14, there is a continuous function \(f \colon \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) and a set \(B \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) which is not Lebesgue measurable, such that \(A=f(B)\) is Lebesgue measurable but \(A\) is not a Borel set, and \(f^{-1}(A) = B\text{.}\) I.e. there is a continuous function \(f\) and a Lebesgue measurable set \(A\) such that \(f^{-1}(A)\) is not Lebesgue measurable. In conclusion, the measurable functions from \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{M})\) to \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{M})\) do not include all continuous functions. We do, however, want to study the continuous functions. Hence the measurable functions from \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{M})\) to \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{M})\) is not the right class to study.
