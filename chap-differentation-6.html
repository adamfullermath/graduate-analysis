    Section 9.4 The Lebesgue Decomposition of function

    By the Lebesgue Decomposition Theorem \(\nu = \nu_a + \nu_s\text{,}\) where \(\nu_a \ll m\) and \(\nu_s \perp s\text{.}\) Let \(f_a\) be the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_a\text{,}\) and let \(f_s\) be the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu_s\text{.}\) By Proposition Proposition 9.2.7, \(f_a\) is absolutely continuous. In this section we will further study \(f_s\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 9.4.1.

    Let \(f\) be a continuous function on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is a continuous singular function if \(f'= 0\) a.e.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 9.4.2.

    Let \(\varphi\) be the Cantor-Lebesgue function on \([0,1]\) (see Example Example 5.2.5), let \(C\) be the Cantor set in \([0,1]\) and let \(U = [0,1]\backslash U.\) By the definition of \(\varphi\text{,}\) there are disjoint open intervals \(\{I_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) such that \(U = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k\) such that \(\varphi\) is constant on each \(I_k\text{.}\) It follows that \(\varphi'(x)= 0\) for all \(x \in U\text{.}\) By Example Example 5.2.3, \(m(U) = 1 =m([0,1])\text{.}\) Hence \(\varphi'=0\) a.e., and so \(\varphi\) is a continuous singular function.
    🔗
    Recall that, by Example Example 9.1.2, if \(\nu\) is the Borel measure on \([0,1]\) satisfying \(\varphi((c,d]) = \varphi(d) - \varphi(c)\text{,}\) then \(\nu\) is a singular measure.
    🔗
    🔗
    Example Example 9.4.2 gives an example of a singular function which gives rise to a singular measure. The following theorem shows that this is not a coincidence.
    🔗

    Proposition 9.4.3.

    Let \(f\) be a right-continuous function of bounded variation on a closed interval \([a,b]\) and let \(\nu_f\) be the Borel signed-measure defined by \(\nu_f((c,d]) = f(d) - f(c).\) Then \(\nu_f\) is singular if and only if \(f' =0\) a.e.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Note that, as argued in Corollary Corollary 9.3.12,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0}\frac{\nu_f(E_r(x))}{r} = f'(x), \end{equation*}
    where \(E_r(x)\) is \((x,x+r]\) or \((x-r,x]\text{.}\) From Theorem Theorem 9.3.11, \(\nu_f\) is singular if and only if, for almost all \(x \in [a,b]\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0}\frac{\nu(E_r(x))}{r} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\nu_f \perp m\) if and only if \(f' = 0\) a.e.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 9.4.4. Lebesgue Decomposition Theorem.

    Let \(f\) be a continuous function of bounded variation on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{,}\) then there are unique continuous functions \(f_a\) and \(f_s\) on \([a,b]\) satisying \(f = f_a + f_s\text{,}\) such that
    🔗
    1. \(f_a\) is absolutely continuous; and
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(f_s\) is singular.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The result follows immediately from Theorem Theorem 9.1.10, Proposition Proposition 9.2.7, and Proposition Proposition 9.4.3.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 9.4.5.

    Let \(\delta_0\) be the Dirac measure on the Borel sets of \([-1,1]\text{,}\) centered at \(0\text{.}\) I.e. \(\delta_0(E) = 1\) if \(0 \in E\text{,}\) and \(\delta_0(E)=0\) otherwise. If \(H\) is the cumulative distribution function of \(\delta_0\text{,}\) then \(H\) is not continuous, see Exercise Exercise 5.4.8. Thus Theorem Theorem 9.4.4, does not apply to \(H\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 9.4.6.

    Let \(\nu\) be a Borel measure on \([a,b]\text{,}\) with cumulative distribution function \(f\text{.}\) If \(f\) is continuous, then applying Theorem Theorem 9.1.10 to \(\nu\) gives the same decomposition as applying Theorem Theorem 9.4.4 to \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(f\) is not continuous, we can still apply Theorem Theorem 9.1.10 to \(\nu\) and decompose \(f = f_a + f_s\text{,}\) where \(f_a\) is absolutely continuous, and \(f_s'= 0\) a.e. In this case \(f_s\) will not be continuous.
    🔗
    This leads us to the questions: what Borel measures have continuous cumulative distribution functions? If \(f\) is right-continuous and of bounded variation, how discontinuous can it be?
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 9.4.7.

    Let \(\nu\) be a Borel signed-measure on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(\nu\) is a continuous Borel signed-measure is \(\nu(\{x\}) = 0\) for all \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 9.4.8.

    Let \(\nu\) be a Borel signed-measure on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{,}\) and let \(f(x) = \nu([a,x))\text{.}\) Then \(\nu\) is continuous if and only if \(f\) is continuous and \(f(a) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    By Theorem Theorem 5.3.6, \(f\) is right continuous. Assume that \(f\) is continuous and \(f(a) = 0\text{.}\) Since \(f(a) = 0\text{,}\) \(\nu(\{a\}) = 0\text{.}\) Take any \(x \in (a,b]\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \nu(\{x\}) &= \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}\nu((x-1/n,x]) \\ & = f(x) - \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}f(x-1/n)\\ &= f(x) - f(x) = 0, \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    by the (left) continuity of \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Conversely, suppose \(\nu(\{x\}) = 0\) for all \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f(a) = \nu(\{a\}) =0\text{.}\) If \(x \in (a,b]\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} f(x) - f(x-1/n) = lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \nu((x-1/n,x])=\nu(\{x\}) = 0. \end{equation*}
    Hence \(f\) is left-continuous, and therefore continuous, at \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In order to get a better idea of the singular part of a function of bounded variation, we need to understand to consider not-necessarily-continuous functions of bounded variation. To this end, we need a quick aside (which may or may not be known to you).
    🔗
    Let \(\Lambda\) be an index set, and let \(\{a_\lambda\}_{\lambda \in \Lambda}\) be a collection of real numbers indexed by \(\Lambda\text{.}\) Let \(\mathcal{F}(\Lambda)\) be the collection of all finite subsets of \(\Lambda\text{.}\) Let \(s\) be a real number. We say the series \(\sum_{\lambda \in \Lambda}a_\lambda\) converges to some \(s\) if for all \(\varepsilon >0\) there is an \(F \in \mathcal{F}(\Lambda)\) such that if \(G \in \mathcal{F}(\Lambda)\) with \(F \subseteq G\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left| s - \sum_{\lambda \in G}a_\lambda\right|<\varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    In this case we write \(\sum_{\lambda\in\Lambda}a_\lambda =s\text{.}\)\footnote{If you are familiar with point-set topology you will recognize this convergence as simply convergence of the net of partial sums \(\left(\sum_{\lambda\in F}a_\lambda\right)_{F \in \mathcal{F}(\Lambda)}\text{.}\)}
    🔗
    If each \(a_\lambda \geq 0\text{,}\) and \(\sum_{\lambda \in \Lambda}a_\lambda = s\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} s = \sup\left\{ \sum_{\lambda \in F}a_\lambda \colon F \subseteq \Lambda\text{ finite}\right\}. \end{equation*}
    Further, if \(\Lambda = \mathbb{N}\) is the set of natural numbers, then \(\sum_{\lambda\in\Lambda}a_\lambda = s\) if and only if \(\sum_{n \in \mathbb{N}}a_n\) converges absolutely.
    🔗

    Lemma 9.4.9.

    Let \(\Lambda\) be an index set, and let \(\{a_\lambda\}_{\lambda \in \Lambda}\) be a collection of real numbers indexed by \(\Lambda\text{.}\) If \(\sum_{\lambda \in \Lambda} a_\lambda\) is convergent, then \(\{\lambda \colon a_\lambda \neq 0\}\) is countable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(\Lambda_{\geq 0} = \{\lambda \in \Lambda \colon a_\lambda \geq 0\}\) and \(\Lambda_{< 0} = \{\lambda \in \Lambda \colon a_\lambda < 0\}\text{.}\) Note that \(\sum_{\lambda\in\Lambda}a_\lambda\) is convergent if and only if \(\sum_{\lambda\in\Lambda_{\geq 0}}a_\lambda\) and \(\sum_{\lambda\in\Lambda_{<0}}a_\lambda\) are both convergent. We will thus assume that \(a_\lambda \geq 0\) for all \(\lambda \in \Lambda\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Suppose the set \(\Lambda_0 = \{\lambda \colon a_\lambda \neq 0\}\) is uncountable. Let \(a_0 = \inf_{\lambda \in \Lambda_0} a_\lambda.\) For each \(n\text{,}\) choose \(\lambda_n \in \Lambda_0\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a_0 \leq a_\lambda < a_0 + 1/n. \end{equation*}
    Let \(\Lambda_n = \{\lambda \in \Lambda_0 \colon a_\lambda \geq a_{\lambda_n}\}.\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \Lambda_0 = \bigcup_{n\geq 1} \Lambda_n. \end{equation*}
    As \(\Lambda_0\) is uncountable, it follows that \(\Lambda_n\) is countable for some \(n\text{.}\) Let \(N\) be such that \(\lambda_N \in \Lambda_0\) and the set \(\Lambda_N\) is uncountable.
    🔗
    For each \(m\text{,}\) choose a finite set \(F_m \subseteq \Lambda_N\) with \(m\) elements. Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{\lambda \in F_n} a_\lambda \geq ma_{\lambda_N}. \end{equation*}
    Hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sup_m \sum_{\lambda \in F_m} a_\lambda = \infty, \end{equation*}
    and therefore
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{\lambda \in \Lambda}a_\lambda = \infty. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Theorem 9.4.10.

    Let \(f\) be a function of bounded variation on \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(f\) the set of discontinuities of \(f\) is countable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Assume that \(f\) is increasing. The general result follows from the increasing case by the Jordan Decomposition Theorem. For each \(x \in (a,b)\text{,}\) let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a_x = \lim_{y\rightarrow x^-}f(y)\text{ and } b_x = \lim_{y\rightarrow x^+}f(y). \end{equation*}
    Since \(f\) is continuous at \(x\) if and only if \(a_x = b_x\text{.}\) Further, since \(f\) is increasing, \(b_x \leq a_y\) when \(x<y\text{.}\) The collection of open intervals \(\{(a_x,b_x)\}_{x\in(a,b)}\) is therefore a disjoint collection (viewing \((a_x,b_x)\) as the empty set when \(a_x = b_x\)); and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{x\in(a,b)} (b_x - a_x) \leq f(b) - f(a) < \infty. \end{equation*}
    By Lemma Lemma 9.4.9 it follows that \(a_x = b_x\) for all but at most countably many points. Thus \(f\) the set of discontinuities of \(f\) is countable.
    🔗
    🔗
    The following result offers a sort of converse to Theorem Theorem 9.4.10: for every countable set \(A\) there is an increasing function whose set of discontinuities is \(A\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Proposition 9.4.11.

    Let \([a,b]\subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a closed interval, and let \(A \subseteq [a,b]\) be a countable set. There is an increasing function \(f\) on \([a,b]\) where \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\backslash A\) and discontinuous on \(A\text{.}\) Further, \(f\) can be chosen to be right-continuous.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We will assume that \(A\) is countably infinite. The case when \(A\) is finite is straightforward. Enumerate the points in \(A\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \{x_1,x_2,x_3,\ldots\}. \end{equation*}
    Let \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) be a sequence of positive real numbers such that \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n<\infty\text{.}\) Define a function \(f\) on \([a,b]\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \sum_{n \colon x_n \leq x}a_n. \end{equation*}
    We will show that \(f\) is right-continuous; \(f\) is discontinuous at every point in \(A\text{;}\) and \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\backslash A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We will first prove right-continuity. Take any \(x \in [a,b)\) and fix \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) Suppose there exists \(\delta>0\) such that \((x,x+\delta) \cap A = \emptyset\text{.}\) If \(y>x\) and \(|x-y|<\delta\text{,}\) then \(|f(x) - f(y)| = 0<\varepsilon\text{.}\) Thus \(f\) is right-continuous at \(x\text{.}\) Otherwise, for each \(\eta>0\) the set \((x,x+\eta)\cap A\) is infinite. Fix \(\eta>0\text{.}\) The series
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n\colon x<x_n\leq x+\eta}a_n<\infty. \end{equation*}
    Hence, there is a finite set \(F \subseteq \{n \colon x<x_n\leq x+\eta\}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{n\colon x<x_n\leq x+\eta}a_n - \sum_{n \in F}a_n < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Fix \(\delta>0\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (x,x+\delta) \cap \{x_n \in A \colon n \in F\} = \emptyset. \end{equation*}
    Take \(y>x\) with \(|x-y|<\delta.\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f(y) -f(x)|\leq\sum_{n\colon x<x_n\leq x+\eta}a_n - \sum_{n \in F}a_n < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Hence \(f\) is right-continuous at \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take any \(x_m \in A\text{,}\) and \(y<x_m\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f(x_m) - f(y)|\geq a_m > 0. \end{equation*}
    Hence \(f\) is not continuous at \(x_m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The argument that \(f\) is (left) continuous on \([a,b]\backslash A\) follows similar reasoning as the argument that \(f\) is right-continuous and is left as an exercise (Exercise Exercise 9.5.7).
    🔗
    🔗
    What do non-continuous Borel signed-measures look like? Let \([a,b]\subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a closed interval and let \(x \in [a,b]\) be any point. The Dirac measure at \(x\text{,}\) \(\delta_x\text{,}\) is not a continuous Borel measure, since \(\delta_x(\{x\}) = 1 \neq 0\text{.}\) Clearly, any linear combination of Dirac measures will not be a continuous Borel signed-measure. Hence, there are many Borel measures which are not continuous. Gives a name to measures which are made from Dirac measures.
    🔗

    Definition 9.4.12.

    A non-zero Borel signed-measure \(\nu\) on a closed interval \([a,b]\) is discrete if there is a countable collection of points \(\{x_n\}^{\infty}_n\) and a sequence of real numbers \((a_n)_{n=1}^\infty\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n \delta_{x_n}, \end{equation*}
    where \(\delta_{x_n}\) is the Dirac measure at the point \(x_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 9.4.13.

    Note that, if \(\nu = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\delta_{x_n}\) is a discrete Borel signed-measure, then the series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) is absolutely convergent.
    🔗
    Conversely, if \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\) is an absolutely convergent series, and \(\{x_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of points in a closed interval \([a,b]\text{,}\) then \(\sum_{n} a_n \delta_{x_n}\) is a discrete Borel signed-measure on \([a,b]\text{,}\) (Exercise Exercise 9.5.8).
    🔗
    🔗
    It turns out that discrete Borel measures are (up to removing a continuous measure) all the non-continuous Borel measures. We can now decompose the Lebesgue decomposition further.
    🔗

    Theorem 9.4.14.

    If \(\nu\) is a Borel signed-measure on \([a,b]\) then there are mutually singular signed Borel measures \(\nu_d\text{,}\) \(\nu_{sc}\) and \(\nu_{ac}\) such that
    🔗
    1. \(\nu = \nu_d + \nu_{sc} + \nu_{ac}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\nu_d\) is a discrete Borel signed-measure;
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\nu_{sc}\) is a singular continuous Borel signed-measure; and
      🔗
      🔗
    4. \(\nu_{ac}\) is an absolutely continuous Borel signed-measure.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    By the Lebesgue decomposition, Theorem Theorem 9.1.10, there are mutually singular measures \(\nu_s\) and \(\nu_{ac}\) where \(\nu_s\) is singular, \(\nu_{ac}\) is absolutely continuous, and \(\nu = \nu_s + \nu_{ac}\text{.}\) It remains to show that we can decompose \(\nu_s\) into a discrete part and a continuous part. We will thus assume that \(\nu=\nu_s\) is singular.
    🔗
    First, assume \(\nu\) is a measure. Let \(f(x) = \nu([a,x])\) be the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is a right-continuous increasing function on \([a,b]\text{.}\) By Theorem Theorem 9.4.10 the set \(D\) of discontinuities of \(f\) is countable. For each \(x \in D\text{,}\) let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a_x = f(x) - lim_{y\rightarrow d^-}f(y)\neq 0. \end{equation*}
    Note that the sequence \(\sum_{x\in D}a_x\) is absolutely convergent, since \(f\) is of bounded variation. Let \(\nu_d\) be the discrete Borel signed-measure
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu_d = \sum_{x\in A} a_x \delta_{x}, \end{equation*}
    and let \(\nu_{sc} = \nu - \nu_d\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For any \(x \notin D\text{,}\) \(\nu(\{x\}) = \nu_d(\{x\}) = 0\text{,}\) and hence \(\nu_{sc}(\{x\}) = 0.\) If \(x \in D\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \nu_{sc}(\{x\}) &= \nu(\{x\}) - \nu_d(\{x\})\\ & = \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \left(\nu((x-1/n,x]))\right) - a_x\\ &= \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}\left(f(x) - f(x-1/n)\right) - a_x \\ &= a_x - a_x = 0. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\nu_{sc}\) is continuous, completing the proof
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(H\) be the Heaviside function on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) That is
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} H(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x<0 \\ 1 & x \geq 0.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    The Heaviside function is the cumulative distribution function for the Dirac measure at \(0\text{,}\) \(\delta_0\) (see Exercise Exercise 5.4.8). Thus, if \(x_0 \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then the cumulative distribution function for the Dirac measure \(\delta_{x_0}\) is \(H(x-x_0).\)
    🔗

    Corollary 9.4.15.

    Let \(f\) be a right-continuous function of bounded variation on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then there are functions \(f_d\text{,}\) \(f_{sc}\text{,}\) and \(f_{ac}\) on \([a,b]\) such that
    🔗
    1. \(f = f_d + f_{sc} + f_{ac}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(f_{ac}\) is absolutely continuous on \([a,b]\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(f_{sc}\) is continuous and singular;
      🔗
      🔗
    4. there are points \(\{x_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) in \([a,b]\) and a sequence of real numbers \((a_n)_n\) such that
      \begin{equation*} f_d(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_nH(x-x_n) \end{equation*}
      for all \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(\nu = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_n\delta_{x_n}\) be a discrete Borel signed-measure on \([a,b]\text{.}\) The result follows from Theorem Theorem 9.4.14, on noting that if \(f(x)=\nu([a,x])\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_nH(x-x_n) = \sum_{n\colon x_n\leq x}a_n. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
