    Chapter 5 Measurable functions

    “Can we convince ourselves of the existence of a mathematical object without defining it? To define always means naming a characteristic property of what is being defined.”
    🔗
    ―
    Henri Lebesgue
    🔗
    🔗
    Many fields of mathematics boil down to one central idea: we study some class of objects and a sensible class of maps between them. At its core, this is the premise of a field called Category Theory. For example, group theory is the study of groups and homomorphisms between them; linear algebra is the study of vector spaces and linear maps. The objects we have been studying are measure spaces. What is the correct set of functions to study between them?
    🔗
    Less generally, our goal is to develop a theory of integration. We will thus be, primarily, interested into functions into the real line? But again, what types of functions? And how are we to view the real line? Should we view the real line as a measure space, with the Lebesgue measure on the Lebesgue measurable sets? Or should we view the real line as a measure space, with the Lebesgue measure restricted to the Borel sets?
    🔗
    In this chapter we introduce the concept of measurable functions into the (extended) real line. These will be the functions of most interest to us. These will be the functions we can attempt to integrate. The more general question of what is the right class of functions between measure spaces is addressed in Remark 5.1.16, but does not play a central role in these notes.
