    Section 11.1 Linear functionals

    As each of \(V\) and \(W\) have a norm, and thus a metric, we can define what it means for a map from \(V\) to \(W\) to be continuous. Explicitly, a map \(f \colon V \rightarrow W\) is continuous if for all \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists \(\delta>0\) such that if \(v,w\in V\) satisfy \(\|v-w\|<\delta\text{,}\) then \(\|f(v) - f(w)\|<\varepsilon.\)
    🔗
    We will be primarily interested in linear maps between vectors. The following proposition gives another characterization of continuity for linear maps.
    🔗

    Proposition 11.1.1.

    Let \(V\) and \(W\) be two normed vector spaces. Let \(T \colon V \rightarrow W\) be a linear map. Then \(T\) is continuous if and only if there exists \(M\geq 0\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|T(v)\|\leq M \|v\| \end{equation*}
    for all \(v\in V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    First suppose that \(T\) is continuous. There exists \(\delta >0\) such that \(\|v-w\|<\delta\) then \(\|T(v) - T(w)\|<1\text{.}\) In particular, since \(T\) is linear, if \(\|v\|<\delta\) then \(\|T(v)\|\leq 1\text{.}\) Take any non-zero \(v \in V\text{.}\) Fix \(\delta'\) with \(\delta>\delta'>0.\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left\|\frac{v}{\delta'^{-1}\|v\|}\right\| =\delta'< \delta. \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left\|T\left(\frac{v}{\delta'^{-1}\|v\|}\right)\right\|\ < 1, \end{equation*}
    and so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|T(v)\| < \delta'^{-1}\|v\|. \end{equation*}
    Conversely, suppose there is an \(M>0\) such that \(\|T(v)\|\leq M\|v\|\) for all \(v\in V\text{.}\) Take \(\varepsilon >0\text{,}\) and let \(\delta = \varepsilon/M\text{.}\) Suppose \(v,w \in V\) satisfy \(\|v-w\|<\delta.\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \|T(v) - T(w)\| & = \|T(v-w)\| \\ &\leq M\|v-w\|\\ &< \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(T\) is continuous.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 11.1.2.

    Let \(V\) be a normed vector space. A linear functional is a continuous linear map from \(V\) to \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Denote by \(V^*\) the collection of all linear functionals on \(V\text{.}\) The space \(V^*\) is called the dual space of \(V\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    If \(V\) is a normed linear space, then its dual \(V^*\) is a vector space under the usual operations of scaling and addition for functions:
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} (\varphi + \psi)(v) &= \varphi(v) + \psi(v); \text{ and } (\alpha \varphi)(v) = \alpha \varphi(v), \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    for all \(\varphi,\psi \in V^*\text{,}\) \(v \in V\) and \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Further, the norm on \(V\) induces a natural norm on \(V^*\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 11.1.3.

    Let \(V\) be a normed vector space. For \(\varphi \in V^*\) define \(\|\varphi\|_{\mathrm{op}}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|\varphi\|_{\mathrm{op}} = \sup\{|\varphi(v)| \colon \|v\|=1\}. \end{equation*}
    We call \(\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{op}}\) the operator norm on \(V^*\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    It is relatively straightforward to show that the operator norm is indeed a norm on \(V^*\text{.}\) We leave the details as an exericse,
    🔗

    Proposition 11.1.4.

    Let \(V\) be a normed vector space and let \(\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{op}}\) be the operator norm on the dual \(V^*\text{.}\) Then \(\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{op}}\) is a norm on \(V^*\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Further
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|\varphi\|_{\mathrm{op}} = \inf \{M \colon |\varphi(v)|\leq M\|v\| \text{ for all }v \in V\}, \end{equation*}
    for all \(\varphi \in V^*.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    When \(V\) is a normed vector space, \(V^*\) with the operator norm will be a Banach space. Note, that \(V^*\) is a Banach space even if \(V\) itself is not a Banach space. We state this result below for posterity, but we will omit the proof.
     1 
    The proof itself is not too difficult. It will follow from an \(\varepsilon/3\) argument using the completeness of the real line; similar to the proof that a uniform limit of continuous functions is continuous.
    🔗

    Theorem 11.1.5.

    Let \(V\) be a normed vector space and let \(\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{op}}\) be the operator norm on the dual \(V^*\text{.}\) Then \(\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{op}}\) is a norm on \(V^*\text{.}\) Further \(V^*\) is a Banach space with the operator norm.
