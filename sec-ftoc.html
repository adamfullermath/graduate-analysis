    Section 9.3 The fundamental theorem of calculus

    The main reference for the rest of this chapter is [6], though we present the results in less generality than what is done in [6]. We begin with a result which has nothing at all to do with differentiation, but will be useful.
    🔗

    Theorem 9.3.1.

    Let \(f\) be a Lebesgue integrable function on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) For all \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists a continuous Lebesgue integrable function \(g\) on \(\mathbb{R}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_\mathbb{R} |f-g|\,dm < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    We will deal first with the non-negative case. Fix \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is integrable, \(\lim_{N\rightarrow \infty} \int_{[-N,N]} f\,dm = \int_\mathbb{R} f\,dm<\infty.\) Hence, there is an \(N>0\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_\mathbb{R} f\,dm - \int_{[-N,N]}f\,dm<\varepsilon/3. \end{equation*}
    Suppose \(g\) is a non-negative continuous function on \([-N,N]\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{[-N,N]} |f- g|\,dm<\varepsilon/3. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For each \(n\text{,}\) we define \(g_n\) by \(g_n(x) = g(x)\) for \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{;}\) \(g_n(x) = 0\) for \(x \notin (-N-\frac{1}{n},N+\frac{1}{n})\text{;}\) \(g_n\) is the straight line from the point \((-N-\frac{1}{n},0)\) to \((-N,g(-N))\) on \((-N-\frac{1}{n},-N)\text{;}\) and \(g_n\) is the straight line from the point \((N,g(N))\) to \((N+\frac{1}{n}),0)\) on \((-N-\frac{1}{n},-N)\) (see Figure Figure 9.3.2). Choose \(n\geq 0\) so that \(g(N)/n<\varepsilon/6\) and \(g(-N)/n<\varepsilon/6\text{.}\) Then,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_\mathbb{R} |f - g_n|\,dm =& \int_{(-\infty,-N-\frac{1}{n})}f\,dm + \int_{(-N-\frac{1}{n},-N)}|f-g_n|\,dm\\ &+ \int_{[-N,N]}|f-g_n|\,dm+\int_{(N,N+\frac{1}{n})}|f-g_n|\,dm + \int_{(N,\infty)} f\,dm \\ \leq& \int_{(-\infty,-N)\cup(N,\infty)}f\,dm+\int_{[-N,N]}|f-g|\,dm\\ &+ \int_{(-N-\frac{1}{n},-N)}g_n\,dm +\int_{(N,N+\frac{1}{n})}g_n\,dm\\ <& \varepsilon/3 + \varepsilon/3 + \varepsilon/6 + \varepsilon/6 = \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence, to prove the result for \(f\text{,}\) it suffice to show that the result holds when \(f\) is restricted to a closed interval. We can thus assume that \(f\) is a non-negative integrable function on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    By the definition of \(\int_{[a,b]}f\,dm\) and by Theorem Theorem 8.3.3,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{[a,b]} f\,dm = \sup\left\{ \int_{[a,b]} \varphi\,dm \colon 0\leq \varphi \leq f, \ \varphi \text{ a step function}.\right\}. \end{equation*}
    Let \(\varphi\) be a step function such that \(0\leq \varphi\leq f\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{[a,b]}f-\varphi\,dm < \varepsilon/2. \end{equation*}
    Suppose \(\varphi = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \chi_{I_i}\) where \(\{I_i\}_{i=1}^n\) are disjoint intervals. Without loss of generalization, assume \(a_i \neq 0\) for each \(1\leq i \leq n\text{.}\) For each \(i\text{,}\) let \(g_i\) be a continuous function with \(0\leq g_i \leq \chi_{I_i}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_\mathbb{R} (\chi_{I_i}- g_i) \,dm < \frac{\varepsilon}{n a_i}. \end{equation*}
    Let \(g = \sum_{i=1}^n a g_i\text{.}\) Then \(g\) is a continuous function with \(0\leq g \leq \varphi \leq f\text{,}\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_{[a,b]}(f-g)\,dm = \int_{[a,b]}(f-\varphi)\,dm - \int_{[a,b]}(\varphi - g)\,dm < \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    If \(f\) is a (not necessarily non-negative) integrable function, then \(f = f_+ - f_-\text{,}\) where \(f_+\) and \(f_-\) are the positive and negative parts of \(f\text{,}\) respectively. By the above argument, there are continuous functions \(g_+\) and \(g_-\) such that \(0\leq g_+ \leq f_+\text{,}\) \(0\leq g_-\leq f_-\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_\mathbb{R} (f_+ - g_+)\,dm < \varepsilon/2 \text{ and } \int_\mathbb{R} (f_- - g_-)\,dm < \varepsilon/2. \end{equation*}
    Let \(g = g_+ - g_-\text{.}\) Then \(g\) is a continuous function and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_\mathbb{R} |f-g|\,dm =& \int_\mathbb{R} |f_+-g_+ - f_- + g_-|\,dm\\ \leq& \int_\mathbb{R}(f_+ - g_+)\,dm+\int_\mathbb{R}(f_- - g_-)\,dm\\ <&\varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    For each \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(r>0\text{,}\) denote by \(B(x,r)\) the open interval of length \(2r\) centred at \(x\text{.}\) That is \(B(x,r) = (x-r,x+r)\text{.}\)
     1 
    The \(B\) in this definition stands for `ball’. That term makes more sense when working in \(\mathbb{R}^3\text{,}\) but we will stick with the notation here, even though we are working in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 9.3.3.

    Suppose \(f\) is an integrable function on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) For each \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(r >0\text{,}\) we define \(A_rf(x)\) as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A_rf(x) = \frac{1}{2r}\int_{B(r,x)} f\,dm. \end{equation*}
    We will call \(A_rf\) the average value function for \(f\) with radius \(r\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Define the Hardy-Littlewood maximal function, \(Hf\) for \(f\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} Hf(x) = \sup_{r>0}A_r|f|(x) = \sup_{r>0} \frac{1}{2r}\int_{\mathbb{R}}|f|\,dm. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Lemma 9.3.4.

    Let \(f\) is an integrable function on some closed interval \([a,b] \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) The function \(A_rf(x)\) is jointly continuous in \(r>0\) and \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Fix \(r_0>0\) and \(x_0 \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Let \((x_n)_n\) be a sequence in \(\mathbb{R}\) converging to \(x_0\text{,}\) and let \((r_n)_n\) be a sequence of positive real numbers converging to \(r_0\text{.}\) Take any \(y\neq x_0 \pm r_0\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{\substack{n \rightarrow \infty \\m \rightarrow \infty}}\chi_{B(x_n,r_m)}(y) = \chi_{B(x_0,r_0)}(y). \end{equation*}
    Thus the sequence \((\chi_{B(x_n,r_m)})_{n,m}\) converges pointwise a.e. to \(\chi_{B(x_0,r_0)}\text{.}\) Hence \((\chi_{B(x_n,r_m)}f)_{n,m}\) converges pointwise a.e. to \(\chi_{B(x_0,r_0)}f\text{.}\) Further, if \(r<r_0 + \frac{1}{2}\) and \(|x-x_0|<\frac{1}{2}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |\chi_{B(x,r)}f| \leq \chi_{B(x_0,r_0)}|f|. \end{equation*}
    It follows now from the Lebesgue Dominated Convergence Theorem that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (x,r) \mapsto \int_{B(x,r)}f\,dm \end{equation*}
    is jointly continuous in \(x\) and \(r>0\text{.}\) It follows that \(A_rf(x)\) is jointly continuous in \(x\) and \(r>0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 9.3.5.

    Let \(f\) be an integrable function, then Hardy-Littlewood maximal function \(Hf\) is measurable and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x\in \mathbb{R} \colon Hf(x)> \alpha\} \end{equation*}
    is open for all \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    For any \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{x\in \mathbb{R} \colon Hf(x)> \alpha\} = \bigcup_{r>0} \{ x\in \mathbb{R} \colon A_r|f|(x) > \alpha\}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(A_r|f|\) is continuous for all \(r>0\text{,}\) \(\{x\in \mathbb{R} \colon Hf(x)> \alpha\}\) is an open set. Thus \(Hf\) is measurable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 9.3.6. The Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Theorem.

    For all integrable \(f\) on \(\mathbb{R}\) and \(\alpha >0\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(\{x\in \mathbb{R} \colon Hf(x)> \alpha\}) \leq \frac{3}{\alpha}\int_\mathbb{R} |f|\,dm. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Fix \(\alpha >0\) and let \(E_\alpha = \{x\in \mathbb{R} \colon Hf(x)> \alpha\}\text{.}\) By Corollary Corollary 9.3.5, \(E_\alpha\) is an open set. By the continuity of \(Hf\text{,}\) for each \(x \in E_\alpha\) there is an \(r_x>0\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A_{r_x}|f|(y) > \alpha \end{equation*}
    for all \(y \in B(x,r_x)\text{.}\) Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E_\alpha = \bigcup_{x\in E_\alpha} B(x,r_x). \end{equation*}
    Take any \(c \in \mathbb{R}\) with \(0<c < m(E_\alpha)\text{.}\) By the inner regularity of Lebesgue measure, Theorem Theorem 5.1.8, there is a compact set \(K \subseteq E_\alpha\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} c < m(K) \leq m(E_\alpha). \end{equation*}
    Note that the sets \(\{B(x,r_x)\}_{x\in E_\alpha}\) form and open cover of \(K\text{,}\) since they are an open cover of \(E_\alpha\text{.}\) As \(K\) is compact, there are sets \(A_1,\ldots,A_m \in \{B(x,r_x)\}_{x\in E_\alpha}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} K \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^m A_k. \end{equation*}
    Choose \(B_1 \in \{A_1,\ldots,A_n\}\) such that \(m(B_1)\) is maximal. Choose \(B_2 \in \{A_1,\ldots,A_n\}\) such that \(m(B_2)\) is maximal in the sets disjoint from \(B_1\text{.}\) Choose \(B_3 \in \{A_1,\ldots,A_n\}\) such that \(m(B_3)\) is maximal in the sets disjoint from \(B_1\) and \(B_2\text{.}\) Proceed in this way to get sets \(B_1,\ldots,B_n\) until we exhaust all of the sets in \(\{A_1,\ldots,A_m\}\text{.}\) Let \(x_j \in E_\alpha\) be the center point of the interval \(B_j\) for \(1\leq j\leq n\text{.}\) For any \(1\leq i \leq m\text{,}\) there is a \(1 \leq j \leq n\text{,}\) such that \(A_i \cap B_j \neq \emptyset.\) Fixing \(j\) such that \(A_i \cap B_j \neq \emptyset\) and that \(m(B_j)\) is maximal. Let \(B_j^*\) be the open interval with the center \(x_j\) as \(B_j\) with \(m(B_j^*) = 3m(B_j).\) Then \(A_i \subseteq B_j^*\text{.}\) Thus \(B_1^*,\ldots,B_n^*\) is an open cover of \(K\text{.}\) We thus have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} c &< m(K) \\ &\leq \sum_{i=1}^n m(B_j^*) \\ &\leq 3\sum_{i=1}^n m(B_j) \\ &< \frac{3}{\alpha} \sum_{k=1}^n \int_{B_k}|f|\,dm \\ &\leq \frac{3}{\alpha} \int_{\mathbb{R}}|f|\,dm, \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    where the penultimate inequality comes from the fact that \(A_{r_{x_j}}|f|(x_j)>\alpha\text{,}\) and the final inequality comes from the fact that \(B_1,\ldots,B_n\) are disjoint. Taking the supremum over all \(c<m(E_\alpha)\) gives the desired inequality.
    🔗
    🔗
    For Fatou’s Lemma we needed the concept of a limit inferior, \(\liminf\text{,}\) for a sequence. For what follows we will need to consider the limit superior of a function. If \(\varphi\) is a real-valued function we define the limit superior of \(\varphi(r)\) as \(r\) approaches \(R\) as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limsup_{r\rightarrow R} \varphi(r) = \lim_{\varepsilon \rightarrow 0} \sup_{0<|r-R|<\varepsilon}\varphi(r)\, \left(= \inf_{\varepsilon>0} \sup_{0<|r-R|<\varepsilon}\varphi(r)\right), \end{equation*}
    and we define the limit inferior of \(\varphi(r)\) as \(r\) approaches \(R\) as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \liminf_{r\rightarrow R} \varphi(r) = \lim_{\varepsilon \rightarrow 0} \inf_{0<|r-R|<\varepsilon}\varphi(r)\, \left(= \sup_{\varepsilon>0} \inf_{0<|r-R|<\varepsilon}\varphi(r)\right). \end{equation*}
    The limit \(\lim_{r\rightarrow R} \varphi(r) = c\) if and only if
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limsup_{r\rightarrow R}\varphi(r) = \liminf_{r\rightarrow R}\varphi(r) =c. \end{equation*}
    Further, by the definition of the Hardy-Littlewood maximal function,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0} |A_rf(x)| \leq Hf(x). \end{equation*}

    Theorem 9.3.7.

    Let \(f\) be a Lebesgue integrable function on \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0}A_rf(x) = f(x) \end{equation*}
    for almost all \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Fix \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) By Theorem Theorem 9.3.1, there is an integrable continuous function \(g\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_\mathbb{R} |f-g|\,dm < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Take \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(\eta>0\text{.}\) By the continuity of \(g\) there is an \(r>0\text{,}\) such that if \(|x-y|<r\text{,}\) then \(|g(x) -g(y)|<\eta\text{.}\) Thus, if \(|x-y|<r\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} |A_r g(x) - g(x)| &= \frac{1}{2r}\left|\int_{B(x,r)} (g(y)-g(x))\,dm(y)\right|\\ &\leq \frac{1}{2r}\int_{B(x,r)} |(g(y)-g(x))|\,dm(y)<\eta. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence, as \(r\rightarrow 0\text{,}\) \(A_rg(x) \rightarrow g(x)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take any \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0}\left|A_rf(x) - f(x)\right| &= \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0}\left|A_r(f-g) + (A_rg-g) + (g-f)(x) \right|\\ &\leq \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0}A_r|f-g|(x) + 0 + |g(x)-f(x)| \\ &\leq H(f-g)(x) + |f-g|(x). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    For \(\alpha >0\) let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} E_\alpha &= \{x\in \mathbb{R} \colon \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0}\left|A_rf(x) - f(x)\right|>0 \}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E_\alpha \subseteq \{x \in \mathbb{R} \colon H(f-g)>\alpha/2\} \cup \{x\in \mathbb{R} \colon |f-g|(x)>\alpha/2\}. \end{equation*}
    By Chebyshev’s inequality, and the choice of \(g\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(\{x\in \mathbb{R} \colon |f-g|(x)>\alpha/2\}) \leq \frac{2}{\alpha}\int_\mathbb{R}|f-g|\,dm < \frac{2\varepsilon}{\alpha}. \end{equation*}
    By the Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Theorem, and the choice of \(g\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(\{x \in \mathbb{R} \colon H(f-g)>\alpha/2\}) \leq \frac{6}{\alpha}\int_\mathbb{R}|f-g|\,dm < \frac{6 \varepsilon}{\alpha}. \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(E_\alpha) \leq \frac{8\varepsilon}{\alpha}. \end{equation*}
    As this holds for all \(\varepsilon>0\text{,}\) it follows that \(m(E_\alpha) = 0\) for all \(\alpha >0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(E = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty E_{1/n}\text{.}\) Then, \(m(E) = 0\text{,}\) and if \(x \notin E\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 = \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0} |A_rf(x) -f(x)|. \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(\lim_{r\rightarrow 0}A_rf(x) = f(x)\) for all \(x \in \mathbb{R} \backslash E\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    This brings us now to an important theorem: The Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem.
    🔗

    Theorem 9.3.8. Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem.

    Let \(f\) be a Lebesgue integrable function on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) For each \(x \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(r>0\text{,}\) let \(E_{r^+}(x) = (x,x+r)\) and \(E_{r^-}(x) = (x-r,x)\text{.}\) Then, for almost all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{m(E_r(x))} \int_{E_r(x)} |f(y) - f(x)|\,dm(y) = 0 \end{equation*}
    and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{m(E_r(x))} \int_{E_r(x)} f\,dm = f(x) \end{equation*}
    where \(E_r = E_{r^+}\text{,}\) \(E_r = E_{r^-}\) or \(E_r = B(x,r)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} L_f = \{x \in \mathbb{R} \colon \lim_{r\rightarrow}\frac{1}{2r}\int_{B(r,x)}|f(y)-f(x)|\,dm(y) =0 \}. \end{equation*}
    For each \(c \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) let \(g_c(x) = |f(x) -c|.\) By Theorem Theorem 9.3.7, there is a Lebesgue measurable set \(E_c \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) such that \(m(E_c) = 0\text{,}\) and if \(x \notin E_c\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} A_rg_c(x) = g_c(x). \end{equation*}
    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E = \bigcup_{q \in \mathbb{Q}} E_q. \end{equation*}
    Then \(m(E) = 0\text{,}\) since \(\mathbb{Q}\) is countable. Take \(x \notin E\) and \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) By the density of \(\mathbb{Q}\) in \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) there is a \(q \in \mathbb{Q}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f(x) - q| < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    For any \(y \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f(y)-f(x)| \leq |f(y)-q| + |f(x)-q| < |f(y)-q| + \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Thus, since \(x \notin E_q\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{2r} \int_{B(x,r)}|f(y)- f(x)|\,dm(y) &\leq \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{2r} \int_{B(x,r)}(|f(y)- q|+ \varepsilon)\,dm(y)\\ &=|f(x) - q| + \varepsilon <2\varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    As \(\varepsilon>0\) is arbitrary, it follows that \(x \in L_f\text{.}\) Hence \(m(\mathbb{R}\backslash L_f) =0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now, for all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) if \(E_r = E_{r^+}\) or \(E_r = E_{r^-}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{r}\int_{E_r}|f(y)-f(x)|\,dm(y) &\leq \frac{1}{r}\int_{B(x,r)}|f(y)-f(x)|\,dm(y) \\ &\leq 2\frac{1}{2r}\int_{B(x,r)}|f(y)-f(x)|\,dm(y). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus the first equality holds for all \(x \in L_f\text{.}\) The second equality follows from Theorem Theorem 9.3.7.
    🔗
    🔗
    We are almost ready to give a concrete description of the Radon-Nikodym derivative of a Borel measure on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) We first need to understand which Borel measures have continuous cumulative distribution functions.
    🔗

    Definition 9.3.9.

    Let \(\nu\) be a Borel measure on the real line. We say that \(\nu\) is outer regular if
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) = \inf \{\nu(U) \colon E \subseteq U,\, U \text{ open}\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Lemma 9.3.10.

    Let \(\nu\) be a Borel measure on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) = \inf \{\nu(U) \colon E \subseteq U,\, U \text{ open}\} \end{equation*}
    for all Borel sets \(E \subseteq [a,b]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take any Borel set \(E \subseteq [a,b]\text{.}\) By the monotonicity of the measure \(\nu\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) \leq \inf \{\nu(U) \colon E \subseteq U,\, U \text{ open}\}. \end{equation*}
    Fix \(\varepsilon >0\) and let \(f\) be the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu\text{.}\) Assume first that \(a \notin E\text{.}\) By Theorem Theorem 5.3.7, there is a countable collection of intervals \(\{(a_k,b_k]\}_{k=1}^\infty\) in \([a,b]\) such that \(E \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (a_k,b_k]\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) + \varepsilon/2 > \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu((a_k,b_k]). \end{equation*}
    By the continuity of measure, for each \(k\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu((a_k,b_k]) = \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \nu((a_k,b_k+r)). \end{equation*}
    Thus, for each \(k\text{,}\) we can choose \(r_k>0\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu((a_k,b_k+r_k)) < \nu((a_k,b_k]) + \varepsilon/2^{k+1}. \end{equation*}
    Let \(U = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (a_k,b_k + r_k)\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \nu(U) &\leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty\nu((a_k,b_k+r_k)) \\ &< \varepsilon/2 + \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu((a_k,b_k]) \\ &< \nu(E) + \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    It follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) = \inf \{\nu(U) \colon E \subseteq U,\, U \text{ open}\}. \end{equation*}
    It is a relatively straightforward exercise to show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(\{a\}) = \inf \{\nu(U) \colon a\in U,\, U \text{ open}\}, \end{equation*}
    which completes the proof.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 9.3.11.

    Let \(\nu\) be a Borel signed-measure on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Let \(f = \frac{d\nu}{dm}\) be the Radon-Nikodym derivative of \(\nu\) with respect to the Lebesgue measure \(m\text{.}\) Then,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{\nu((x,x+r))}{r} =\lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{\nu((x-r,x))}{r} = \lim_{r\rightarrow 0}\frac{\nu((x-r,x+r)}{2r}, \end{equation*}
    for almost all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left|\frac{\nu((x,x+r))}{r}\right| \leq \frac{|\nu|((x,x+r))}{r}\leq \frac{|\nu|((x-r,x+r))}{r}= 2 \frac{|\nu|(B(x,r))}{2r}. \end{equation*}
    Similarly
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left|\frac{\nu((x-r,x))}{r}\right| \leq \frac{|\nu|(B(x,r))}{2r}. \end{equation*}
    Thus, to prove the result we can assume \(\nu\) is a measure, and we need only need to show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{\nu(B(r,x))}{2r}=f(x) \end{equation*}
    for almost all \(x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Assume first that \(\nu \perp m\text{.}\) In this case \(f = 0\text{.}\) By the assumption that \(\nu\perp m\text{,}\) there are Borel sets \(A\) and \(B=A^c\) so that \(\mathbb{R} = A \cup B\) and \(\nu(A) = m(B) = 0\text{.}\) For each \(k\text{,}\) let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F_k = \left\{x \in A \colon \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0}\frac{\nu(B(x,r))}{2r}>\frac{1}{k}\right\}. \end{equation*}
    Fix \(k\text{.}\) We will show that \(m(F_k) =0\text{.}\) The argument is similar to that in the Hardy-Littlewood Maximal Theorem, Theorem Theorem 9.3.6.
    🔗
    Since \(\nu\) is outer regular, Lemma Lemma 9.3.10, for each \(\varepsilon>0\) there is an open set \(U_\varepsilon \supseteq A\text{,}\) such that \(\nu(U_\varepsilon)<\varepsilon.\) For each \(x \in U_\varepsilon\text{,}\) there is a \(r_x>0\text{,}\) so that \(B(x,r_x) \subseteq U_\varepsilon.\) Arguing as in Theorem Theorem 9.3.6, if \(c < m\left(\bigcup_{x\in F_k} B(x,r_x)\right)\) then there is a finite collection \(x_1,\ldots,x_n \in F_k\) so that \(\{B(x_i,r_{x_i})\}_{i=1}^n\) are disjoint and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} c < 3 \sum_{i=1}^n m(B(x_i,r_{x_i}))\leq \sum 3k\sum_{i=1}^n \nu(B(x_i,r_{x_i}))\leq 3k\varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Taking the supremum over all \(c \leq m(F_k)\) we get \(m(F_k)\leq 3k\varepsilon\) for all \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) Hence \(m(F_k) = 0\text{.}\) Let \(F = \bigcup_k F_k\text{.}\) Then \(m(F) = 0\text{,}\) and if \(x \notin F\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limsup_{r\rightarrow 0}\frac{\nu(B(x,r))}{2r} =0 \end{equation*}
    and hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0}\frac{\nu(B(x,r))}{2r} =0. \end{equation*}
    Now let \(\nu\) be an arbitrary Borel measure on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Applying the Lebesgue Decomposition Theorem to \(\nu\text{,}\) we have \(\nu = \nu_a + \nu_s\) where \(\nu_a \ll m\) and \(\nu_s \perp m\text{.}\) In this case
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \frac{d\nu}{dm} = \frac{d\nu_a}{dm} = f. \end{equation*}
    By the Lebesgue Differentiation Theorem, Theorem Theorem 9.3.8 and the Radon-Nikodym Theorem,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{\nu_a(B(x,r))}{2r} = \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{2r}\int_{B(x,r)}f\,dm = f(x). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{\nu(B(x,r))}{2r} = \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{\nu_a(B(x,r))}{2r} + \lim_{r\rightarrow 0} \frac{\nu_s(B(x,r))}{2r} = f(x) + 0 = f(x) \end{equation*}
    for almost all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 9.3.12.

    Let \(f\) be a right-continuous function of bounded variation on a closed interval \([a,b]\) and let \(\nu= \nu_f\) be the signed Borel-measure on \([a,b]\) extending the premeasure \(\nu((c,d]) = f(d) - f(c)\) and \(\nu(\{a\}) = 0\text{.}\) Then \(f'\) is defined for almost all \(x \in [a,b]\) and \(f'\) is integrable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    If \(h \neq 0\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x+h) - f(x) = \begin{cases}\nu((x,x+h]) & \text{if }h>0 \\ -\nu((x+h,x])& \text{if }h<0.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    It follows from Theorem Theorem 9.3.11
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f'(x) = \lim_{h\rightarrow 0}\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}=\frac{d\nu}{dm}(x) \end{equation*}
    for a.a. \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\) As the Radon-Nikodym derivative \(\frac{d\nu}{dm}\) is integrable, \(f'\) is integrable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 9.3.13. Fundamental Theorem of Calculus for Lebesgue Integrals.

    Let \(f\) be a function on a closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Then the following are equivalent
    🔗
    1. \(f\) is absolutely continuous on \([a,b]\text{;}\)\
      🔗
      🔗
    2. for \(x \in [a,b]\text{,}\) \(f(x) - f(a) = \int_{[a,x]} h\,dm\) for some integrable function \(h\) on \([a,b]\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(f\) is differentiable almost everywhere on \([a,b]\text{,}\) \(f'\) is integrable on \([a,b]\text{,}\) and for \(x \in [a,b]\)
      \begin{equation*} f(x) - f(a) = \int_{[a,x]}f'\,dm. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    By replacing \(f\) with \(f - f(a)\) we can assume \(f(a) = 0\text{.}\) Assume \(f\) is absolutely continuous. By Proposition Proposition 9.2.6, \(f\) is of bounded variation. Thus \(f'\) exists a.e. and \(f'\) is integrable by Corollary Corollary 9.3.12. Let \(\nu\) be the signed-Borel measure such that \(f\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\nu\text{.}\) Then, by Proposition Proposition 9.2.7, \(\nu\) is absolutely continuous. Thus, by Theorem Theorem 9.3.11
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \nu([a,x]) = \int_{[a,x]}f'\,dm. \end{equation*}
    Hence (i) implies (iii). That (iii) implies (ii) is clear by setting \(h = f'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, suppose (ii) holds. Let \(h\) be an integrable function on \([a,b]\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \int_{[a,x]}h\,dm \end{equation*}
    for \(x\in[a,b]\text{.}\) Let \(\nu\) be the Borel signed-measure
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) = \int_E h\,dm \end{equation*}
    for Borel sets \(E \subseteq[a,b]\text{.}\) Then \(\nu\) is absolutely continuous, and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \nu([a,x]). \end{equation*}
    By Proposition Proposition 9.2.7, \(f\) is absolutely continuous.
