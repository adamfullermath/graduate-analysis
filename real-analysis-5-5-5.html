    Exercises 4.3 Exercises

    1.

    Let \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) be measures on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. For a real number \(c\geq 0\text{,}\) define \(c\mu\) on \(\mathcal{F}\) by \((c\mu)(E) = c\mu(E)\) for all \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{.}\) Show that \(c\mu\) is a measure on \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Define \(\lambda\) on \(\mathcal{F}\) by
      \begin{equation*} \lambda(E) = \mu(E) + \nu(E). \end{equation*}
      Show that \(\lambda\) is a measure on \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\) We denote \(\lambda\) by \(\mu + \nu\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Suppose \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are measures on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\) and \(\mu \geq \nu\) (i.e. \(\mu(E) \geq \nu(E)\) for all \(E\in \mathcal{F}\)). Define \(\lambda\) on \(\mathcal{F}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lambda(E) = \sup\{\mu(F) - \nu(F) \colon F \subseteq E \text{ measurable, } \nu(F)< \infty\}. \end{equation*}
    1. Show that \(\lambda\) is a measure on \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Show that \(\nu + \lambda = \mu\) (where \(\nu + \lambda\) is defined by Exercise 4.3.1).
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Show that if \((X,\mathcal{F},\nu)\) is \(\sigma\)-finite then the \(\lambda\) is the unique measure satisfying \(\nu + \lambda = \mu\text{.}\) (Hint: Assume there are two such measures \(\lambda_1\) and \(\lambda_2\) and show they have to be equal on sets with \(\nu(E)<\infty\text{.}\) Then move to general measurable sets.)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space. Let \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\) be measures on \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\) Further assume that one or both of \(\mu_1\) and \(\mu_2\) are finite measures. Define \(\nu = \mu_1 - \mu_2\text{,}\) i.e. \(\nu(E) = \mu_1(E) - \mu_2(E)\) for all \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{.}\) Show that \(\nu\) is a signed measure on \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space. Let \(\mathcal{V}\) be the set of all finite signed measures on \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\) Define addition \(+\) on \(\mathcal{V}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (\nu_1 + \nu_2)(E) = \nu_1(E) + \nu_2(E) \end{equation*}
    for all \(\nu_1,\nu_2 \in \mathcal{F}\) and all \(E \in F\text{;}\) and define scalar multiplication on \(\mathcal{V}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (c\nu)(E) = c(\nu(E)) \end{equation*}
    for all \(c\in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) \(\nu \in \mathcal{V}\text{,}\) and \(E \in \mathcal{F}\)
    🔗
    1. Show that \(\nu_1 + \nu_2 \in \mathcal{V}\) for all \(\nu_1, \nu_2 \in \mathcal{V}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Show that \(c\nu \in \mathcal{V}\) for all \(c \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(\nu \in \mathcal{V}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Show that \(\mathcal{V}\) is a vector space under the given operations of addition and scalar multiplication.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Let \(\nu\) be a signed measure on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\) Suppose \(E \in \mathcal{F}\) with \(|\nu(E)|<\infty\text{.}\) Show that if \(A \subseteq E\text{,}\) then \(|\nu(A)|<\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Let \(\nu\) be a signed measure on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\) Suppose \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are positive sets. Show that \(E = \bigcup_k E_k\) is a positive set.
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Let \(\nu\) be a signed measure on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\) and let \(A\) be a positive set. Define a function \(\nu_A\) on \(\mathcal{F}\) by \(\nu_A(E) = \nu(A \cap E)\) for all \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{.}\) Show that \(\nu_A\) is a measure on \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    8.

    Prove Proposition 4.1.14: Let \(\nu\) be a signed measure on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{.}\) Show that that the total variation of \(\nu\) can be calculated by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |\nu|(X) = \sup \left\{ \sum_{k=1}^n |\nu(E_k)| \colon \{E_k\}_{k=1}^n \text{ disjoint sets in }\mathcal{F}\right\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    9.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space and let \(\mathcal{V}\) the vector space of finite signed measures on \((X,\mathcal{F})\text{,}\) see Exercise 4.3.4. For \(\nu \in \mathcal{V}\text{,}\) define \(\|\nu\|_{\textrm{var}}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|\nu\|_{\textrm{var}} = |\nu|(X). \end{equation*}
    Show that \(\|\cdot\|_{\mathrm{var}}\) defines a norm on \(\mathcal{V}\text{.}\) That is, show that
    🔗
    1. \(\|\nu\|_{\textrm{var}} = 0\) if and only if \(\nu = 0\text{,}\) the zero measure in \(\mathcal{V}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\|c\nu\|_{\textrm{var}} = |c|\|\nu\|_{\textrm{var}}\) for all \(c \in \mathbb{R}\text{;}\) and
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\|\nu_1 + \nu_2\|_{\textrm{var}} \leq \|\nu_1\|_{\textrm{var}} + \|\nu_2\|_{\textrm{var}}\) for all \(\nu_1, \nu_2 \in \mathcal{V}\text{.}\) I.e. show that \(\|\cdot\|_{\textrm{var}}\) satisfies the triangle inequality.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    10.

    Define \(f\) on \([0,1]\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases} \sin(\frac{1}{x}) & x \neq 0 \\ 0 & x = 0.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Show that \(f\) is not of bounded variation.
    🔗
    🔗

    11.

    Define \(g\) on \([0,1]\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = \begin{cases} x\sin(\frac{1}{x}) & x \neq 0 \\ 0 & x = 0.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Show that \(g\) is continuous. Show that \(g\) is not of bounded variation.
    🔗
    🔗

    12.

    Let \(f(x) = \sin(x)\) for \(x \in [0,2\pi]\text{.}\) Write \(f\) as the difference of two increasing, continuous functions.
    🔗
    🔗

    13.

    Let \(f(x) = \cos(x)\) for \(x \in [0,2\pi]\text{.}\) Write \(f\) as the difference of two increasing, continuous functions.
    🔗
    🔗

    14.

    Let \(p(x)\) be a polynomial on the closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Show that \(p\) is the difference of two increasing, continuous functions.
    🔗
    🔗

    15.

    Let \(f\) be a function of bounded variation on an interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Show that, if \(a \leq c < d \leq b\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} TV(f|_{[c,d]}) = T_f(d) - T_f(c). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    16.

    Let \(f\) be a function of bounded variation on the closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Suppose \(f = m -n\) where \(m\) and \(n\) are increasing functions. Show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} TV(f) \leq (m(b) - m(a)) + (n(b) - n(a)). \end{equation*}
    Show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} TV(f) = (m(b) - m(a)) + (n(b) - n(a)) \end{equation*}
    if and only if there is a constant \(c\) such that \(m = \frac{1}{2}(T_f + f) + c\) and \(n = \frac{1}{2}(T_f - f) +c\text{.}\) (HINT: Compare this question to Proposition 4.1.13).
