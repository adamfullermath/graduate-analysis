    Section 5.1 Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb{R}\)

    We will now apply the Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem, Theorem Theorem 4.3.16, to define the promised Lebesgue measure. Our goal is to extend the length function \(\ell\) to a measure. In Example Example 4.3.11 (see Exercise Exercise 4.4.13) we saw three different semirings of intervals. Exercise Exercise 4.4.16 tells us that \(\ell\) is a premeasure when restricted to each of these semirings. This gives three possibilities of where to apply the Carathéodory-Hahn theorem. The next proposition shows that this choice does not matter. We get the same outer measure no matter the choice.
    🔗

    Proposition 5.1.1.

    Let \(\mathcal{I}\) be the semiring of all intervals in \(\mathbb{R}\text{;}\) let \(\mathcal{I}_f\) be the semiring of all finite length intervals; and let \(\mathcal{I}_h\) be the semiring of intervals of the form \((a,b]\text{,}\) where \(a,b \in \mathbb{R}\) with \(a \leq b\text{.}\) Let \(m^*\) be the outer measure induced by \(\ell\) on \(\mathcal{I}\text{;}\) let \(m_f^*\) be the outer measure induced by \(\ell\) restricted to \(\mathcal{I}_f\text{;}\) and let \(m_h^*\) be the outer measure induced by \(\ell\) restricted to \(\mathcal{I}_h\text{.}\) Then for each \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(A) = m^*_f(A) = m^*_h(A) = \inf\left\{ \sum_{i=1}^\infty \ell((a_i,b_i) \colon A \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty (a_i,b_i)\right\}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    Since \(\mathcal{I}_h \subseteq \mathcal{I}_f \subseteq \mathcal{I}\) it follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(A) \leq m_f^*(A) \leq m_h^*(A) \end{equation*}
    for all \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Take any \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) If \(m^*(A) = \infty\text{,}\) then by the above inequalities, \(m_f^*(A) = \infty\) and \(m_h^*(A) = \infty\text{.}\) Suppose \(m^*(A) < \infty\text{.}\) Fix \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) There exist intervals \(\{I_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) such that \(A \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(A) + \varepsilon > \sum_{k=1}^\infty \ell(I_k). \end{equation*}
    As \(m^*(A)<\infty\) it follows that \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty \ell(I_k)<\infty\text{,}\) and so \(I_k\) is of finite length for each \(k\text{.}\) Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(A)+\varepsilon > \sum_{k=1}^\infty \ell(I_k) \geq m_f^*(A). \end{equation*}
    As this holds for all \(\varepsilon >0\text{,}\) it follows that \(m^*(A) \geq m_f^*(A)\text{.}\) Hence \(m^* = m_f^*\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We will now show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(A) = \inf\left\{ \sum_{i=1}^\infty \ell((a_i,b_i) \colon A \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty (a_i,b_i)\right\}. \end{equation*}
    Let \(m_0^*\) denote the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m_0^*(A) = \inf\left\{ \sum_{i=1}^\infty \ell((a_i,b_i) \colon A \subseteq \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty (a_i,b_i)\right\}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(m^*=m_f^*\) is an infimum over all covers by intervals of finite length, and \(m_0^*\) is an infimum over all covers by open intervals of finite length, it follows that \(m^*(A) \leq m_0^*(A)\) for all \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now take any \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) If \(m^*(A) = \infty\text{,}\) then \(m_0^*(A)= \infty\text{.}\) Suppose that \(m^*(A) <\infty\text{.}\) Fix \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) Let \(\{I_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) be a countable collection of finite length intervals such that \(A \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(A) + \varepsilon > \sum_{k=1}^\infty \ell(I_k). \end{equation*}
    For each \(k\text{,}\) let \(a_k = \inf I_k\) and let \(b_k = \sup I_k\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (a_i - \frac{\varepsilon}{4^k}, b_i + \frac{\varepsilon}{4^k}). \end{equation*}
    That is, \(\{ (a_i - \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{k+1}}, b_i + \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{k+1}})\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a cover of \(A\) by open intervals of finite length. Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} m_0^*(A) &\leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \ell((a_i - \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{k+1}}, b_i + \frac{\varepsilon}{2^{k+1}})) \\ & = \varepsilon + \sum_{k=1}^\infty\ell(I_k) \\ & < m^*(A)+ 2 \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    As the above inequality holds for all \(\varepsilon >0\text{,}\) it follows that \(m_0*(A) \leq m^*(A)\text{.}\) Hence \(m^* = m_0^*\text{.}\) A similar argument shows that \(m_h^* = m_0^*\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 5.1.2.

    The function \(m^* \colon \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R}) \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) defined by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(A) = \inf \left\{ \sum_{k=1}^\infty \ell(I_k) \colon A \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k,\ \{I_k\}_{k=1}^\infty \text{ finite length open intervals}\right\} \end{equation*}
    is call the Lebesgue outer measure on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The elements of the \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{M}\) of \(m^*\)-measurable sets are called Lebesgue measurable sets.
    🔗
    The Carathéodory measure \(m\) got by restricting \(m^*\) to \(\mathcal{M}\) is called the Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Proposition Proposition 5.1.1 shows that the Lebesgue outer measure \(m^*\) is the outer measure induced by the length function \(\ell\text{.}\) Thus by the Carathéodory-Hahn theorem, Theorem Theorem 4.3.16, all intervals \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) are Lebesgue measurable, and \(m(I) = \ell(I)\text{.}\) Further, since \(\ell\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, the measure space \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{M}, m)\) is a complete \(\sigma\)-finite measure space, and \(m\) is the unique extennsion of \(\ell\) to a measure on \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.3.

    If \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is a countable set, then \(A\) is Lebesgue measurable and \(m(A) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take any \(b \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Then \(\{b\}\) can be written as a closed interval of \(0\) length
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{b\} = [b,b]. \end{equation*}
    Hence all singleton sets are Lebesgue measurable with Lebesgue measure \(0\text{.}\) Let \(A\) be a countable set. If \(A\) has \(n\) elements then we can write \(A\) as \(A = \{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}\text{;}\) if \(A\) is countably infinite we can write \(A\) as \(A = \{a_1,a_2,\ldots\}\text{.}\) Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \bigcup_{i=1}^n \{a_i\} \text{ or }A = \bigcup_{i=1}^\infty \{a_i\}. \end{equation*}
    In either case, \(A\) is a countable union of disjoint sets of measure \(0\text{.}\) Hence \(m(A) = 0\) by the countable additivity of \(m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 5.1.4.

    Theorem Theorem 5.1.3 says that all countable sets have Lebesgue measure \(0\text{.}\) It does not say that if \(m(A) = 0\) then \(A\) is countable. We will see examples of uncountable sets of zero Lebesgue measure later.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.5.

    All Borel sets in \(\mathbb{R}\) are Lebesgue measurable. That is, \(\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    By the construction of \(m\) (i.e. the Carathéodory-Hahn theorem), all open intervals are Lebesgue measurable. Any open set \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) can be written as a disjoint union of countably many open sets (Proposition Proposition 2.1.9), and hence is also Lebesgue measurable. As the Borel sets \(\mathcal{B}\) are the sets in the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the open sets, and each open set is in \(\mathcal{M}\text{,}\) it follows that \(\mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 5.1.6.

    Finite sets, countably infinite sets, and intervals are all examples of Borel sets in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Theorem Theorem 5.1.5 says that all Borel sets are Lebesgue measurable, but we have not exhibited any sets which are Lebesgue measurable but not Borel.
    🔗
    In the other direction, we know that \(\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R})\text{,}\) but we have not exhibited any subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) which are not Lebesgue measurable. That is, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{B} \subseteq \mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R}) \end{equation*}
    but it is not clear if these inclusions are strict. We will see in Section Section 5.2 that each inclusion is indeed strict: there are Lebesgue measurable sets which are not Borel sets; and there are subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) which are not Lebesgue measurable.
    🔗
    🔗
    We will now look at some approximation and regularity properties of the Lebesgue measure. By Proposition Proposition 5.1.1 the Lebesgue outer measure \(m^*\) is an induced outer measure: \(m^*\) is the outer measure induced by the length function \(\ell\) on the collection of all intervals in the real line. We can thus apply Proposition Proposition 4.3.15 to \(m^*\text{.}\) That is, letting \(\mathcal{I}_{\sigma\delta}\) be the countable intersections of countable unions of intervals, if \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) then there is a set \(A \in \mathcal{I}_{\sigma\delta}\) such that \(E \subseteq A\) and \(m^*(A) = m^*(E)\text{.}\) Further, if \(E\) is Lebesgue measurable then \(m(A\backslash E) = 0\) (noting here that all the sets in \(\mathcal{I}_{\sigma\delta}\) are Lebesgue measurable since every interval is Lebesgue measurable). The following result is a strengthening of Proposition Proposition 4.3.15 for the Lebesgue measure.
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.7. Outer and inner approximation of Lebesgue measure.

    Let \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) The following are equivalent.
    🔗
    1. \(E\) is Lebesgue measurable;
      🔗
      🔗
    2. for every \(\varepsilon >0\) there exists an open set \(U\) containing \(E\) such that \(m^*(U\backslash E) < \varepsilon\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. there is a \(G_\delta\)-set \(G\) contraing \(E\) such that \(m^*(G\backslash E) = 0\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. for every \(\varepsilon >0\) there exists a closed set \(C\) contained in \(E\) such that \(m^*(E\backslash C) < \varepsilon\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. there is an \(F_\sigma\)-set \(F\) contained in \(E\) such that \(m^*(E\backslash F) = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The complement of an open set is a closed set, hence the complement of a \(G_\delta\) set is an \(F_\sigma\) set. Since the \(\sigma\)-algebra of Lebesgue measurable sets is closed under complements, it follows that (ii) is equivalent to (iv) and (iii) is equivalent to (v).
    🔗
    Suppose that \(E\) is Lebesgue measurable. We will show that (ii) holds. Assume \(m(E) < \infty\text{.}\) Fix \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) By the definition of the Lebesgue outer measure \(m^*\text{,}\) there exists open intervals \(\{I_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) such that \(E \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(E) + \varepsilon > \sum_{k=1}^\infty \ell(I_k). \end{equation*}
    Let \(U = \bigcup_k I_k\text{.}\) Then \(U\) is open, \(E \subseteq U\text{,}\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(U) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty\ell(I_k) < m(E) + \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Since \(m(U)< \infty\) we can apply the excision property (Proposition Proposition 4.2.1), to get \(m(U\backslash E) < \varepsilon\text{.}\) Thus (ii) holds. The case when \(m(E) = \infty\) is left as an exercise (Exercise Exercise 5.4.3).
    🔗
    Now assume (ii) holds. To show (iii) holds is a similar argument to that in Proposition Proposition 4.3.15. For each \(n\) there is an open set \(U_n\) such that \(m^*(U_n \backslash E) < \frac{1}{n}\text{.}\) Let \(G\) be the \(G_\delta\) set \(G = \bigcap_n U_n\text{.}\) Then \(E \subseteq G\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(G \backslash E) \leq m^*(U_n \backslash E) < \frac{1}{n} \end{equation*}
    for all \(n\text{.}\) Hence \(m^*(G \backslash E) =0.\)
    🔗
    Finally, suppose that (iii) holds for a set \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Take a \(G_\delta\) set \(G\) containing \(E\) with \(m^*(G\backslash E) = 0\text{.}\) Since \(G\) is a \(G_\delta\) set, \(G\) is Lebesgue measurbale; and since \(m^*(G\backslash E) = 0\text{,}\) \(G \backslash E\) is also Lebesgue measurable (Exercise Exercise 4.4.12). Noting that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E = G \cap (G\backslash E)^c \end{equation*}
    it follows that \(E\) is Lebesgue measurable.
    🔗
    🔗
    The following theorem is similar to Theorem Theorem 5.1.7. We record it however, as we will be interested in generalizations of this result in the sequel. The result describes regularity conditions for the Lebesgue measure.
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.8.

    Let \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be Lebesgue measurable, then
    🔗
    1. (outer regularity)
      \begin{equation*} m^*(E) = \inf \{ m(U) \colon E \subseteq U,\ U \text{ open}\}; \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. (inner regularity)
      \begin{equation*} m^*(E) = \sup \{ m(K) \colon K \subseteq E,\ K \text{ compact}\}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Condition (i) follows immediately from the definition of the Lebesgue outer measure. We will use condition (i) to prove (ii).
    🔗
    Take measurable \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Assume first that \(E\) is bounded, so that \(E \subseteq [a,b]\) for some closed interval \([a,b]\text{.}\) Let \(E' = [a,b] \backslash E\text{.}\) Fix \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) By (i), there is an open set \(U\) such that \(E' \subseteq U\) and \(m(U) < m(E') + \frac{1}{n}\text{.}\) Let \(K = [a,b] \backslash U\text{.}\) Since \(U\) is open, \(K\) is closed. Thus, \(K\) is a compact set by the Heine-Borel theorem (Theorem Theorem 2.2.2). Further, since \(E' \subseteq U\text{,}\) \(K \subseteq E\text{.}\) By repeated application of the excision property and monotonicity of \(m\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} m(K) &= (b-a) - m(U \cap [a-b]) \\ &\geq (b-a) - m(U)\\ &> (b-a) - m(E') - \frac{1}{n}\\ &= (b-a) - [(b-a) - m(E)] - \frac{1}{n} \\ &= m(E) - \frac{1}{n}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(E) = \sup\{m(K) \colon K \subseteq E\text{ compact}\}. \end{equation*}
    The case when \(m(E) = \infty\) is left as an exercise (Exercise Exercise 5.4.4).
    🔗
    🔗
    We end this section with a result (due to Lebesgue) showing the connection between the Lebesgue measure and Riemann integration. We first establish/recall some notation and concepts to do with Riemann integration.
    🔗
    Let \([a,b]\) be a closed interval in \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) and let \(f \colon [a,b] \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be a bounded function. Let \(P = \{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n\}\) be a partition of \([a,b]\text{,}\) where
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} a = x_0 < x_1 < \ldots < x_n = b. \end{equation*}
    We define \(O(f,P)\) as
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} O(f,P) = \sum_{k=1}^n \sup_{x,y \in [x_{k-1},x_k]}|f(x) - f(y)|(x_k - x_{k-1}) \end{equation*}
    The function \(f\) is Riemann integrable if for all \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists a partition \(P\) of \([a,b]\) such that \(O(f,P)< \varepsilon\text{.}\)
     1 
    You may have seen other definitions of Riemann integrable. Can you prove that your definition of Riemann integrable is equivalent to the definition given here?
    🔗

    Theorem 5.1.9.

    Let \(f\) be a bounded function on the closed interval \([a,b]\text{,}\) and let \(D = \{x \in [a,b] \colon f \text{ is not continuous at }x\}\text{.}\) Then \(f\) is Riemann integrable if and only if \(D\) has Lebesgue measure \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose that \(f\) is Riemann integrable. Take \(x \in [a,b]\text{.}\) The function \(f\) is not continuous at \(x\text{,}\) if there exists \(\varepsilon\) such that for all \(\delta >0\) there exists \(y \in (x-\delta,x + \delta)\cap [a,b]\) such that \(|f(x)-f(y)|\geq \varepsilon\text{.}\) Thus, if we set
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} D_\eta = \{ x\in [a,b] \colon \forall \delta >0 \exists y\in (x-\delta,x+\delta) \text{ such that }|f(x)-f(y)|\geq\eta\}, \end{equation*}
    then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} D = \bigcup_{\eta >0} D_\eta. \end{equation*}
    That that, if \(\eta_2 > \eta_1 > 0\text{,}\) then \(D_{\eta_2} \subseteq D_{\eta_1}.\) Thus, we can write \(D\) as a countable union
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} D = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty D_{\frac{1}{n}}. \end{equation*}
    Fix \(\eta >0\text{.}\) We will show that \(m^*(D_\eta) = 0\text{.}\) Take any \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is Riemann integrable, there exists a partition
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P = \{a=x_0 < x_1 < \ldots < x_n = b\} \end{equation*}
    such that \(O(f,P)< \varepsilon\text{.}\) Let \(\Lambda = \{k \colon D_\eta \cap [x_{k-1},x_k] \neq \emptyset\}.\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \eta\sum_{k \in \Lambda}(x_k - x_{k-1}) &\leq \sum_{k\in \Lambda} \sup_{x,y \in [x_{k-1},x_k]}|f(x)-f(y)|\\ &\leq \sum_{k=1}^n \sup_{x,y \in [x_{k-1},x_k]}|f(x)-f(y)|\\ &= O(f,P)< \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Since \(D_\eta \subseteq \bigcup_{k \in \Lambda} [x_{k-1},x_k]\) it follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(D_\eta) < \frac{\varepsilon}{\eta}. \end{equation*}
    The above inequality holds for all \(\varepsilon>0\text{,}\) and so \(m^*(D_\eta) = 0\text{.}\) It follows that \(D_\eta\) is Lebesgue measurable and \(m(D_\eta) = 0\) (see Exercise Exercise 4.4.12). We have now that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m^*(D) \leq \sum_{n=1}^\infty m^*(D_{\frac{1}{n}}) = 0 \end{equation*}
    and so \(D\) is also Lebesgue measurable with \(m(D) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Suppose now that \(D\) has Lebesgue measure \(0\text{.}\) Given \(\varepsilon > 0\text{,}\) there exists open intervals \(\{I_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(D) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \ell(I_k) < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Let \(U = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k\) and \(K = [a,b] \backslash U\text{.}\) The set \(U\) is a union of open intervals, and so \(U\) is open in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Thus, \(K\) is closed. As \(K \subseteq [a,b]\text{,}\) \(K\) is both closed and bounded, and so \(K\) is compact by the Heine-Borel Theorem, Theorem Theorem 2.2.2.
    🔗
    The function \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\backslash D\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Hence, for each \(x \in [a,b]\backslash D\) there exists \(\delta_x>0\) such that, for if \(y \in [a,b]\) and \(|x-y|<\delta_x\) then \(|f(x)-f(y)|<\varepsilon\text{.}\) The open intervals \(\{(x-\frac{\delta_x}{2}, x+\frac{\delta_x}{2})\}_{x\in K}\) form an open cover of \(K\text{.}\) Since \(K\) is compact, there are is a finite set \(x_1,x_2,\ldots,x_n \in K\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} K \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^n (x_i - \frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}, x_i + \frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}). \end{equation*}
    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} P = \{a,b\} \cup (\{x_i-\frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}, x_i + \frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2} \colon i = 1,\ldots,n\} \cap [a,b]). \end{equation*}
    The set \(P\) is finite, and contains both \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) we can thus order \(P\) and view \(P\) as a partition of \([a,b]\text{.}\) Let the ordered elements of \(P\) be denoted by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{a=y_0 < y_1 <\ldots < y_m = b\}. \end{equation*}
    Note that, if there are \(i\) and \(j\) such that \((x_i - \frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}, x_i + \frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}) \cap (y_{j-1},y_j) \neq \emptyset\) then \([y_{j-1},y_j] \subseteq [x_i - \frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}, x_i + \frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}] \subseteq (x_i -\delta_{x_i}, x_i + \delta_{x_i})\text{.}\) Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \{ j \colon \exists \ i \text{ such that }[y_{j-1},y_j] \subseteq [x_i-\frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}, x_i + \frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}]\} \end{equation*}
    and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} B = \{ j \colon [y_{j-1},y_j] \cap (x_i-\frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2},x_i+\frac{\delta_{x_i}}{2}) = \emptyset \text{ for all }i\}. \end{equation*}
    Note, if \(j \in A\text{,}\) and \(x,y \in [y_{i-1},y_i]\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f(x)-f(y)|\leq |f(x) - f(x_i)| + |f(x_i) - f(x)| <2 \varepsilon, \end{equation*}
    for some \(i\text{,}\) by the choice of \(\delta_{x_i}\text{.}\) Further
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{j \in B} (y_{i-1},y_i) \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty I_k, \end{equation*}
    and so
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{j \in B} (y_i - y_{i-1}) < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Let \(M = \sup_{x \in [a,b]}|f(x)|\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} O(f,P) =& \sum_{j=1}^m \sup_{x,y \in [y_{j-1},y_j]}|f(x) - f(y)|(y_i - y_{j-1}) \\ =& \sum_{j\in A}\sup_{x,y \in [y_{j-1},y_j]}|f(x) - f(y)|(y_i - y_{j-1})\\ &+ \sum_{j\in B}\sup_{x,y \in [y_{j-1},y_j]}|f(x) - f(y)|(y_i - y_{j-1})\\ <& 2\varepsilon \sum_{j\in A}(y_i - y_{j-1}) + 2M \sum_{j\in B}(y_i - y_{j-1})\\ <& 2\varepsilon(b-a) + 2M \varepsilon = 2\varepsilon(b-a + M). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(f\) is Riemann integrable.
