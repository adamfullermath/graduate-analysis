    Exercises 1.4 Exercises

    1.

    Let \(u = (u_1,\ldots,u_n)\) and \(v = (v_1,\ldots,v_n)\) be two vectors in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) and let \(p\) be the polynomial
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} p(x) = \left(\sum_{i=1}^n u_i^2\right)x^2 + 2 \sum_{i=1}^n u_iv_i x + \sum_{i=1}^n v_i^2. \end{equation*}
    1. Show that \(p(x)\geq 0\) for all \(x \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)(Hint: write \(p(x)\) as a sum of \(n\) squares).
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Use (i) to prove the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality. That is, show that,
      \begin{equation*} \left(\sum_{i=1}^n u_iv_i \right)^2 \leq \left(\sum_{i=1}^n u_i^2\right)^2\left(\sum_{i=1}^n v_i^2\right)^2. \end{equation*}
      (Hint: What does (i) tell us about the descriminant of \(p\text{?}\))
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Show that the \(2\)-norm \(\|v\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n v_i^2}\text{,}\) defined in Example 1.1.2, defines a norm on \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{.}\) (HINT: Use Exercise 1.4.1 for the triangle inequality).
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Equip \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with the \(2\)-norm. Let \((v_k)_k\) be a sequence in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) with each
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} v_k = (v_k^1,v_k^2,\ldots,v_k^n). \end{equation*}
    Show that sequence \((v_k)_k\) is a Cauchy sequence in \(\mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) if and only if \((v_k^i)_k\) is a Cauchy sequence in \(\mathbb{R}\) for \(1\leq i \leq n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Show that \(\mathbb{R}^n\) with the \(2\)-norm is a Banach space. (Hint: Consider Exercise 1.4.3).
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Let \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be an interval and let \(C_b(I)\) be the collection of all continuous real-valued bounded functions on \(I\text{.}\) For \(f \in C_b(I)\) let \(\|f\|_\infty\) be the \(\sup\)-norm
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|f\|_\infty = \sup_{x\in I}|f(x)|. \end{equation*}
    1. Show that \(C_b(I)\) with \(\|\cdot\|_\infty\) is a normed vector space.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Let \((f_n)_n\) be a sequence in \(C_b(I)\) and let \(f \in C_n(I)\text{.}\) Show that \((f_n)_n\) converges to \(f\) with respect to the \(\sup\)-norm if and only if \((f_n)_n\) converges uniformly to \(f\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Show that \(C_b(I)\) is a Banach space.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Let \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be an interval and let \(C_0(I)\) be the collection of all continuous real-valued functions on \(I\) which vanish at infinity.
    🔗
    1. Show that \(C_0(I)\) is a vector subspace of \(C_b(I)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Show that \(C_0(I)\) is a Banach space with the \(\sup\)-norm.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Let \(f\) be a continuous function on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Show that \(f\) vanishes at infinity, i.e. \(f \in C_0(\mathbb{R})\text{,}\) if and only if
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\rightarrow \infty}f(x) = \lim_{x\rightarrow -\infty}f(x)=0. \end{equation*}
    Let \(g\) be a continuous function on an open interval \((a,b)\text{.}\) Show that \(g\) vanishes at infinity, i.e. \(g \in C_0((a,b))\text{,}\) if and only if
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x\rightarrow b^-}g(x) = \lim_{x\rightarrow a^-}g(x)=0. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    8.

    Prove Proposition 1.1.17: Let \(V\) be a real vector space, and let \(\|\cdot\|_1\) and \(\|\cdot\|_2\) be two norms on \(V\text{.}\) The norm \(\|\cdot\|_2\) is equivalent to \(\|\cdot\|_2\) if and only if the norm \(\|\cdot\|_2\) is equivalent to \(\|\cdot\|_1\)
    🔗
    🔗

    9.

    Let \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be an interval and let \(C_c(I)\) be the collection of all continuous real-valued compactly supported functions on \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Show that \(C_c(I)\) is a vector subspace of \(C_0(I)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Show that \(C_c(\mathbb{R})\) is not a Banach space.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    10.

    Let \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be an interval and let \(C_c(I)\) be the collection of all continuous real-valued compactly supported functions on \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Show that \(C_c(I)\) is a Banach space if and only if \(I\) is a closed bounded interval, in which case
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} C_c(I) = C_0(I) = C_b(I). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    11.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a vector space, let \(\mathcal{F}\) be the collection of all finite a.e. measurable functions on \(X\text{,}\) and define the equivalence relation \(\sim\) on \(\mathcal{F}\) by \(f\sim g\) if and only if \(f=g\) a.e.
    🔗
    Show that the vector space operations on \(\mathcal{F}/\sim\) are well-defined. That is, show that if \(f_1,f_2,g_1,g_2 \in \mathcal{F}\) with \(f_1\sim f_2\) and \(g_1 \sim g_2\) then \((f_1 + g_1) \sim (f_2 + g_2)\text{;}\) and if \(f_1\sim f_2\) and \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then \(\alpha f_1 \sim \alpha f_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    12.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space. Let \(f\) and \(g\) be essentially bounded measurable functions on \(X\text{.}\) Show that if \(\alpha \in \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) then \(\alpha f\) is essentially bounded. Show that \(f+g\) is essentially bounded.
    🔗
    🔗

    13.

    The proof of Young’s inequality, Lemma 1.2.12, relies on the fact that \(f(x) = e^x\) is a convex function. This will be proved in this exercise. Take \(u,v \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Assume that \(u \leq v\text{.}\) Show that
      \begin{equation*} e^v \geq e^u + e^u(v-u). \end{equation*}
      (HINT: First use the Mean Value Theorem to show that there is a \(w\) with \(u\leq w \leq v\) such that \(e^v=e^u +e^w(v-u)\)).
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Assume that \(u \geq v\text{.}\) Show that
      \begin{equation*} e^v \geq e^u + e^u(v-u). \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Take \(\lambda \in[0,1]\) and let \(w = (1-\lambda) u + \lambda v\text{.}\) Note that, by (i) and (ii),
      \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} e^u &\geq e^w + e^w(u-w) \\ e^v &\geq e^w + e^w(v-w). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
      Use these inequalities to show that
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} (1-\lambda)e^u + \lambda e^v \geq e^w. \end{equation*}
      That is, show that \(e^x\) is a convex function.
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    14.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space and take \(f,g \in L^2(X,\mu)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    1. Show that
      \begin{equation*} fg = \frac{1}{2}[(f+g)^2 - f^2 - g^2]. \end{equation*}
      What can we conclude about \(fg\text{?}\) (i.e. is \(fg\) in some \(L^p\)-space?)
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Use the polynomial
      \begin{equation*} \lambda^2 \int_X |f|^2\,d\mu + 2\lambda \int_X fg \,d\mu + \int_X |g|^2\,d\mu \end{equation*}
      to prove the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (Theorem 1.2.15). I.e., prove
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \left| \int_X fg\,d \mu \right| \leq \int_X |f|^2\,d\mu \int_X |g|^2\,d\mu. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Show that the map \(\langle \cdot,\cdot\rangle \colon L^2(X,\mu) \times L^2(X,\mu) \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) given by
      \begin{equation*} \langle f,g\rangle = \int_X fg \,d\mu \end{equation*}
      defines an inner-product on \(L^2(X,\mu)\) such that \(\|f\|_2 = \langle f,f\rangle ^{1/2}\) for all \(f \in L^2(X,\mu)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    15.

    Let \(A\) be an uncountable set, and let \(1\leq p <\infty\text{.}\) Show that \(\ell^p(A)\) is not separable.
