    Section 2.1 Measure spaces

    There will be a scarcity of examples in this section. In Section 2.3 we will develop tools to construct measures. After that, we will have access to a plethora of examples. Let \(X\) be a non-empty set. A measure will assign a non-negative value to subsets of \(X\text{,}\) but not necessarily all subsets. We need the following definition(s) to describe the domain of a measure.
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.1.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set, and let \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) be the power set of \(X\text{.}\) A collection of sets \(\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra
     1 
    The suffix `\(\sigma\)-’ is usually used to denote a countably infinite version of whatever word comes after the `\(\sigma\)-’. With that information you can probably infer what an algebra of sets is.
    if
    🔗
    1. \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{M}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) then \(A^c = X\backslash A \in \mathcal{M}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. if \(\{A_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of sets in \(\mathcal{M}\text{,}\) then \(\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.2.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set, and let \(\mathcal{M}\) be a \(\sigma\)-algebra of subsets of \(X\text{.}\) The pair \((X, \mathcal{M})\) is called a measurable space.
    🔗
    🔗
    We record some basic properties of \(\sigma\)-algebras which we will need frequently.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.1.3.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M})\) be a measurable space. Then
    🔗
    1. \(X \in \mathcal{M}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\mathcal{M}\) is closed under countable intersections, i.e. if \(\{A_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) then
      \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty A_n \in \mathcal{M}; \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(\mathcal{M}\) is closed under finite unions and finite intersections;
      🔗
      🔗
    4. if \(A,B \in \mathcal{M}\) then \(A\backslash B \in M\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    SInce \(\emptyset^c = X\text{,}\) it follows from conditions (i) and (ii) of Definition 2.1.1, that \(X \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(\{A_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection sets in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) Then, it follows from conditions (ii) and (iii) of Definition 2.1.1 the \(\bigcup_n A_n^c \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\) Using De Morgan’s Identity, and condition (ii) again, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty A_n = X \left[\backslash \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n^c\right] \in \mathcal{M}. \end{equation*}
    Suppose \(A_1, \ldots, A_k\) is a finite collection of sets in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) For \(n >k\text{,}\) let \(A_n = \emptyset\text{.}\) Then, by condition (i) of Definition 2.1.1, \(\{A_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of subsets in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) By condition (iii),
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{n=1}^k A_n = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \in \mathcal{M}. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\mathcal{M}\) is also closed under finite unions. Similarly, we can show that \(\bigcap_{n=1}^k A_n \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, take \(A, B \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\) Note
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A\backslash B = A \cap B^c. \end{equation*}
    As \(\mathcal{M}\) is closed under complements and finite intersections, it follows that \(A \backslash B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.4.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set, and let \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) be the power-set of subsets of \(X\text{.}\) Then \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra, and thus \((X, \mathcal{P}(X))\) is a measurable space.
    🔗
    🔗
    There are occasions when we will consider measurable spaces of the form \((X,\mathcal{P}(X))\text{,}\) (particularly if \(X\) is countable). However, in most cases we will consider \(\sigma\)-algebras which are strictly contained in \(\mathcal{P}(X)\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Proposition 2.1.5.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set. Let \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) be a non-empty collection of sets. Then there is a unique \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{M}\) such that \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{M}\text{,}\) and if \(\mathcal{N}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra containing \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{N}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    That is, there is a unique smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra containing \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(Z\) be the collection of all \(\sigma\)-algebras of \(X\) that contain \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Note that \(Z\) is non-empty, as \(\mathcal{P}(X) \in Z\text{.}\) Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{M} = \bigcap_{\mathcal{N} \in Z} \mathcal{N}. \end{equation*}
    That \(\mathcal{M}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra with the desired properties follows readily.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.6.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set, and let \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}\) be a non-empty collection of subsets of \(X\text{.}\) Let \(\mathcal{M}\) be the unique, smallest \(\sigma\)-algebra containing \(\mathcal{S}\) (in the sense of Proposition 2.1.5). We call \(\mathcal{M}\) the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.7.

    Let \(X\) be a topological space. Denote by \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the open sets in \(X\text{.}\) We call \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) the Borel sets of \(X\). If \(A \in \mathcal{B}(X)\) we say that \(A\) is a Borel set in X.
    🔗
    When \(X = \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) we will denote the Borel sets \(\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})\) by \(\mathcal{B}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.1.8.

    Recall that every open set \(\mathcal{U} \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is a countable union of open intervals. Thus, the open sets in \(\mathbb{R}\) are in the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the open intervals. Hence, the Borel sets \(\mathcal{B}\) is the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the open intervals in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We will now (finally) give the definition of a measure and a measure space.
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.9.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M})\) be a measure space. A function \(\mu \colon \mathcal{M} \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) is a measure on \((X,\mathcal{M})\) if
    🔗
    1. \(\mu(\emptyset) = 0\text{;}\) and
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(\{A_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of disjoint sets in \(\mathcal{M}\) then
      \begin{equation*} \mu\left( \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty A_n \right) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \mu(A_n). \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    If \(\mu\) is a measure on a measurable space \((X,\mathcal{M})\text{,}\) we call the triple \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) a measure space. The sets \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) are called measurable sets in the measure space \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.1.10.

    A function which satisfies condition (ii) in Definition 2.1.9 is said to be countable additive on disjoint sets. This property is frequently abbreviated to simply `countably additive’. We must be careful to remember that countable additivity only applies to disjoint sets.
    🔗
    🔗
    The condition of countable additivity implies finite additivity. If \(\{A_k\}_{k=1}^n\) is a finite collection of disjoint sets in a \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{M}\text{,}\) we can extend \(\{A_k\}_{k=1}^n\) to a countably infinite collection \(\{A_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) by letting \(A_k = \emptyset\) for \(k>n\text{,}\) as we did in Proposition 2.1.3. Then, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^n\right) &= \mu\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^\infty\right) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(A_k) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^n \mu(A_k). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Note that the definition of a measure does not assume that \(\mu\) only takes finite values, i.e. \(\mu(A) = \infty\) is an allowed value, and there is no assumption that the series in condition (ii) of Definition 2.1.9 are convergent. We use the usual rules of addition with \(\infty\) and \(- \infty\text{:}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \infty + x &= \infty, \text{ for all }x \in \mathbb{R};\\ -\infty + x &= -\infty, \text{ for all }x \in \mathbb{R};\\ \infty + \infty &= \infty; \text{ and}\\ -\infty + (-\infty) &= -\infty. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    However, we must be careful to avoid \(-\infty + \infty\text{,}\) as \(-\infty + \infty\) is undefined.
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.11.

    A measure space \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is \(\sigma\)-finite if there is a countable collection of sets \(\{A_n\}_{n=1}^\infty\) in \(\mathcal{M}\) such that
    🔗
    1. \(\mu(A_n)< \infty\) for each \(n\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle X = \bigcup_n^\infty A_n.\)
      🔗
      🔗
    If \(\mu(X) < \infty\) then \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is a finite measure space.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.12.

    A measure space \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is complete if, for each \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) with \(\mu(A) = \emptyset\text{,}\) if \(B \subseteq A\) then \(B \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.1.13.

    We will pause here to assess where we are going. One of our main motivations for developing measure theory, as discussed in Section 1.1 and Section 1.2, is to develop a theory of integration on the real line that is more robust than Riemann integration.
    🔗
    For an interval \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) let \(\ell(I)\) denote the length of \(I\text{.}\) To form a Riemann sum for a function \(f\text{,}\) we partition an interval \([a,b]\) into subintervals
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} [a,b] = I_1 \cup \ldots\cup I_n \end{equation*}
    and the Riemann sum is given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*) \ell(I_i) \end{equation*}
    where \(x_i^*\) is some element of \(I_i\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The main idea of Lebesgue integration is to define a measure \(m\) on (a \(\sigma\)-algebra of) subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) so that \(m(I) = \ell(I)\) for intervals \(I\text{.}\) With \(m\) in hand, we can then decompose \([a,b]\) using sets other than intervals. I.e., the idea is to use, instead of just Riemann sums, sums of the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*) m(E_i) \end{equation*}
    where \(x_i^* \in E_i\text{,}\) and \(\{E_i\}_{i=1}^n\) is a disjoint collection of measurable sets such that \(\bigcup_{i=1}^n E_i = [a,b]\text{.}\) Broadening the scope of the sets we can `measure’, i.e. using \(m\) to measure sets that are not necessarily intervals, we will develop a theory of integration stronger than Riemann integration.
    🔗
    The exact definition of \(m\text{,}\) and the \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{M}\) it acts on, will have to wait until we have some more tools in our belt. We can see now, however, that if \(m\) is to do what we want it to do, then \(\mathcal{M}\) will have to contain all intervals in \(\mathbb{R}\) (and hence \(\mathcal{M}\) will have to contain all the Borel sets \(\mathcal{B}\)).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.14.

    Let \(X\) be any non-empty set and let \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) be the \(\sigma\)-algebra of all subsets of \(X\text{.}\) Define \(c \colon \mathcal{P}(X) \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} c(A) = \infty \end{equation*}
    if \(A\) contains infinitely many elements, and \(c(A) = n\) if \(A\) has \(n\) elements, for \(n\) finite. Then \((X,\mathcal{P}(X),c)\) is a measure space. We call \(c\) the counting measure on \(X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The measure space \((X,\mathcal{P}(X),c)\) is always complete; it is \(\sigma\)-finite if and only if \(X\) is countable; it is finite if and only if \(X\) is finite.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.15.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space. Fix \(x \in X\text{.}\) Define \(\delta_x\) on \(\mathcal{F}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \delta_x(A) = \begin{cases} 1 &\text{if }x \in A\\ 0 &\text{if }x \notin A\end{cases}. \end{equation*}
    Then \(\delta_x\) is a measure on \((X,\mathcal{F})\) called the Dirac measure at \(x\).
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.16.

    Let \(X\) be a topological space. A measure \(\mu\) on the Borel sets \(\mathcal{B}(X)\) is a Borel measure if \(\mu(K) < \infty\) for all compact subsets \(K \subseteq X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We will rarely want to define a measure on a set which does not have some natural topology on it. Indeed, most examples of measures that we will see will be Borel measures. The Dirac measure of Example 2.1.15 is Borel measure when \(X\) is a topological space and \(\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{B}(X)\) is the Borel sets (see Exercise 2.4.4). Even the counting measure of Example 2.1.14 is a Borel measure if we endow \(X\) with the discrete topology (see Exercise 2.4.3).
    🔗
    Probability theory can be naturally described in terms of measure theory. Probability theory is a subject in its own right and has led to some differences in naming and notation conventions.
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.17.

    A probability space is a finite measure space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)\) where \(P(\Omega) = 1\text{.}\) The measure \(P\) is called a probability measure.

    though it has had an influence on some of the theory we will see along the way.

    Definition 2.1.18.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) be a measure space. A property \(P\) is said to hold for almost all \(x\in X\) (abbreviated as a.a. \(x \in X\)) or almost everywhere (abbreviate e.e.) if there is a measurable set \(E \subseteq X\) with \(\mu(E) = 0\) such that \(P\) holds on \(X \backslash E\text{.}\)
