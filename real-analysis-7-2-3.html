    Section 1.1 Product measures

    Given two measure spaces \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\text{,}\) we want to construct a measure on \(X\times Y\) from \(\mu\) and \(\nu\text{.}\) We will call this the product measure of \(\mu\) and \(\nu\text{,}\) and denote the product measure by \(\mu \times \nu\text{.}\) The basic road-map for this construction is simple: if \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) and \(B \in \mathcal{N}\text{,}\) we define \((\mu \times \nu)(A\times B) = \mu(A)\nu(B)\text{.}\) We then show that \(\mu \times \nu\) is a premeasure on a semiring. Thus, by the Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem (Theorem II.2.3.16), \(\mu\times\nu\) extends to a measure on \(X \times Y\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.1.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M})\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N})\) be two measurable spaces. If \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) and \(B \in \mathcal{N}\text{,}\) we call the set \(A \times B\) a measurable rectangle in \(X \times Y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 1.1.2.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be measure spaces; let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the measurable rectangles in \(X\times Y\text{;}\) and let \(\mu \times \nu\) be the extended real-valued function on \(\mathcal{S}\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (\mu\times\nu)(A\times B) = \mu(A)\nu(B) \end{equation*}
    for all \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) and \(B \in \mathcal{N}\text{.}\) Then \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring, and \(\mu \times \nu\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Recall, from Exercise II.2.4.14, that the collection \(\mathcal{S}\) of all measurable rectangles in \(X \times Y\) forms a semiring of sets.
    🔗
    Note that \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{S}\text{,}\) and \((\mu\times\nu)(\emptyset)=\mu(\emptyset)\nu(\emptyset)=0\text{.}\) To show \(\mu\times \nu\) is a premeasure it remains to show that \(\mu\times\nu\) is finitely additive and countably monotone.
    🔗
    Suppose \(A \times B\) is a measurable rectangle such that
    \begin{equation*} A\times B = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k \times B_k, \end{equation*}
    where \(\{A_k \times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are disjoint measurable rectangles. For a fixed \(x \in A\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} B = \bigcup_{k \colon x \in A_k} B_k. \end{equation*}
    Note that, if \(x \in A_{k_1}\) and \(x \in A_{k_2}\text{,}\) then since \(A_{k_1}\times B_{k_1}\) and \(A_{k_2}\times B_{k_2}\) are disjoint, \(B_{k_1}\) and \(B_{k_2}\) are disjoint. Thus, the collection \(\{B_k\}_{\{k \colon x \in A_k\}}\) are a disjoint collection of measurable sets is in \(Y\text{,}\) and hence
    \begin{equation*} \nu(B) = \sum_{k \colon x \in A_k} \nu(B_k) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu(B_k)\chi_{A_k}(x). \end{equation*}
    Hence, for any \(x \in X\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \nu(B) \chi_A(x) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu(B_k)\chi_{A_k}(x). \end{equation*}
    Now, by the series version of the Monotone Convergence Theorem, Corollary II.6.1.18,
    \begin{equation*} \mu(A)\nu(B) = \int_X \nu(B) \chi_A \,d\mu= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \int_X\nu(B_k)\chi_{A_k}\,d\mu = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(A_k)\nu(B_k). \end{equation*}
    Hence, \(\mu\times \nu\) is countably additive, and therefore finitely additive.
    🔗
    Note that, for measurable rectangles \(A\times B\) and \(C \times D\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} A \times B \subseteq C \times D \end{equation*}
    if and only if
    \begin{equation*} A \subseteq C \text{ and } B \subseteq D. \end{equation*}
    Thus, if \(A \times B \subseteq C \times D\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} (\mu\times \nu)(A\times B) \leq (\mu\times \nu)(C\times D). \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(\mu\times \nu\) is monotonic.
    🔗
    Let \(A\times B\) be a measurable rectangle and \(\{A_k\times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) be measurable rectangles, such that
    \begin{equation*} A\times B \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k \times B_k. \end{equation*}
    Since the measurable rectangles \(\mathcal{S}\) are a semiring, and \(\mu\times \nu\) is finitely additive, we can assume that the sets \(\{A_k \times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are disjoint. Thus
    \begin{align*} (\mu\times\nu)(A\times B)\amp= (\mu\times\nu)\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (A\times B)\cap (A_k \times B_k) \right) \\ \amp= \sum_{k=1}^\infty (\mu\times\nu)\left((A\times B)\cap (A_k \times B_k )\right)\\ \amp\leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty (\mu\times\nu)(A_k \times B_k). \end{align*}
    Hence \(\mu\times \nu\) is countably monotone, and therefore \(\mu\times \nu\) is a premeasure on the measurable rectangles.
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be two measure spaces, and let \((\mu\times\nu)(A\times B)= \mu(A)\nu(B)\) for \(A\in \mathcal{M}\) and \(B \in \mathcal{N}\text{.}\) By Proposition 1.1.2, \(\mu\times\nu\) is a premeasure on a semiring. Thus, by the Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem (Theorem II.2.3.16), \(\mu\times\nu\) extends to a measure on \((\mu\times\nu)^*\)-measurable sets in \(X\times Y\) (where \((\mu\times\nu)^*\) is the outer measure induced by the premeasure \(\mu\times\nu\)). We denote this extension by \(\mu \times \nu\text{.}\) The measure \(\mu\times \nu\) is called the product measure of \(\mu\) and \(\nu\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Remark 1.1.3.

    Recall that the Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem does not guarantee uniqueness of the Carathéodory extension. In particular, if \(\mathcal{P}\) is the collection of \(\mu\times\nu\)-measurable sets, we are not guaranteed that \(\mu\times \nu\) is the only measure on \((X\times Y,\mathcal{P})\) satisfying
    \begin{equation} (\mu\times\nu)(A\times B)= \mu(A)\nu(B),\tag{1.1} \end{equation}
    for all \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) and \(B \in \mathcal{N}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    We do have uniqueness when \(\mu\times\nu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite on \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) In particular, if \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are \(\sigma\)-finite then \(\mu\times \nu\) will be \(\sigma\)-finite (Exercise 1.4.1), and therefore the product measure is the unique measure satisfying (1.1).
    🔗
    An example where the product measure is not the unique measure satisfying (1.1) is given in Exercise 1.4.2
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.1.4.

    A word of warning: Some texts define the \(\sigma\)-algebra that \(\mu\times \nu\) is defined on to be the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the measurable rectangles. Our definition has \(\mu\times \nu\) as a function on the \(\sigma\)-algebra of \((\mu\times\nu)^*\)-measurable sets. This may be a larger \(\sigma\)-algebra. Exercise 1.4.3 gives sufficient conditions for when the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by measurable rectangles is smaller than \((\mu\times\nu)^*\)-measurable sets.
    🔗
    We discuss some differences between these different definitions of product measure further in Remark 1.1.14
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 1.1.5.

    Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be non-empty sets and let \(E \subseteq X\times Y\text{.}\) For \(x \in X\) the \(x\)-section \(E_x\) of \(E\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} E_x = \{y \in Y \colon (x,y) \in E \}. \end{equation*}
    For \(y \in Y\) the \(y\)-section \(E^y\) of \(E\) is defined as
    \begin{equation*} E^y = \{x \in X \colon (x,y) \in E\}. \end{equation*}
    If \(f\) is a function on \(X\times Y\text{,}\) and \(x\in X\) then the \(x\)-section \(f_x\) of \(f\) is a function on \(Y\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} f_x(y)= f(x,y). \end{equation*}
    If \(y\in Y\) then the \(y\)-section \(f^y\) of \(f\) is a function on \(X\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} f^y(x)= f(x,y). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    We will prove two theorems relating an integral with respect to a product measure \(\mu\times\nu\) and nested integrals with respect to \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) separately: Fubini’s Theorem and Tonelli’s Theorem.
    🔗
    We begin with some lemmas. The goal of the following lemmas is to describe \((\mu\times \nu)(E)\) as an integral with respect to \(\mu\text{.}\) This allows us to (nearly immediately) describe the integrals of simple functions on \(X\times Y\text{.}\) Then, using simple approximations of measurable functions, we will get the general results needed.
    🔗
    Recall that if \(\mathcal{R} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) is a collection of subsets of a set \(X\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{R}_\sigma\) is the collection of all countable unions of sets from \(\mathcal{R}\text{,}\) and \(\mathcal{R}_{\sigma\delta}\) is the countable intersection of sets from \(\mathcal{R}_\sigma\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Lemma 1.1.6.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be measure spaces and let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the collection of all measurable rectangles in \(X \times Y\text{.}\) If \(E \in \mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) satisfying \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{,}\) then the \(x\)-section \(E_x\) is \(\nu\)-measurable for all \(x \in X\text{,}\) and
    \begin{equation*} (\mu\times\nu)(E) = \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take \(E \in \mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}.\) By Exercise 1.4.3, \(E_x\) is \(\nu\)-measurable for all \(x \in X\text{.}\) Alternatively, this can be proved directly by first considering \(E \in \mathcal{S}\text{,}\) then \(E \in \mathcal{S}_\sigma\text{,}\) and then \(E \in \mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(E = A\times B \in \mathcal{S}\) for some \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) and \(B \in \mathcal{N}\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} E_x = \begin{cases} B &\text{if }x \in A\\ \emptyset &\text{if }x \notin A.\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    Thus
    \begin{equation*} \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x) = \int_X \nu(B)\chi_A\,d\mu = \nu(B)\mu(A) = (\mu\times\nu)(E). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now suppose \(E \in \mathcal{S}_\sigma\text{.}\) Thus, there are measurable rectangles \(\{A_k\times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) such that \(E = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (A_k \times B_k)\text{.}\) Since \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring, we can assume that the sets \(\{A_k \times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are disjoint. Then, for each \(x \in X\text{,}\) the \(x\)-sections \(\{(A_k\times B_k)_x\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are disjoint and \(E_x = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty(A_k\times B_k)_x\text{.}\) Thus, applying the series version of the Monotone Convergence Theorem (Corollar~\ref{cor: MCT series}), we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x) &= \int_X \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu((A_k\times B_k)_x)\,d\mu(x)\\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \int_X \nu((A_k\times B_k)_x)\,d\mu(x)\\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty (\mu\times\nu)(A_k \times B_k)\\ &= (\mu\times\nu)(E). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now suppose that \(E\in \mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) and \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{.}\) There are \(\mathcal{S}_\sigma\) sets \(\{E_k\}\) such that \(E = \bigcap_{k=1}^\infty E_k\text{.}\) Since \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring, we can assume that the sets \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are descending. As \((\mu\times\nu)(E)\) is defined as the outer measure induced by \(\mu\times\nu\) on \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) we can further assume that \((\mu\times\nu)(E_1)= 0\text{.}\) Thus, by the continuity of measure, \((\mu\times\nu)(E)= \lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}(\mu\times\nu)(E_k).\) For each \(x\in X\) and \(k\text{,}\) the \(x\)-section \((E_k)_x\) is \(\nu\)-measurable. Hence
    \begin{equation*} E_x = \bigcap_{k=1}^\infty (E_k)_x \end{equation*}
    is \(\nu\)-measurable. Further, since
    \begin{equation*} (\mu\times\nu)(E_k)=\int_X \nu((E_k)_x)\,d\mu(x)<\infty, \end{equation*}
    \(\nu((E_k)_x)<\infty\) for almost all \(x\text{.}\) Hence, for almost all \(x \in X\text{,}\) \(\nu(E_x)=\lim_{k\rightarrow\infty} \nu((E_k)_x).\) Thus, by the Lebesgue Dominated Convergence Theorem,
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x) &= \lim_{k\rightarrow\infty} \int_X\nu((E_k)_x)\,d\mu(x)\\ &= \lim_{k\rightarrow\infty}\nu(E_k)\\ &= \nu(E). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.1.7.

    If \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) are \(\sigma\)-finite, then conclusion of Lemma 1.1.6 holds for any \(E\) in the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the measurable rectangles, not just the \(\mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\)-sets of finite measure. This is explored in Exercise 1.4.4.
    🔗

    Lemma 1.1.8.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be measure spaces with \(\nu\) a complete measure. If \(E \subseteq X\times Y\) is \((\mu\times \nu)\)-measurable with \((\mu\times\nu)(E)=0\text{,}\) then the \(x\)-section \(E_x\) is \(\nu\)-measurable for almost all \(x\in X\) and \(\nu(E_x)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take \(\mu\times\nu\)-measurable \(E \subseteq X\times Y\) with \((\mu\times \nu)(E)= 0\text{.}\) By Proposition II.2.3.15, there is a set an \(\mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\)-set \(A\) such that \(E \subseteq A\text{,}\) and \((\mu\times\nu)(A) = 0\text{.}\) Since \((\mu\times\nu)(A) = 0<\infty\text{,}\) we can apply Lemma 1.1.6, to get
    \begin{equation*} 0 = (\mu\times\nu)(A) = \int_X \nu(A_x)\,d\mu. \end{equation*}
    Since the map \(x \mapsto \nu(A_x)\) is non-negative, it follows that \(\nu(A_x)=0\) for all most all \(x\in X\text{.}\) Since \(E_x \subseteq A_x\) for all \(x\in X\text{,}\) it follows from the completeness of \(\nu\) that if \(\nu(A_x)=0\) then \(E_x\) is \(\nu\)-measurable and \(\nu(A_x)=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Lemma 1.1.9.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be measure spaces with \(\nu\) a complete measure. If \(E\subseteq X\times Y\) is \((\mu\times\nu)\)-measurable with \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{,}\) then the \(x\)-sections \(E_x\) are \(\nu\)-meaurable for almost all \(x\in X\text{,}\) and the function \(x \mapsto \nu(E_x)\) is a \(\mu\)-measurable function. Further,
    \begin{equation*} (\mu\times\nu)(E) = \int_{X} \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Since \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{,}\) by Proposition II.2.3.15 there is an \(\mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\)-set \(A\subseteq X\times Y\) such that \(E \subseteq A\) and \((\mu\times\nu)(E) = (\mu\times\nu)(A)\text{.}\) Thus \((\mu\times\nu)(A\backslash E)=0\) by the excision property.
    🔗
    By Lemma 1.1.6, \(A_x\) is measurable for all \(x\in X\text{,}\) and \((A\backslash E)_x\) is measurable for almost all \(x\in X\text{.}\) For each \(x\in X\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} E_x = A_x \backslash (A\backslash E)_x. \end{equation*}
    Thus, for almost all \(x\in X\text{,}\) \(E_x\) is \(\nu\)-measurable and
    \begin{equation*} \nu(A_x) = \nu(E_x) + \nu((A\backslash E)_x)= \nu(E_x). \end{equation*}
    Applying Lemma 1.1.6 again, we have
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} (\mu\times \nu)(E) &= (\mu\times \nu)(A)\\ &= \int_X\nu(A_x)\,d\mu \\ &= \int_X\nu(E_x)\,d\mu. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 1.1.10. Fubini’s Theorem.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space, let \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be a complete measure space and let \(f\) be a measurable function \(X\times Y\) which is integrable with respect to \(\mu\times \nu\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Then for almost all \(x\in X\text{,}\) the \(x\)-section \(f_x\) of \(f\) is integrable with respect to \(\nu\) and
    \begin{equation*} \int_{X\times Y} f\,d(\mu\times\nu) = \int_X\left(\int_Y f(x,y)\,d\nu(y)\right)d\mu(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    First suppose that \(f =\chi_E\) where \(E\subseteq X\times Y\) is a measurable set with \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{.}\) Note that, for each \(x \in X\text{,}\) the \(x\)-section \(f_x\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} f_x = \chi_{E_x}. \end{equation*}
    By Lemma 1.1.9, \(E_x\) is measurable for almost all \(x\in X\text{,}\) hence \(f_x\) is measurable for almost all \(x\in X\text{.}\) Further, again by Lemma 1.1.9,
    \begin{equation*} (\mu\times\nu)(E) = \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x) <\infty. \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\nu(E_x)<\infty\) for almost all \(x\in X\text{.}\) Thus, for almost all \(x\in X\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \infty > \nu(E_x) = \int_Y f_x\,d\mu. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(f_x\) is integrable for almost all \(x\text{.}\) We have now that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_{X\times Y} f\,d(\mu\times \nu)& = (\mu\times\nu)(E)\\ & = \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu(x)\\& = \int_X\left(\int_Y f(x,y)\,d\nu(y)\right)d\mu(x). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    By the linearity of integration, the result holds when \(f\) is an integrable simple function on \(X\times Y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now suppose that \(f\) is a non-negative integrable function on \(X \times Y\text{.}\) Let \((\phi_n)_n\) be an increasing sequence of simple functions which converge pointwise to \(f\text{.}\) By the above arguments, and the Monotone Convergence Theorem, it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\int_X\left(\int_Y \phi_n(x,y)\,d\nu(y)\right)\,d\mu(x) &= \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\int_{X\times Y} \phi_n \,d(\mu\times \nu)\\ & = \int_{X\times Y} f\,d(\mu\times\nu). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    To complete the proof we will show that
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\int_X\left(\int_Y \phi_n(x,y)\,d\nu(y)\right)\,d\mu(x) = \int_X\left(\int_Y f(x,y)\,d\nu(y)\right)d\mu(x). \end{equation*}
    By removing a set of measure zero if necessary, we can assume that the \(x\)-sections \((\phi_n)_x\) are integrable for all \(x\in X\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Thus, for each \(x\in X\text{,}\) \(((\phi_n)_x)_n\) is an increasing sequence of measurable functions, converging pointwise to \(f_x\text{.}\) Thus \(f_x\) is measurable for each \(x\in X\text{.}\) For each \(n\text{,}\) let define a function \(h_n\) on \(X\) by
    \begin{equation*} h_n(x) = \int_Y (\phi_n)_x\,d\nu, \end{equation*}
    and define a function \(h\) on \(X\) by
    \begin{equation*} h(x) = \int_Y f_x\,d\nu. \end{equation*}
    Since each \(\phi_n\) is simple, it follows that \(h_n\) is integrable for all \(n\text{.}\) Thus sequence \((h_n)_n\) is an increasing sequence of non-negative measurable functions. By the Monotone Covergenece Theorem applied to \(((\phi_n)_x)_n\text{,}\) \((h_n)_n\) converges pointwise to \(h\text{.}\) Applying the Monotone Convergence Theorem to the sequence \((h_n)_n\text{,}\) we get
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\int_X h_n \,d\mu = \int_X h\,d\mu; \end{equation*}
    i.e.
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \int_X \left(\int_Y \phi_n(x,y)\,d\nu(y) \right)\,d\mu(x) = \int_X \left(\int_Y f(x,y)\,d\nu(y) \right)\,d\mu(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    If \(f\) is an arbitrary integrable function on \(X\times Y\text{,}\) then \(f = f_+ - f_-\) where \(f_+\) is the positive part of \(f\text{,}\) and \(f_-\) is the negative part of \(f\text{.}\) Since the result holds for \(f_+\) and \(f_-\text{,}\) the result holds for \(f\) by the linearity of integration.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.1.11.

    Let \(f\) be a measurable function on a product space \(X\times Y\text{.}\) Note that, in order to apply Fubini’s theorem, we must first know that \(f\) is integrable with repsect to the product measure \(\mu \times \nu\text{.}\) It is possible that the iterated integral
    \begin{equation*} \int_X \left( \int_Y f(x,y)\,d\nu(y)\right) \,d\mu(x) \end{equation*}
    exists and is finite, but \(f\) itself is not integral with resepct to \(\mu \times \nu\text{.}\) An example is given in Exercise 1.4.5.
    🔗
    🔗
    The shortcoming of Fubini’s Theorem discussed in Remark 1.1.11 does not arise when when we deal exclusively with \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces. This is the famed theorem of Tonelli.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.1.12. Tonelli’s Theorem.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces, with \(\nu\) a complete measure, and let \(f\) be a non-negative \((\mu\times\nu)\)-measurable function on \(X\times Y\text{.}\) Then, for almost all \(x\in X\text{,}\) the \(x\)-section \(f_x\) of \(f\) is \(\nu\)-measurable, and the (almost everywhere defined) function on \(X\)
    \begin{equation*} x \mapsto \int_Y f_x\,d\nu \end{equation*}
    is \(\mu\)-measurable. Further,
    \begin{equation*} \int_{X \times Y}f\,d(\mu\times \nu) = \int_X \left( \int_Y f(x,y)\,d\nu(y)\right) \,d\mu(x). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Since, \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) are \(\sigma\)-finite, the product measure \(\mu \times \nu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite on \(X\times Y\text{,}\) by Exercise 1.4.1. Thus, by the Simple Approximation Theorem, Theorem II.5.2.4, there is an increasing sequence of simple functions \((\phi_n)_n\) which converge pointwise to \(f\) and each \(\phi_n\) is supported in a set of finite measure. For each \(n\text{,}\) since \(\phi_n\) is a supported in a set of finite measure, \(\phi_n\) is integrable with respect to \(\mu\times \nu\text{.}\) Hence, we can apply Fubini’s Theorem to each function \(\phi_n\text{,}\) to get
    \begin{equation*} \int_{X\times Y}{\phi_n}\,d{(\mu\times\nu)} = \int_{X}{\left(\intd{Y}\,d{\phi_n(x,y)}{\nu(y)}\right)}{\mu(x)}. \end{equation*}
    The rest of the proof proceeds in a similar vein to that of Fubini’s Theorem.
    🔗
    🔗
    With obvious modifications, replacing \(x\)-sections with \(y\)-sections, we can prove the analogous results of Fubini’s Theorem and Tonelli’s Theorem on \(X\times Y\text{,}\) for measure space \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) when \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is complete. Fubini’s Theorem and Tonelli’s Theorem are often combined into one result, the Fubini-Tonelli Theorem, which combines the two results.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.1.13. The Fubini-Tonelli Theorem.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be \(\sigma\)-finite complete measure spaces, and let \(f\) be a \((\mu\times\nu)\)-measurable function on \(X\times Y\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_{X\times Y}{|f|}\,d{(\mu\times\nu)} &= \int_{X}{\left(\intd{Y}\,d{|f(x,y)|}{\nu(y)}\right)}{\mu(x)}\\ &= \int_{Y}{\left(\intd{X}\,d{|f(x,y)|}{\mu(x)}\right)}{\nu(y)}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Further, if any of the three integrals above are finite, then \(f\) is integrable and
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_{X\times Y}{f}\,d{(\mu\times\nu)} &= \int_{X}{\left(\intd{Y}\,d{f(x,y)}{\nu(y)}\right)}{\mu(x)}\\ &= \int_{Y}{\left(\intd{X}\,d{f(x,y)}{\mu(x)}\right)}{\nu(y)}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 1.1.14.

    We note in Remark 1.1.4, that some people only define the product measure to act on the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the measurable rectangles. Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces, let \(\mu \times \nu\) be the product measure on \(X\times Y\text{,}\) and let \(\pi\) be the restriction of \(\mu\times \nu\) be the restriction of \(\mu \times \nu\) to the \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{F}\) generated by the measurable rectangles. The analogue of Fubini’s Theorem

    Subsection Products of three or more measures

    If \(\{(X_k,\M_k,\mu_k)\}_{k=1}^n\) are a collection of $n$ measure spaces, we define the measurable rectangles in \(X = \prod_{k=1}^n X_k\) as the sets
    \begin{equation*} \left\{\prod_{k=1}^N A_k \colon A_k \in \mathcal{M}_k\, \text{for }k=1,\ldots,n \right\}. \end{equation*}
    We can similarly define a product measure on \(X\text{.}\) We leave the details of the next proposition to the exercises, Exercise 1.4.6 and Exercise 1.4.7.
    🔗

    Proposition 1.1.15.

    Let \(\{(X_k,\mathcal{M}_k,\mu_k)\}_{k=1}^n\) be measure spaces, let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the measurable rectangles in \(\prod_{k=1}^n X_k\text{;}\) and let \(\mu_1 \times \ldots \mu_n\) be the extended real-valued function on \(\mathcal{S}\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (\mu_1 \times \ldots \times \mu_n)(A_1\times \ldots \times A_n) = \mu_1(A_1)\mu_2(A_2)\ldots \mu_n(A_n) \end{equation*}
    for \(A_k \in \mathcal{M}_k\) for \(1\leq k \leq n\text{.}\) Then \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring, and \(\mu_1\times\ldots\times \mu_n\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    By Proposition 1.1.15, we can thus extend \(\mu_1\times\ldots\times \mu_n\) to a measure on \(\prod_{k=1}^n X_k\) by the Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem. This measure is also called the product measure. Just as in the \(n=2\) case, it is not guaranteed that the measure \(\mu_1\times\ldots\times \mu_n\) is the unique extension of the its restriction to the measurable rectangles. However, when each measure space \((X_k,\mathcal{M}_k,\mu_k)\) is a \(\sigma\)-finite measure space, for \(1\leq k \leq n\text{,}\) then we do have uniqueness. The uniqueness results also gives the following associativity result.
    🔗

    Proposition 1.1.16.

    Let \((X_1,\mathcal{M}_1,\mu_1)\text{,}\) \((X_2,\mathcal{M}_2,\mu_2)\text{,}\) and \((X_3,\mathcal{M}_3,\mu_3)\) be \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces. Then
    \begin{equation*} \mu_1 \times \mu_2 \times \mu_3 =(\mu_1 \times \mu_2) \times \mu_3 =\mu_1 \times (\mu_2 \times \mu_3). \end{equation*}
