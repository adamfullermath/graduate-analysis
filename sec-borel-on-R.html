    Section 3.3 Borel measures on \(\mathbb{R}\)

    The Lebesgue measure \(m\) is induced by the length function on intervals: \(\ell((c,d])) = d - c\text{.}\) Given an increasing function \(g\) on \(\mathbb{R}\) we can define a weighted length on intervals by \(\mu((a,b]) = g(b) - g(a)\text{.}\) We in this section we show how, with some reasonable constraints on \(g\text{,}\) this gives rise to a Borel measure. Conversely, we show how Borel measures on \(\mathbb{R}\) give rise to functions. These processes will be, up to the addition of a constant, inverses of each other.
    🔗
    We will begin with constructing functions from Borel measures.
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.1.

    Let \(I\) be a closed interval in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Let \(\mu\) be a finite Borel measure on \(I\text{.}\) Define a function \(g_\mu \colon I \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g_\mu(x) = \mu(\{y \in I \colon y\leq x\}). \end{equation*}
    The function \(g_\mu\) is called the cumulative distribution function for \(\mu\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.3.2.

    The term cumulative distribution function is borrowed from probability theory.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.3.

    Let \(\gamma\) be the Gaussian distribution. The cumulative distribution function of \(\gamma\) is usually denoted by \(\Phi\text{.}\) By definition \(\Phi\) is given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \Phi(x) = \gamma((-\infty,x]) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^x e^\frac{-x^2}{2}. \end{equation*}
    Note that \(\Phi\) is continuous on every closed interval \([a,b]\) in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Further,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \Phi'(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{\frac{-x^2}{2}}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    A cumulative distribution functions need not be continuous, but they will satisfy a one-sided version of continuity. We define this formally now, before showing all cumulative distribution functions satisfy it in Theorem Theorem 3.3.6.
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.5.

    Let \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a closed interval. A function \(g \colon I \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) is right-continuous on \(I\) if given \(x_0 \in I\) such that there exists \(\varepsilon >0\) such that \(x_0 + \varepsilon \in I\) (i.e. \(x_0\) is not a right end-point of \(I\)), then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{x \rightarrow x_0^+} g(x) = g(x_0). \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Theorem 3.3.6.

    Let \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a closed interval and let \(\mu\) be a finite Borel measure on \(I\text{.}\) Then the cumulative distribution function \(g_\mu\) for \(\mu\) is increasing and right-continuous.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(\mu\) be a finite Borel measure on \(I\) and let \(g = g_\mu\) be the corresponding cumulative distribution function. Note that, if \(a, b \in I\) with \(a<b\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} g(b) - g(a) &= \mu(\{x \in I \colon x \leq b\}) - \mu(\{x \in I \colon x \leq a\})\\ &= \mu((a,b]) \geq 0. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(g\) is an increasing function.
    🔗
    We will now show that \(g\) is right-continuous. Take \(x_0 \in I\text{,}\) such that there is a \(\varepsilon >0\) such that \(x_0 + \varepsilon \in I\text{.}\) Let \((x_k)_k\) be a sequence in \(I\) converging to \(x_0\text{,}\) with \(x_k > x_0\) for each \(k\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{k=1}^\infty (x_0,x_k] = \emptyset. \end{equation*}
    Hence, since \(\mu\) is a finite measure, continuity of measure, Theorem Theorem 2.2.2, gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 = \mu(\emptyset) = \lim_{k\rightarrow \infty} \mu((x_0,x_k]) = \lim_{k \rightarrow \infty} [g(x_k) - g(x_0)]. \end{equation*}
    Hence \(g\) is right-continuous.
    🔗
    🔗
    We will now show how a Borel measure can be recovered from its cumulative distribution function. More generally, we show that any increasing, right-continuous function defines a Borel measure.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.3.7.

    Let \(I \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a closed interval, and let \(g \colon I \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be a non-negative, increasing, right-continuous function. If \(I\) has a minimal element we will also assume that \(g\) is non-negative. Then there is a unique Borel measure \(\mu_g\) on \(I\) satisfying \(\mu_g((c,d]) = g(d) - g(c).\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    First assume that \(I\) does not have a minimal element. That is, assume \(I = (-\infty,b]\) for some \(b\text{,}\) or \(I = \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the semiring of subsets of \(I\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{S} = \{ (c,d] \colon c,d \in I,\ c<d \} \cup \{\emptyset\}. \end{equation*}
    Define \(\mu\) on \(\mathcal{S}\) by \(\mu(\emptyset) = 0\) and \(\mu((c,d]) = g(d)- g(c).\) We will show that \(\mu\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) It is straightforward to show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu((c,d]) = \sum_{k=1}^n \mu((c_k,d_k]] \end{equation*}
    if \(\{(c_k,d_k]\}_{k=1}^n\) are disjoint intervals in \(\mathcal{S}\) with \((c,d] = \bigcup_k (c_k,d_k]\text{.}\)
    🔗
    It remains to show that \(\mu\) is countably monotonic. Suppose \((c,d]\) and \(\{(c_k,d_k]\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are in \(\mathcal{S}\) and satisfy
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} (c,d] \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (c_k,d_k]. \end{equation*}
    Fix \(\varepsilon > 0\) such that \(c+ \varepsilon < d\text{.}\) For each \(k\text{,}\) choose \(d_k'\) such that \(d_k < d_k'\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g(d_k') - g(d_k) < \frac{\varepsilon}{2^k} . \end{equation*}
    (if \(d_k\) is the end-point of \(I\) for some \(k\) we can extend \(g\) so that \(g(x) - g(d_k)\) for \(x > d_k\)). Note that we can \(d_k'\) by the right continuity of \(g\text{.}\) We now have the following inclusions
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} [c+\varepsilon,d] \subseteq (c,d] \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (c_k,d_k] \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty (c_k,d_k'). \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(\{(c_k,d_k')\}_k\) is an open cover of \([c+\varepsilon,d]\text{.}\) By the Heine-Borel Theorem, \([c+\varepsilon,d]\) is compact, thus there is a finite subcover of \([c+\varepsilon, d]\text{.}\) We (relabelling if necessary) denote the sets in this subcover by \((c_1,d_1'),\ldots,(c_n,d_n').\) As \(g\) is increasing, it follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} g(d) - g(c+\varepsilon)&\leq \sum_{k=1}^n g(d_k') - g(c_k) \\ & < \sum_{k=1}^n [g(d_k) - g(c_k) + \frac{\varepsilon}{2^k}] \\ & < \sum_{k=1}^\infty [g(d_k) - g(c_k) + \frac{\varepsilon}{2^k}]\\ & = \sum_{k=1}^\infty [g(d_k) - g(c_k)] + \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Taking a limit as \(\varepsilon\) goes to \(0\) (from above), the right continuity of \(g\) gives that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g(d) - g(c) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty [g(d_k) - g(c_k)], \end{equation*}
    and so \(\mu\) is countably monotonic. Hence \(\mu\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    When \(I\) has a minimal element \(a\text{,}\) so that \(I = [a,b]\) or \(I = [a,\infty)\) we define \(\mathcal{S}\) to be
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{S} = \{a\} \cup \{ (c,d] \colon c,d \in I,\ c<d \} \cup \{\emptyset\}. \end{equation*}
    We define \(\mu\) as above with the extra condition \(\mu(\{a\}) = g(a)\text{.}\) Again, \(\mu\) will be a premeasure on the semiring \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In both cases, the Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem says that \(\mu\) extends to a measure on \(I\text{,}\) which we will still denote by \(\mu\text{.}\) The uniqueness of \(\mu\) follows again from the Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem, and the fact that \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite on \(I\text{.}\) As the sets in \(\mathcal{S}\) will be \(\mu\)-measurable, the Borel sets will be measurable with respect to \(\mu\text{.}\) If \(K = [c,d]\) is a closed interval in \(I\text{,}\) then \(\mu([c,d]) < \infty\text{.}\) It follows that \(\mu(K) < \infty\) for any compact set \(K\text{,}\) and thus \(\mu\) defines a Borel measure on \(I\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 3.3.8.

    When \(I = \mathbb{R}\) and \(g(x) = x\text{,}\) the measure \(\mu_g\) constructed by Theorem Theorem 3.3.6 is the Lebesgue measure.
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 3.3.9.

    Let \(I\) be a closed interval in \(\mathbb{R}\) and let \(g\) be a non-negative, increasing, right-continuous function on \(I\text{.}\) If \(I\) is not bounded below, further assume \(\lim_{x\rightarrow -\infty}g(x) = 0\text{.}\) Let \(\mu_g\) be the Borel measure on \(I\) satisfying \(\mu_g((c,d]) = g(d) - g(c)\) constructed in Theorem Theorem 3.3.7.
    🔗
    If \(\mu_g\) is a finite Borel measure then \(g\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\mu_g\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(a\) is the minimal element of \(I\text{.}\) Then, for each \(x \in I\text{,}\) the construction of \(\mu_g\) in Theorem Theorem 3.3.6 gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu(\{y \in I \colon y \leq x \} &= \mu([a,x]) \\ &= \mu(\{a\}) + \mu((a,x]) \\ &= g(a) + (g(x) - g(a)) = g(x). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(g\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\mu_g\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now suppose \(I\) is not bounded below and that \(\lim_{x\rightarrow -\infty} g(x) = 0.\) Fix \(x \in I\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu((-\infty,x]) &= \mu\left(\bigcup_{-n<x} (-n,x]\right) \\ &= \lim_n \mu((-n,x]) \\ &= g(x) - \lim_n g(-n) \\ &= g(x), \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    and hence \(g\) is the cumulative distribution function for \(\mu_g\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Corollary 3.3.10.

    Let \([a,b]\) be a closed bounded interval in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Every Borel measure on \([a,b]\) is uniquely determined by its cumulative distribution function.
    🔗

    \section*{Exercises}
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.11.

    Let \(m\) be the Lebesgue measure on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Suppose \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is Lebesgue measurable. Take \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) Show that there are disjoint Lebesgue measure able sets \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k \end{equation*}
    and \(m(E_k)<\varepsilon\) for each \(k\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.12.

    Let \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Show that \(m^*(A) = m^*(A + \lambda)\) for all \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Show that \(A\) is Lebesgue measurable if and only if \(A + \lambda\) is Lebesgue measurable for all \(\lambda \in \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.13.

    Complete the proof of Theorem Theorem 3.1.7. That is, show that if \(E\) is measurable then for all \(\varepsilon >0\) there is an open set \(U\) containing \(E\) such that \(m(U\backslash E) < \varepsilon\text{.}\) (You can assume that the result holds when \(m(E)<\infty\text{;}\) the proof of that case is given in the proof of Theorem Theorem 3.1.7).
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.14.

    Complete the proof of Theorem Theorem 3.1.8. That is, show that if \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is Lebesgue measurable then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(E) = \sup\{m(K) \colon K \subseteq E,\ K\text{ compact}\}. \end{equation*}
    (The case when \(m(E)<\infty\) is given in the proof of Theorem Theorem 3.1.8).
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.15.

    Let \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a subset, and let \(\sim_\mathbb{Q}\) be the relation defined in Theorem Theorem 3.2.1. That is, \(x \sim_\mathbb{Q} y\) if and only if there is a \(q \in \mathbb{Q}\) such that \(x = y + q\text{.}\) Show that \(\sim_\mathbb{Q}\) is an equivalence relation.
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.16.

    Let \(\psi \colon [a,b] \rightarrow [c,d]\) be a strictly increasing continuous bijection. Show that \(\psi\) has a continuous inverse.
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.17.

    Let \(f \colon \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) be a continuous function. Show that \(f^{-1}(B)\) for any Borel set \(B \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.18.

    Let \(\delta_0\) be the Dirac measure on the Borel sets \(\mathbb{B}\) in \(\mathbb{R}\) (Example Checkpoint 2.4.4). That is, for \(A \in \mathcal{B}\text{,}\) \(\delta_0(A) = 0\) if \(0 \notin A\text{,}\) and \(\delta_0(A) = 1\) if \(0 \in A\text{.}\) Find the cumulative distribution function \(H\) for \(\delta_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.19.

    Let \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{B},m)\) be the measure space of the Lebesgue measure restricted to the Borel sets \(\mathcal{B}\text{.}\) Let \((\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}_0, m_0)\) be the completion of \((\mathbb{R},\mathcal{B},m)\text{.}\) Show that \(\mathcal{B}_0 = \mathcal{M}\text{,}\) the \(\sigma\)-algebra of all Lebesgue measurable sets; and that \(m_0 = m\text{,}\) the Lebesgue measure. I.e., show that the completion of the Lebesgue measure restricted to the Borel sets, is the Lebesgue measure on all Lebesgue measurable sets.
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.20.

    Let \(\varphi\) be the Cantor Lebesgue function on \([0,1]\text{,}\) let \(C\) be the Cantor set, and let \(U = [0,1] \backslash C\text{.}\) Show that \(\varphi'(x) = 0\) for all \(x \in U\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.21.

    Let \(C \subseteq [0,1]\) be the Cantor set; let \(U = [0,1]\backslash C\text{;}\) and let \(\varphi \colon [0,1] \rightarrow [0,1]\text{.}\) Let \(\mu\) be the Borel measure on \([0,1]\) induced by \(\varphi\) (using Theorem Theorem 3.3.7). Show that \(\mu([0,1]) = 1\text{;}\) \(\mu(C) = 1\) and \(\mu(U) = 0\text{.}\)
