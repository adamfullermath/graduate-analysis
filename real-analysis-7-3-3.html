    Section 2.1 Locally compact groups

    Recall that a group \(G\) is a non-empty set with an associative binary operation which has a (necessarily unique) identity and every element has an inverse. We call the binary operation the group operation for \(G\). A group \(G\) is called abelian if its binary operation is commutative.
    🔗
    For an arbitrary group \(G\text{,}\) we will often call the group operation the product, and use concatenation to denote the product of two elements; and we will denote the inverse by \(^{-1}\) That is, if \(x,y\in G\) then the product of \(x\) and \(y\) will be denoted by \(xy\text{;}\) and the inverse of \(x\) is \(x^{-1}\text{.}\) Notable exceptions to this rule are groups where the binary operation is usually called addition denoted by ‘\(+\)’ and inverses are denoted by ‘\(-\)’, e.g. the group of real numbers with the operation of addition. For an arbitrary group \(G\text{,}\) we will denote the identity element of \(G\) by \(e\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.1.

    Let \(G\) be a group. If there is a topology on the set \(G\) such that the maps
    product
    🔗
    \(G \times G \rightarrow G,\ (x,y) \mapsto xy;\) and
    inverse
    🔗
    \(G \rightarrow G\text{,}\) \(x \mapsto x^{-1}\)
    are continuous,
     1 
    \(G\times G\) is endowed with the product topology here
    then we \(G\) is a topological group.
    🔗
    If \(G\) is a topological group whose topology is locally compact and Hausdorff we call \(G\) a locally compact Hausdorff group.
    🔗
    🔗
    Whilst the terminology may be new, you are probably familiar with several topological groups.
    🔗

    Example 2.1.2.

    Let \(G\) be any group. If we give \(G\) the discrete topology, i.e. the topology in which every subset of \(G\) is open, then \(G\) is a locally compact Hausdorff space. A group endowed with the discrete topology is called a discrete group.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.3.

    The real line \(\bR\) with the operation of addition is a locally compact Hausdorff group. More generally, \(\bR^n\) is a locally compact Hausdorff group under addition. See Exercise 2.3.1.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.4.

    More generally, if \(X\) is a normed vector space, then \(X\) (the operation of vector addition) is a Hausdorff topological group with the topology induced by the norm. Note that, in general, \(X\) is not locally compact.
     2 
    In fact, \(X\) is locally compact if and only if \(X\) is finite dimensional.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.5.

    The non-zero reals, \(\bR\backslash\{0\}\) are a topological group with the operation of multiplication.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.6.

    Let
    \begin{equation*} \bT = \{z \in \bC \colon |z| = 1 \}. \end{equation*}
    Then \(\bT\text{,}\) with the operation of multiplication, is a locally compact Hausdorff group.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.7.

    The general linear group (of degree \(n\)) \(GL_n(\bR)\text{,}\) is the set of all \(n\times n\) invertible matrices. The general linear group is a group with the operation of matrix multiplication. The general linear group \(GL_m(\bR)\) is a subset of the finite dimensional vector space \(M_{n\times n}(\bR)\) of all \(n\times n\) matrices. Endowing \(M_{n\times n}(\bR) \simeq \bR^{n^2}\) with its usual topology, induces a topology on \(GL_n(\bR)\) which makes \(GL_n(\bR)\) a locally compact Hausdorff group.
    🔗
    There are several ways we can describe the topology on \(M_{n\times n}(\bR)\text{.}\) The easiest way is in terms of a norm. We can put a \(p\)-norm on \(M_{n\times n}(\bR)\) for \(1 \leq p \leq \infty\) by identifying \(M_{n\times n}(\bR)\) with \(\bR^{n\times n}.\) We can also put the operator norm on \(M_{n \times n}(\bR)\) as described in Remark 1.3.2. All these norms are equivalent, and so give the same topology on \(M_{n\times n}(\bR)\text{;}\) it doesn’t matter what norm you use (see Exercise 1.4.10). Exercise 2.3.4 asks you to show \(GL_n(\bR)\) is a topological group using the operator norm.
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(G\) be a topological group, and let \(A,B \subseteq G\text{,}\) and \(x \in G\text{.}\) We define the following pieces of notation:
    \begin{align*} x A \amp = \{xa \colon a \in A \} \\ Ax \amp = \{ax \colon a \in A \} \\ AB \amp = \{ab \colon a \in A,\ b \in B \} \text{ and }\\ A^{-1} \amp = \{a^{-1} \colon a \in A \}. \end{align*}
    If \(A = A^{-1}\text{,}\) then we say that \(A\) is symmetric.
    🔗

    Remark 2.1.8.

    If \(G\) is a topological group, then the map \(x \mapsto x^{-1}\) is continuous. This map is injective and surjective, and is its own inverse. That is, the map \(x \mapsto x^{-1}\) is a continuous bijection with a continuous inverse, i.e. a homeomorphism. In particular, this means that if \(A \subseteq G\) is open/closed/compact, then \(A^{-1}\) is open/closed/compact, respectively.
    🔗
    Similarly, if we fix \(x \in G\text{,}\) then the map \(y \mapsto xy\) is a homeomorphism, as is the map \(y \mapsto yx\text{.}\) Thus these two maps also preserve open sets, closed sets, and compact sets.
    🔗
    🔗

    Lemma 2.1.9.

    Let \(G\) be a topological group. Let \(A \subseteq G\) be a neighborhood of the identity \(e\text{.}\) Then
    1. there is a symmetric neighborhood \(B\) of the identity, such that \(A \subseteq B\text{;}\) and
      🔗
    2. there is a neighborhood \(C\) of the identity, such that \(CC\subseteq A\text{.}\)
      🔗
    In particular, there is a symmetric neighborhood \(D\) of the identity such that \(DD \subseteq A.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    For the first point, let \(B = A \cap A^{-1}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(\Pi \colon G\times G \rightarrow G\) denote the product map, \((x,y)\mapsto xy.\) By the continuity of \(\Pi\text{,}\) and since \(ee=e\text{,}\) \(\Pi^{-1}(A)\) is a neighborhood of \((e,e)\) in \(G \times G.\) By the definition of the product topology, there is an open set \(C \subseteq G\text{,}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} (e,e) \in C \times C \subseteq \Pi^{-1}(A). \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(C\) is an open neighborhood of the identity, satisfying \(CC\subseteq A.\)
    🔗
    Finally, if we let \(D = C \cap C^{-1}\text{,}\) then \(D\) is a symmetric neighborhood such that \(DD \subseteq A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.10.

    Let \(G\) be a topological group, let \(x \in G\text{,}\) and let\(f\) be a bounded real-valued function on \(G\text{.}\) Define the left translation of \(f\) by \(x\) as the function
    \begin{equation*} (L_xf)(y) = f(x^{-1}y) \end{equation*}
    and define the right translation of \(f\) by \(x\) as the function
    \begin{equation*} (R_xf)(y) = f(yx). \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The function \(f\) is left uniformly continuous if, for all \(\varepsilon>0\) there is a neighborhood \(U\) of the identity such that
    \begin{equation*} \|L_xf -f \|_\infty \lt\varepsilon \end{equation*}
    for all \(x \in U.\)
    🔗
    The function \(f\) is right uniformly continuous if, for all \(\varepsilon>0\) there is a neighborhood \(U\) of the identity such that
    \begin{equation*} \|R_xf -f \|_\infty \lt\varepsilon \end{equation*}
    for all \(x \in U.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.1.11.

    The inverse in the definition of \(L_x\) raise some eyebrows. Why introduce the inverse at all? There are two clear justifications for this. Firstly, our definitions of \(L_x\) and \(R_x\) preserve the group operations under composition. That is, for \(x,y \in G\)
    \begin{align*} L_x\circ L_y\amp= L_{xy},\ R_x \circ R_y = R_{xy}, \\ L_x^{-1} \amp = L_{x^-1},\text{ and }R_x^{-1}=R_{x^{-1}}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    The second point in favour of our notation, is that \(L_x\) and \(R_x\) on characteristic functions are consistent with left and right translations of sets. That is, if \(x \in G\) and \(E \subseteq G\) then
    \begin{equation*} L_x \chi_E = \chi_{xE} \text{ and } R_x \chi_E = \chi_{Ex}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.1.12.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact group. Every function in \(C_c(G)\) is left and right uniformly continuous.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Fix \(f \in C_c(G)\text{,}\) and let \(K\) be the (necessarily compact) closure of the support of \(f\text{.}\) Take \(\varepsilon\gt 0\text{.}\) For each \(x \in K\text{,}\) let \(U_x \subseteq G\) be the open set
    \begin{equation*} V_x = f^{-1}\left((f(x) - \varepsilon/2, f(x)+\varepsilon/2) \right), \end{equation*}
    and let \(U_x = x^{-1}V_x\text{.}\) Then, for each \(y\in U_x\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} |f(xy)-f(x)| \lt \varepsilon/2. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    By Lemma 2.1.9, there is a symmetric open open neighborhood \(W_x\) of \(e\text{,}\) such that \(W_xW_x\subseteq U_x\text{.}\) Since \(K\) is compact, there are \(x_1,\ldots,x_n \in K\) such that
    \begin{equation*} K = \bigcup_{i=1}^n x_i W_{x_i}. \end{equation*}
    Let \(W = \bigcap_{i=1}^n W_{x_i}\) and fix \(y \in W.\)
    🔗
    Take \(x \in K\text{.}\) There is a \(i\) such that, \(x \in x_i W_{x_i}\text{.}\) Since \(W_{x_i}\subseteq U_{x_i}\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} |f(x) - f(x_i)| \lt \varepsilon/2. \end{equation*}
    Note that
    \begin{equation*} xy = x_i (x_i^{-1} x)y \in x_i W_{x_i}W \subseteq x_i U_{x_i}, \end{equation*}
    and hence
    \begin{equation*} |f(xy)-f(x_i)|\lt \varepsilon/2. \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    \begin{gather*} |f(xy)-f(x)|\leq |f(xy)-f(x_i)| + |f(x_i)-f(x)|\lt \varepsilon. \end{gather*}
    🔗
    Take \(x \notin K\text{.}\) Then \(f(x) = 0\text{.}\) If \(xy \in K\text{,}\) then \(xy \in x_i W_{x_i}\) for some \(i\text{.}\) Thus,
    \begin{equation*} x_i^{-1}x = x_i^{-1}xy y^{-1} \in U_{x_i}, \end{equation*}
    since \(x_i^{-1}xy \in W_{x_i}\) and \(y^{-1} \in W \subseteq W_{x_i}\) (recall that each \(W_{x_j}\) is symmetric). Hence,
    \begin{equation*} |f(x_i)| = |f(x_i) - f(x)|\lt \varepsilon/2. \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} |f(xy) - f(x)| \leq |f(xy)-f(x_i)| + |f(x_i)|\lt \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    If \(xy\notin K\text{,}\) then \(f(xy) = 0\) and so, \(|f(xy) - f(x)|=0.\)
    🔗
    We have shown that there is a neighborhood \(W\) of \(e\text{,}\) such that if \(y \in W\) and \(x \in G\) then
    \begin{equation*} |f(xy) - f(x)|\lt \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \|R_y f -f\|_\infty \lt \varepsilon \end{equation*}
    for all \(y \in W\text{,}\) and hence \(f\) is right uniformly continuous. That \(f\) is left uniformly continuous can be shown similarly.
