    2.

    Let \(([0,1],\mathcal{M},m)\) be the measure space consisting of the Lebesgue measure on the Lebesgue measurable subsets of \([0,1]\text{,}\) and let \(([0,1],\mathcal{M},c)\) be the measure space of consisting of the counting measure on the Lebesgue measurable subsets of \([0,1]\text{.}\) Let \(\mathcal{N}\) denote the \(\sigma\)-algebra of subsets of \([0,1]\times[0,1]\) which are measurable with respect to the product measure \(m\times c\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For \(E \in \mathcal{N}\) and \(y \in [0,1]\text{,}\) let \(E^y\) be the \(y\)-section of \(E\text{.}\) Define a map \(\sigma\) on \(\mathcal{N}\) by
    \begin{equation*} \sigma(E) = \sum_{y\in[0,1]} m(E^y) \end{equation*}
    (See Lemma II.7.4.9 and the preceding paragraphs for information on series of sets indexed by uncountable sets).
    🔗
    1. Show that \(\sigma\) is a measure on \(([0,1]\times[0,1],\mathcal{N})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Show that \(\sigma(A\times B)=m(A)c(B)\) for all \(A,B \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Let \(D = \{(x,x) \colon x \in [0,1]\}\text{.}\) Note that
    \begin{equation*} D = \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty \bigcup_{k=1}^n \left[\frac{k-1}{n},\frac{k}{n} \right] \times \left[\frac{k-1}{n},\frac{k}{n} \right], \end{equation*}
    and hence \(D \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\)
    1. Show that \(\sigma(D) = 0\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. Show that \((m\times c)(D)=\infty\text{.}\) (Hint: show that if \(\{A_k\times B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are measurable rectangles such that \(D \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k \times B_k\text{,}\) then
      \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^\infty m(A_k)c(B_k) = \infty.) \end{equation*}
      🔗
    We have thus given an example of a measure \(\sigma\) on \([0,1]\times[0,1]\) which agrees with the product measure \(m\times c\) on measurable rectangles, but is not equal to the product measure.
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be measure spaces.
    🔗
    1. Let \(\mathcal{L}\) be the collection of sets \(F \subseteq X\times Y\) such that the \(x\)-section \(F_x\in \mathcal{N}\) for all \(x \in X\text{.}\) Show that \(\mathcal{L}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Show that if \(E \subseteq X\times Y\) is in the \(\sigma\)-algebra generated by the measurable rectangles, then the \(x\)-section \(E_x \in \mathcal{N}\) for all \(x \in X\text{.}\) (HINT: Show that the measurable rectangles are in the \(\sigma\)-algebra \(\mathcal{L}\text{.}\))
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Take non-empty \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) such that \(\mu(A)=0\text{;}\) and take \(B \subseteq Y\) which is not measurable, i.e. \(B\notin \mathcal{N}\text{.}\) Show that \(A\times B\) is measurable with respect to the product measure \(\mu\times \nu\text{.}\) Show that \(A\times B\) is not in \(\mathcal{L}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) be \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces and let \(\mathcal{F}\) be the \(\sigma\)-algebra of subsets of \(X \times Y\) generated by the measurable rectangles. In this exercise we will prove, for all \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{,}\) that the map \(x \mapsto \nu(E_x)\) is measurable and
    \begin{equation} (\mu\times \nu)(E) = \int_X \nu(E_x)\,d\mu.\tag{1.2} \end{equation}
    We will do this over several parts.
    🔗
    For \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{,}\) define a map \(\Phi_E\) on \(X\text{,}\) by \(\Phi_E(x)= \nu(E_x)\text{.}\) Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be all the measurable rectangles in \(X \times Y\text{;}\) let \(\mathcal{S}'\) be all finite unions of disjoint sets in \(\mathcal{S}\text{;}\) let \(\mathcal{C}\) be the smallest collection of subsets containing \(\mathcal{S}\) which is closed under countable unions and countable intersections; and let \(\mathcal{G}\) be the collection of sets \(E \in \mathcal{F}\) for which \(\Phi_E\) is measurable and (1.2) holds.
    1. Note that if \(E \in \mathcal{F}\text{,}\) then the \(x\)-section \(E_x\) is measurable for all \(x \in X\text{.}\) Hence \(\Phi_E\) is defined on all \(x \in X\text{.}\) (HINT: This is already done in Exercise 1.4.3).
      🔗
    2. Show that \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{G}\text{.}\) (HINT: This is already done in Lemma 1.1.6 when \((\mu\times\nu)(E)<\infty\text{.}\) What needs to change in the argument if \((\mu\times\nu)(E)=\infty\text{?}\)).
      🔗
    3. Show that \(\mathcal{G}\) is closed under countable unions.
      🔗
    Until we state otherwise, we will now assume that \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) are finite measure spaces.
    🔗
    1. Show that \(\mathcal{G}\) is closed under countable intersections.
      🔗
    2. Conclude that \(\mathcal{C} \subseteq \mathcal{G}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    For \(E \in \mathcal{C}\) let
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{C}(E) = \{ F \in \mathcal{C} \colon E \cap F,\, E\backslash F,\, F \backslash E \in \mathcal{C}\}. \end{equation*}
    1. Show that \(\mathcal{S}' \subseteq \mathcal{C}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    2. Show that for each \(E \in \mathcal{C}\text{,}\) \(\mathcal{C}(E)\) is closed under countable unions and countable intersections.
      🔗
    3. Show that if \(E \in \mathcal{S}'\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{S}' \subseteq \mathcal{C}(E)\) (HINT: Recall Lemma II.2.3.13). Conclude that \(\mathcal{C}(E) = \mathcal{C}\text{.}\)
      🔗
    4. Show that if \(F \in \mathcal{C}\text{,}\) then \(\mathcal{C}(F)= \mathcal{C}\text{.}\) (Consider the symmetry in definition of \(\mathcal{C}(E)\) and part~\ref{part: meas rect sigma-alg}.)
      🔗
      🔗
    5. Show that \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed under complements, finite unions, and finite intersections.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. Show that \(\mathcal{C}\) is closed under countable unions. Conclude that \(\mathcal{C}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.
      🔗
      🔗
    7. Conclude that \(\mathcal{C} = \mathcal{F}\text{,}\) and hence \(\mathcal{G} = \mathcal{F}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    8. Assume now that \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) and \((Y,\mathcal{N},\nu)\) are \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces. Show that \(\mathcal{G} = \mathcal{F}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Let \(([0,1],\mathcal{M},m)\) be the measure space consisting of the Lebesgue measure on the Lebesgue measurable subsets of \([0,1]\text{;}\) let \(([0,1],\mathcal{M},c)\) be the measure space of consisting of the counting measure on the Lebesgue measurable subsets of \([0,1]\text{;}\) and let \(D = \{(x,x) \colon x \in [0,1]\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Show that
    \begin{equation*} \int_{[0,1]\times[0,1]} \chi_D\,d(m\times c) \neq \int_{[0,1]}\left(\int_{[0,1]} \chi_D(x,y)\,dc(y)\right)dm(x). \end{equation*}
    Why does this not contradict Fubini’s Theorem? Why does this not contradict Tonelli’s Theorem? (HINT: The first part of this question is essentially a rephrasing of Exercise 1.4.2.)
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Let \(\{(X_K,\mathcal{M}_k,\mu_k)\}_{k=1}^n\) be measure spaces, let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the measurable rectangles in \(\prod_{k=1}^n X_k\text{.}\) Show that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring.
    🔗
    Note that this exercise completes part of the proof of Proposition 1.1.15. The remainder is proved in Exercise 1.4.7.
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Let \(\{(X_K,\mathcal{M}_k,\mu_k)\}_{k=1}^n\) be measure spaces, let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the measurable rectangles in \(\prod_{k=1}^n X_k\text{;}\) and let \(\mu_1 \times \ldots \times \mu_n\) be the extended real-valued function on \(\mathcal{S}\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} (\mu_1 \times \ldots \times \mu_n)(A_1\times \ldots \times A_n) = \mu_1(A_1)\mu_2(A_2)\ldots \mu_n(A_n) \end{equation*}
    for \(A_k \in \mathcal{M}_k\) for \(1\leq k \leq n\text{.}\) Show that \(\mu_1 \times \ldots \times\mu_n\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Note that this exercise completes part of the proof of Proposition 1.1.15. The first part is proved in Exercise 1.4.6.
    🔗
    🔗

    8.

    (a)

    Let \(a,b>0\) be real numbers. Let
    \begin{equation*} \Delta = \{(x,y) \colon 0\leq x \leq a,\ 0\leq y\leq \frac{b}{a}x\}. \end{equation*}
    Show that \(m_2(\Delta) = ab/2\text{.}\) (HINT: Cover \(\Delta\) with rectangles, and use the continuity of measure).
    🔗
    🔗

    (b)

    Let \(\Delta\) be a triangular region in \(\bR^2\) with one side parallel to the \(x\)-axis or \(y\)-axis. Show that \(m_2(\Delta)=ab/2\text{,}\) where \(a\) is the length of a side parallel to an axis, and \(b\) is the perpendicular distance to the opposite vertex. (HINT: Part (a) deals with right-angle triangles. Other right-angle triangles can be dealt with similarly. For arbitrary \(\Delta\text{,}\) write \(\Delta\) as the union or difference of two right-angle triangles.)
    🔗
    🔗

    (c)

    Let \(P\) be a parallelogram in \(\bR^2\) with one (and hence two) sides parallel to the \(y\)-axis. Show that if the length of the sides parallel to the \(y\)-axis is \(a\) and the perpendicular distance between these sides is \(b\text{,}\) then \(m_2(P) = ab\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
