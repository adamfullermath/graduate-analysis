    Section 1.3 \(L^p\)-approximations and the Riesz-Fischer Theorem

    In the proof, we will incidentally end up considering different types of convergence we now have for measurable functions.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.3.1. Riesz-Fischer Theorem.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space. Then \(L^p(X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is a Banach space for all \(1\leq p\leq \infty\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    First take \(1\leq p < \infty\text{.}\) Let \((f_n)_n\) be a Cauchy sequence in \(L^p(X,\mu)\text{.}\) Choose \(n_1<n_2< \ldots\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|f_n - f_m\|_\infty < \frac{1}{2^k} \end{equation*}
    for \(n,m \geq n_k\text{.}\) Let \(g_1 = f_{n_1}\) and \(g_k = f_{n_{k+1}} - f_{n_{k}}\) for \(j >1\text{.}\) Then,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \|g_k\|_p \leq \|g_1\|_p + \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^k} < \infty. \end{equation*}
    Let \(B =\sum_{k=1}^\infty \|g_k\|_p\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(G_n = \sum_{k=1}^n |g_k|\) and \(G = \sum_{k=1}^\infty |g_k|.\) Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|G_n\|_p \leq \sum_{k=1}^n \|g_k\|_p \leq B \end{equation*}
    for all \(n\text{.}\) Since \((G_n)_n\) is an increasing sequence converging pointwise to \(G\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_X G^p\,d\mu = \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \int_X G_n^p\,d\mu =\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}\|G_n\|_p^p \leq B^p, \end{equation*}
    by the Monotone Convergence Theorem. Thus \(G \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{.}\) In particular, \(G\) is finite a.e. and so the series \(\sum_{k=1}^\infty g_k\) converges absolutely a.e. Let \(F = \sum_{k=1}^\infty g_k\) be the pointwise a.e. limit. Then \(|F|\leq G\text{,}\) and hence \(F \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{.}\) Further,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left| F - \sum_{k=1}^n g_k\right|^p \leq \left(|F| + \sum_{k=1}^n|g_k|\right)^p\leq (2G)^p. \end{equation*}
    Hence, by the Lebesgue Dominated Convergence Theorem,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}\left\|F-\sum_{k=1}^n g_k\right\|_p^p = \lim_{n\rightarrow 0}\int_X \left|F-\sum_{k=1}^ng_k\right|^p\,d\mu = 0. \end{equation*}
    Hence the sequence of partial sums \((\sum_{k=1}^n g_k)_n\) converges to \(F\) in \(p\)-norm. Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^m g_k = f_{n_m}. \end{equation*}
    Thus the subsequence \((f_{n_k})_k\) converges to \(F\) in \(p\)-norm. It follows that \((f_n)_n\) converges to \(F\text{,}\) and hence \(L^p(X,\mu)\) is complete.
    🔗
    Now let \(p= \infty\) and let \((f_n)_n\) be a Cauchy sequence in \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{.}\) By excising a set of measure \(0\) if necessary, assume that each \(f_n\) is bounded. Take any \(\varepsilon>0\text{.}\) There is an \(N\) such that for all \(n,m\geq N\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|f_n-f_m\|_\infty < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Take any \(x \in X\text{,}\) and \(n,m\geq N\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f_n(x) - f_m(x)|\leq \|f_n-f_m\|_\infty < \infty. \end{equation*}
    Thus, \((f_n(x))_n\) is a Cauchy sequence of real numbers. Since \(\mathbb{R}\) is complete, \((f_n(x))_n\) is convergent. Define a function \(f\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} f_n(x) \end{equation*}
    for each \(x \in X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    There is an \(N \geq 0\) such that if \(n,m\geq N\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|f_n - f_m\|_\infty \leq 1. \end{equation*}
    Thus, if \(n \geq N\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|f_n\|_\infty= \|f_n - f_N + f_N\|_\infty \leq \|f_N\|_\infty + 1. \end{equation*}
    Letting
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} M = \max\{\|f_1\|_\infty,\ldots,\|f_{N-1}\|_\infty,\|f_N\|_\infty +1\}, \end{equation*}
    we have \(\|f_n\|_\infty\leq M\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Thus, for \(x \in X\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f(x)| = \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} |f_n(x)| \leq M. \end{equation*}
    Hence \(f \in L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) Choose \(N\text{,}\) such that if \(n,m\geq N\) then \(\|f_n-f_m\|_\infty<\varepsilon/2.\) Then, for \(x\in X\text{,}\) if \(n \geq N\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |f(x) - f_N(x)| = \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}|f_n(x) - f_N(x)|<\varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \|f-f_n\|_\infty &= \sup_{x\in X}|f(x) - f_n(x)\|_\infty \\ &= \sup_{x\in X} \lim_{m\rightarrow \infty} |f_m(x) -f_n(x)|\\ &< \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(f\) is the limit of \((f_n)_n\) in the \(\infty\)-norm, and hence \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\) is complete.
    🔗
    🔗
    For the \(p<\infty\) our proof was a little but stronger than the statement given. In the proof of the Riesz-Fischer theorem for \(p<\infty\) we showed that, if \((f_n)_n\) is a Cauchy sequence in \(L^p(X,\mu)\) then \((f_n)_n\) converges to some \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\) in \(p\)-norm and there is a subsequence of \((f_n)_n\) that converges pointwise a.e. to \(f\text{.}\) We record this statement in the following corollary.
     1 
    Corollary is not the correct word; the result is a corollary of the proof not a corollary of the statement of the Riesz-Fischer theorem itself. The word `Porism’ has been used for such results, [1].
    🔗

    Corollary 1.3.2.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space and let \(1\leq p<\infty.\) If \((f_n)_n\) is a sequence in \(L^p(X,\mu)\) which converges to some \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\) in \(p\)-norm, then there is a subsequence of \((f_n)_n\) which converges pointwise a.e. to \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In general, pointwise a.e. convergence does not imply convergence in the \(p\)-norm for \(1\leq p < \infty\text{;}\) and convergence in the \(p\)-norm does not imply pointwise a.e. convergence. We can see this in the following examples.
    🔗

    Example 1.3.3.

    For \(n\geq 2\text{,}\) let \(f_n\) be the function on \([0,1]\) given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \begin{cases}n^2 x &x \in[0,\frac{1}{n}],\\ -n^2x+2n &x\in[\frac{1}{n},\frac{2}{n}], \\ 0 & x\in[\frac{2}{n},1].\end{cases} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The sequence \((f_n)_n\) converges pointwise to \(0\text{.}\) Also
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{[0,1]}f_n\,dm = \frac{1}{2}\cdot \frac{2}{n}\cdot{n}=1 \end{equation*}
    and so each \(f_n \in L^1([0,1],m)\) with \(\|f_n\|_1 = 1\text{.}\) Hence \((f_n)_{n\geq 2}\) does not converge to \(0\) in \(1\)-norm.
    🔗
    Note that, the sequence \((f_n)_{n\geq 2}\) can not converge to anything in \(1\)-norm. Indeed, if \((f_n)_{n\geq 2}\) converges in \(1\)-norm to some \(f \in L^1([0,1],m)\text{,}\) then by Corollary 1.3.2, a subsequence would converge pointwise a.e. to \(f\text{.}\) Since \((f_n)_{n\geq 2}\) converges pointwise to \(0\text{,}\) any subsequence converges pointwise to \(0\text{,}\) and so we must have \(f=0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 1.3.5.

    For each \(n\geq 1\text{,}\) let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f_n = \chi_{\left[\frac{k}{2^m},\frac{k+1}{2^m}\right]}, \end{equation*}
    where \(m\geq 0\) and \(0\leq k < 2^m\) satisfy
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} n = 2^m +k. \end{equation*}
    These functions are sometimes called the typewriter functions or dancing functions. Explicitly, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f_1 = \chi_{[0,1]},\ f_2 = \chi_{[0,1/2]},\ f_3=\chi_{[1/2,1]}, f_4 = \chi_{[0,1/4]}, f_5 =\chi_{[1/4,1/2]},\ldots \end{equation*}
    If \(n = 2^m +k\) as above,
    🔗
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{[0,1]}f_n\,dm = \frac{1}{2^m}. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(f_n \in L^1([0,1],m)\) and \((f_n)_n\) converges to \(0\) in \(1\)-norm. However, \((f_n)_n\) does not converge pointwise (nor pointwise a.e.) to any function. Indeed, for any \(x \in [0,1]\) there are infinitely many \(n\) such that \(f_n(x) = 0\) and infinitely many \(n\) such that \(f_n(x) =1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The \(\infty\)-norm plays wholly more nicely with our earlier modes of convergence.
    🔗

    Proposition 1.3.7.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space and let \((f_n)_n\) be a sequence in \(L^{\infty}(X,\mu)\text{.}\) Then \((f_n)_n\) converges to some \(f \in L^\infty(X,\mu)\) if and only if there is a measure zero set \(X_0\) such that \(f_n\) converges uniformly to \(f\) on \(X\backslash X_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In particular, if \((f_n)_n\) converges to \(f\) in the \(\infty\)-norm, then \(f_n\) converges pointwise a.e. to \(f\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We turn our attention now to the question of separability of \(L^p\)-spaces. We first establish that simple functions are dense in \(L^p(X,\mu)\text{,}\) \(1\leq p<\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Proposition 1.3.8.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space, and let \(1\leq p<\infty\text{.}\) The collection of simple functions of the form \(\varphi = \sum_{k=1}^na_k\chi_{E_i}\) where \(E_i \in \mathcal{M}\) with \(\mu(E_i)<\infty\) is dense in \(L^p(X,\mu)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(\varphi\) be a simple function on \(X\text{.}\) There are unique scalars \(a_1,\ldots,a_n\) and disjoint measurable sets \(E_1,\ldots,E_n\) such that \(\varphi = \sum_{k=1}^n a_i\chi_{E_i}.\) If \(\varphi \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|\varphi\|_p^p = \sum_{k=1}^n |a_i|^p \mu(E_i) < \infty, \end{equation*}
    and hence \(\mu(E_i)<\infty\) for \(1\leq i \leq n\text{.}\) Conversely, it \(\mu(E_i)<\infty\) for \(1\leq i \leq n\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_X|\varphi|^p\,d\mu = \sum_{k=1}^n |a_i|^p \mu(E_i) < \infty, \end{equation*}
    and hence \(\varphi \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take any \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{.}\) By the simple approximation theorem there are simple functions \((\phi_n)_n\) converging pointwise to \(f\) such that \(|\phi_n|\leq |f|\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Since \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\) and \(|\phi_n|^p \leq |f|^p\text{,}\) it follows that \(\phi_n \in L^p(X,\mu)\) for all \(n\text{.}\) Further
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |\phi_n - f|^p \leq (|\phi_n|+|f|)^p\leq 2^p|f|^p. \end{equation*}
    As \(2^p|f|^p\) is integrable and is an upper bound for \(|\phi_n-f|^p\text{,}\) the Lebesgue Dominated Convergence theorem says
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 = \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\int_X|\phi_n-f|^p\,d\mu. \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}\|\phi_n-f\|_p =0\text{.}\) I.e., \((\phi_n)_n\) converges to \(f\) in the \(p\)-norm.
    🔗
    🔗
    When we are working with the Lebesgue measure on subsets of the real line we go further: replacing simple functions with step functions. We will then use this to show that \(L^p(E,m)\) is separable when \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is Lebesgue measurable, and \(1\leq p<\infty\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Lemma 1.3.9.

    Let \([a,b]\subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a bounded closed interval, and let \(1\leq p < \infty\text{.}\) The step functions on \([a,b]\) are dense in \(L^p([a,b],m).\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(E \subseteq [a,b]\) be a Lebesgue measurable set. We will show that we can approximate \(\chi_E\) with a step function in the \(p\)-norm. Fix \(\varepsilon>0\text{.}\) By the outer regularity of the Lebesgue measure, there is an open set \(E \subseteq U\subseteq \mathbb{R}\) such that \(m(U\backslash E)< \varepsilon^p/2.\) Since \(U \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is open, \(U\) is a disjoint union of countably many open intervals \(\{I_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\text{.}\) Choose \(n\text{,}\) so that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=n+1}^\infty m(I_k)<\varepsilon^p/2, \end{equation*}
    and let \(V = \bigcup_{k=1}^n I_k\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(V \backslash E) \subseteq m(U\backslash E) < \varepsilon^p/2 \end{equation*}
    and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} m(E\backslash V) \subseteq m(U \backslash V) =\sum_{k=n+1}^\infty m(I_k)<\varepsilon^p/2. \end{equation*}
    Note that, since \(V\) is a finite union of disjoint intervals, \(\chi_V\) is a set function. By the above calculations we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \|\chi_E - \chi_V\|_p &= \left(\int_{[a,b]}|\chi_E - \chi_V|^p \,dm\right)^{1/p} \\ &= [m(E\backslash V) + m(V \backslash E)]^{1/p} < \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    As simple functions are linear combinations of characteristic functions, it follows now that step functions are dense in the simple functions in \(L^p([a,b],m)\text{.}\) Hence, by Proposition 1.3.8, the step functions are dense in \(L^p([a,b],m)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Lemma 1.3.10.

    Let \([a,b]\subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a bounded closed interval, and let \(1\leq p < \infty\text{.}\) The Banach space \(L^p([a,b],m)\) is separable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take an arbitrary step function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \varphi = \sum_{k=1}^n c_i \chi_{[x_{i-1},x_i]} \end{equation*}
    where \(a=x_0<x_1<\ldots<x_n=b\) is a partition of \([a,b]\text{,}\) let \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) Choose rationals \(y_1,\ldots,y_{n-1} \in [a,b]\) such that, for \(1\leq i\leq n-1\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} y_i \leq x_i \text{ and }x_i - y_i <\frac{\varepsilon}{2n(b-a)^p}, \end{equation*}
    and let \(y_0=a\) and \(y_n =b\text{.}\) Choose \(q_1,\ldots, q_n \in \mathbb{Q}\) such that for \(1\leq i \leq n\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |q_i - c_i| < \frac{\varepsilon^p}{[2n(x_{i}-x_{i-1})]^{1/p}}, \end{equation*}
    and let \(\psi\) be the step function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \psi = \sum_{i=1}^n q_i \chi_{[y_{i-1},y_i}]. \end{equation*}
    Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int_{[a,b]}|\varphi-\psi|^p\,dm &= \sum_{i=1}^n \int_{[x_{i-1},y_i]}|c_i-q_i|^p\,dm + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\int_{[y_i,x_i]}|x_i - y_{i+1}|^p\,dm\\ &\leq \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\varepsilon^p}{2n} + \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} \frac{\varepsilon^p}{2n}\leq \varepsilon^p. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\|\varphi-\psi\|_p < \varepsilon.\)
    🔗
    We have shown that the collection \(\mathcal{S}_\mathbb{Q}([a,b])\) of all step functions taking rational values on partitions of \([a,b]\) with rational interior endpoints is dense in the collection of all step functions. It follows from Lemma 1.3.9, that \(\mathcal{S}_\mathbb{Q}([a,b])\) is dense in \(L^p([a,b],m)\text{.}\) It is not hard to see that \(\mathcal{S}_\mathbb{Q}([a,b])\) is countable, and hence \(L^p([a,b])\) is separable.
    🔗
    🔗
    Having shown that \(L^p([a,b],m)\) is separable, we can now show that \(L^p(E,m)\) is separable for all \(1\leq p<\infty\) and all Lebesgue measurable \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 1.3.11.

    Let \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be a Lebesgue measurable set, and let \(1\leq p< \infty\text{.}\) The Banach space \(L^p(E,m)\) is separable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    For a closed bounded interval \([a,b] \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) let \(\mathcal{S}_\mathbb{Q}([a,b])\) be defined as in the proof of Lemma 1.3.10. That is, \(\mathcal{S}_\mathbb{Q}([a,b])\) is all rational-valued step functions on \([a,b]\) defined using partitions of \([a,b]\) using rational values.
    🔗
    First assume that \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is bounded. Let \([a,b]\) be a closed bounded interval such that \(E \subseteq [a,b]\text{.}\) Since \(\mathcal{S}_\mathbb{Q}([a,b])\) is dense in the set of simple functions on \([a,b]\text{,}\) the set
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \{\psi|_E \colon \psi \in \mathcal{S}_\mathbb{Q}([a,b])\} \end{equation*}
    is dense in the collection of simple functions on \(E\text{.}\) Hence, \(L^p(E,m)\) is separable, by Proposition 1.3.8.
    🔗
    Assume now that \(E\) is unbounded. For each \(n\in\mathbb{N}\text{,}\) let \(E_n = E \cap [-n,n]\text{.}\) By the above argument, since \(E_n\) is bounded, there is a countable set \(\mathcal{S}_n\) which is dense in \(L^p(E_n,m).\) Extend each \(\varphi \in \mathcal{S}_n\) to \(E\) by setting \(\varphi(x) = 0\) for \(x \in E \backslash E_n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take \(\varepsilon >0\) and fix \(f \in L^p(E,m).\) By the continuity of integration, Proposition II.6.2.6,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\int_{E_n} |f|^p\,dm = \int_E |f|^p\,dm<\infty. \end{equation*}
    Thus, we can choose \(n\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_{E\backslash E_n} |f|^p\,dm < \varepsilon^p/2. \end{equation*}
    Since \(\mathcal{S}_n\) is dense in \(L^p(E_n,m)\text{,}\) there exists \(\varphi \in \mathcal{S}_n\) such that \(\|f|_{E_n} - \varphi\|^p_p < \varepsilon/2.\) Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \|f-\varphi\|^p_p &= \int_E |f-\varphi|^p\,dm \\ &= \int_{E_n} |f-\varphi|^p\,dm + \int_{E\backslash E_n} |f|^p\,dm\\ &<= \frac{\varepsilon^p}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon^p}{2} = \varepsilon^p. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\|f-\varphi\|_p<\varepsilon\text{.}\) Hence, the countable set \(\bigcup_n \mathcal{S}_n\) is dense in \(L^p(E,m)\text{,}\) and so \(L^p(E,m)\) is separable.
    🔗
    🔗
    The \(L^p\)-spaces for Lebesgue measure are not the only separable nice class of separable \(L^p\)-spaces: \(\ell^p\)-spaces are also separable.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.3.12.

    Let \(A\) be a non-empty set and take \(1\leq p < \infty\text{.}\) Then \(\ell^p(A)\) is separable if and only if \(A\) is countable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    If \(A\) is finite, then \(\ell^p(A) \simeq \mathbb{R}^n\text{,}\) where \(n\) is the cardinality of \(A\text{.}\) Hence \(\ell^p(A)\) is separable.
    🔗
    If \(A\) is countably infinite, then there is a bijection from \(A\) to \(\mathbb{N}\text{.}\) This bijection defines an isometric isomorphism from \(\ell^p(A)\) to \(\ell^p\text{.}\) We will thus assume that \(A = \mathbb{N}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The simple functions in \(\ell^p\) are the sequences which are finitely non-zero. Take simple \(a = (a_1,a_2,\ldots,a_n,0,0,\ldots)\) and \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) For \(1\leq i \leq n\text{,}\) choose \(q_i \in \mathbb{Q}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |q_i-a_i|<\frac{\varepsilon}{n^{1/p}} \end{equation*}
    and let \(q = (q_1,q_2,\ldots,q_n,0,\ldots).\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n |q_i-a_i|^p< \varepsilon^p, \end{equation*}
    and so \(\|a-q\|_p<\varepsilon.\) Hence the finitely non-zero rational sequences are dense in the finitely non-zero sequences. As the finitely non-zero sequences are dense in \(\ell^p\) by Proposition 1.3.8, it follows that the rational-valued simple functions are dense in \(\ell^p\text{.}\) There are countably many finitely non-zero rational sequences, and hence \(\ell^p\) is simple.
    🔗
    That \(\ell^p(A)\) is not separable when \(A\) is uncountable is left as an exercise, Exercise 1.4.15.

    Take \(1\leq p <\infty\text{.}\) We have shown that \(L^p(E,m)\) is separable for all Lebesgue measurable \(E \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{,}\) and that \(\ell^p\) is separable. What happens when \(p=\infty\text{?}\) In this case, we are not guaranteed separability.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.3.13.

    The Banach spaces \(L^\infty([0,1],m)\) and \(\ell^\infty\) are not separable.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Consider first \(L^\infty([0,1],m)\text{.}\) For each \(x \in [0,1]\text{,}\) let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F_x = \chi_{[0,x]}. \end{equation*}
    Each \(F_x\) is bounded and measurable, and hence \(F_x \in L^\infty([0,1],m)\text{.}\) Note that, if \(0\leq x < y \leq 1\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|F_x-F_y\|_\infty = \|\chi_{(x,y]}\|_\infty = 1. \end{equation*}
    Suppose \(S\) is a dense subset of \(L^\infty\text{.}\) For each \(x\in [0,1]\text{,}\)fix \(s_x\in S\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \|F_x - s_x\|_\infty. \end{equation*}
    Note that, if \(x \neq y\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} 1 &= \|F_x-F_y\|_\infty\\ &\leq \|F_x -s_x\|_\infty + \|s_x - F_y\|_\infty \\ &< \frac{1}{2} + \|s_x - F_y\|_\infty. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus, when \(x \neq y\text{,}\) \(\|s_x-F_y\|>\frac{1}{2}.\) In, particular, if \(x\neq y\) then \(s_x \neq s_y.\) Hence, the collection \(\{s_x\}_{x\in[0,1]}\) is uncountable. Therefore, \(S\) is uncountable, and hence \(L^\infty([0,1],m)\) is not separable.
    🔗
    The proof that \(\ell^\infty\) is not separable is similar. We will outline the details. Let \(F\) be the collection of all sequences in of zeros and ones. Note that this is an uncountable collection. Indeed, if we can identify the binary expansion of any \(x \in [0,1)\) with a sequence in \(F\text{,}\) and hence \(F\) and \([0,1)\) have the same cardinality. Further, if \(a,b\in F\) and \(a\neq b\text{,}\) then \(a-b\) is a sequence of \(-1,0\) and \(1\) with at least one non-zero term. Thus, \(\|a-b\|_\infty=1\text{.}\) Using \(F\) in the role that \(\{F_x\}_{x\in[0,1]}\) played in \(L^\infty([0,1],m)\text{,}\) we can show that any dense set in \(\ell^\infty\) has to be uncountable. Hence \(\ell^\infty\) is not separable.
