    Section 2.2 Radon measures on locally compact groups

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group. In this section we will study, and prove the existence of, Radon measures which are invariant under the action of \(G\) on its Borel subsets. These are what we call Haar measures.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.1.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group. A non-zero Radon measure \(\mu\) on \(G\) is a left Haar measure if for all Borel sets \(E\subseteq G\) and \(x \in G\)
    \begin{equation*} \mu(xE) = \mu(E). \end{equation*}
    Similarly, a Radon measure \(\mu\) on \(G\) is a right Haar measure if \(\mu(Ex)=\mu(E)\) for all Borel sets \(E \subseteq G\) and \(x \in G\text{.}\) If a measure \(\mu\) is both a left and a right Haar measure we will call \(\mu\) a Haar measure.
    🔗
    🔗
    We have seen some examples of Haar measures and several others are readily available.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.2.

    Let \(G\) be a discrete group (see Example 2.1.2). The counting measure on \(G\) is both a Haar measure.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.3.

    The Lebesgue measure \(m_n\) on the topological group \(\bR^n\) is a Haar measure by Proposition 1.2.7.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.4.

    Let \(G\) be the group \(\bR\backslash \{0\}\) as in Example 2.1.5. Define a measure \(\mu\) on the Borel sets in \(G\) by
    \begin{equation*} \mu(B) = \int_B \frac{1}{|x|}dm(x) \end{equation*}
    for each Borel set \(B \subseteq \bR\backslash\{0\}\text{,}\) where \(m\) is the Lebesgue measure. The measure \(\mu\) is a Haar measure, see Exercise 2.3.5.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.5.

    Let \(\bT\) be the topological group described in Example 2.1.6. Note that if \(z\in \bT\) then there is a unique \(\theta \in [0,2\pi)\) such that \(z= e^{i\theta}\text{.}\) Define a function
    \begin{align*} f \colon [0,2\pi) \amp\rightarrow \bT \\ \theta \amp \mapsto e^{i\theta}. \end{align*}
    Let \(m\) be the Lebesgue measure on \([0,2\pi)\) and define a measure \(\mu\) on \(\bT\) by
    \begin{equation*} \mu(B)= m(f^{-1}(B)). \end{equation*}
    Then \(\mu\) is a Haar measure on \(\bT\text{,}\) Exercise 2.3.6
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.2.6.

    Recall the general linear group \(GL_n(\bR)\) from Example 2.1.7. Identify the \(n\times n\) matrices \(M_{n\times n}(\bR)\) with \(\bR^{n^2}\text{,}\) and let \(m_{n^2}\) be the Lebesgue measure on \(M_{n\times n}(\bR) = \bR^{n^2}.\) Define a measure on the Borel sets of \(GL_m(\bR)\) by
    \begin{equation*} \mu(B) = \int_{B} \frac{1}{|det(X)|^n}\,dm_{n^2}. \end{equation*}
    Note that if \(A \in GL_n(\bR)\) then \(A\) defines an invertible linear map
    \begin{align*} L_A \colon M_{n\times n}(\bR) \amp \rightarrow M_{n\times n}(\bR) \\ X \amp \mapsto AX. \end{align*}
    We can thus view \(L_A\) as an \(n^2 \times n^2\) matrix. One can show that \(\det(L_A) = \det(A)^n\text{.}\)
     1 
    For small \(n\) you can show this by hand, and perhaps use those calculations to construct a general argument. Alternatively, you can show that there is a symmetric orthogonal \(n^2\times n^2\) matrix \(U\) such that \(U L_A U = A \otimes I_n\text{,}\) where \(I_n\) is the identity matrix on \(\bR^n\text{.}\) The result follows immediately from this.
    Thus, that \(\mu\) is a left Haar measure follows from Theorem 1.3.6. That \(\mu\) is a right Haar measure follows from the a similar argument, by considering multiplication on the right.
    🔗
    🔗
    In all the examples above, we have exhibited Haar measures, i.e. measures that are simultaneously both left and right Haar measures. It is possible for a measure to be a left Haar measure and note a right Haar measure, or a right Haar measure and not a left Haar measure, as the next example shows.
    🔗

    Example 2.2.7.

    Let \(G\) be the semidirect product \(\bR\rtimes (0,\infty)\) with product defined as
    \begin{equation*} (x,s)(y,t) = (x+sy,st), \end{equation*}
    for \(x,y \in \bR\) and \(s,t \gt 0\text{.}\) Then \(G\) is a locally compact Hausdorff group (with the product topology on \(\bR \times (0,\infty)\)). As a group, \(G\) is isomorphic to the group of affine linear transformations on \(\bR\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Define functions \(f_L\) and \(f_R\) on \(G (= \bR\times (0,\infty)\subseteq \bR^2)\) by \(f_L(x,s)=\frac{1}{s^2}\) and \(f_R(x,s)=\frac{1}{s}.\) Define measures \(\mu_L\) and \(\mu_R\) on \(G\) by
    \begin{equation*} \mu(B) = \int_B f_L\,dm_2 \text{ and } \mu_R(B) = \int_B f_R \,dm_2 \end{equation*}
    for each Borel \(B \subseteq G\text{,}\) where \(m_2\) is the Lebesgue measure on \(\bR^2\) (restricted to \(G\)).
    🔗
    Take any Borel \(B \subseteq G\) and \((y,t)\in G\text{.}\) Then, with the aide of Tonelli’s Theorem and Theorem 1.3.6, we have
    \begin{align*} \mu_L((y,t)B) \amp= \int_{(y,t)B} f_L\,dm_2 \\ \amp= t^2 \int_{\bR^2} f_L(tx,ts) \chi_{(y,t)B}(tx,ts)\,dm_2(x,s) \\ \amp= t^2 \int_{\bR}\int_{\bR} f_L(tx,ts) \chi_{(y,t)B}(tx,ts)\,dm(x)\,dm(s) \\ \amp= t^2 \int_{\bR}\int_{\bR} f_L(tx,ts) \chi_{B}((y,t)^{-1}(tx,ts))\,dm(x)\,dm(s) \\ \amp= t^2 \int_{\bR}\int_{\bR} f_L(tx,ts) \chi_{B}((t^{-1}y+x,s))\,dm(x)\,dm(s) \\ \amp= t^2 \int_{\bR}\int_{\bR} f_L(tx,ts) \chi_{B}((x,s))\,dm(x)\,dm(s) \\ \amp= t^2\int_{B} \frac{1}{(ts)^2}\,dm_2 = \mu_L(B), \end{align*}
    where the third last equality apples (1.2) (from Lemma 1.3.8). Hence \(\mu_L\) is a left Haar measure. However, \(\mu_L\) is not a right Haar measure. Similarly, one can show that \(\mu_R\) is a right Haar measure, but not a left Haar measure. Details are left to Exercise 2.3.7.
    🔗
    🔗
    Whilst left and right Haar measures may be different, they will always come in pairs. The next lemma shows that a group with a left Haar measure will always have a right Haar measure, and a group with a right Haar measure will always have a left Haar measure. The prove is straightforward, and omitted.
    🔗

    Lemma 2.2.8.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group and let \(\mu\) be a Radon measure on \(G\text{.}\) Define a Radon measure \(\tilde{\mu}\) by
    \begin{equation*} \tilde{\mu}(A) = \mu(A^{-1}). \end{equation*}
    If \(\mu\) is a left Haar measure, then \(\tilde{\mu}\) is a right Haar measure; if \(\mu\) is a right Haar measure, then \(\tilde{\mu}\) is a left Haar measure.
    🔗
    🔗
    We turn our attention now to proving the existence of Haar measures. We will prove the existence of left Haar measures, the existence of right Haar measures will follow by Lemma 2.2.8. From Theorem III.2.4.11 we know that there is a one-to-one correspondence between Radon measures on \(G\) and positive linear functionals on \(C_c(G)\text{.}\) We first address the question of which linear functionals give rise to left Haar measures. We will then prove the existence of such linear functionals.
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.9.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group. A functional \(\phi\) on \(C_c(G)\) is left invariant if
    \begin{equation*} \phi(L_xf)=\phi(f) \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\in G\) and \(f \in C_c(G)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Lemma 2.2.10.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group. Let \(\mu\) be a Radon measure on \(G\) and let \(\phi\) be the positive linear functional on \(C_c(G)\) defined by
    \begin{equation*} \phi(f) = \int_G f \,d\mu \end{equation*}
    for all \(f \in C_c(G).\) The measure \(\mu\) is a left Haar measure, if and only if \(\phi\) is left invariant.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    First assume that \(\mu\) is a left Haar measure. Then, if \(B \subseteq G\) is a Borel set, and \(x \in G\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \int_X L_x \chi_B\,d\mu = \int_X \chi_{xB}\,d\mu = \mu (xB) = \mu(B) = \int_X \chi_B\,d\mu. \end{equation*}
    By the linearity of integration, it follows that
    \begin{equation*} \int_G L_x f\,d\mu = \int_G f\,d\mu \end{equation*}
    for all positive simple functions \(f\) on \(G\text{.}\) That \(\phi\) is left invariant follows by the Simple Approximation Theorem and the Monotone Convergence Theorem.
    🔗
    Assume now that \(\phi\) is left invariant. Fix \(U \subseteq G\) open and take any compact subset \(K \subseteq U\text{.}\) By Urysohn’s Lemma, there is an \(f \in C_c(G)\) such that \(0\leq f \leq 1\text{,}\) \(f(x) = 1\) for \(x\in K\) and \(f(x)=0\) for \(x \in G \backslash U\text{.}\) Take any \(x \in G\text{.}\) Then,
    \begin{align*} \mu(xK) \amp = \int_{xK}1\,d\mu \\ \amp = \int_G L_x \chi_{K}\,d\mu \\ \amp \leq \int_G L_x f\,d\mu \\ \amp = \int_G f\,d\mu\\ \amp \leq \mu(U). \end{align*}
    By the inner regularity of open sets (since \(\mu\) is a Radon measure)
    \begin{align*} \mu(xU) \amp = \sup\{\mu(K) \colon K \subseteq xU \text{ compact}\} \\ \amp = \sup\{\mu(K) \colon x^{-1}K \subseteq U \text{ compact}\} \\ \amp = \sup\{\mu(xK) \colon K \subseteq U \text{ compact}\}\\ \amp \leq \mu(U). \end{align*}
    A similar argument shows that \(\mu(U) \leq \mu(xU).\) We have thus shown that \(\mu(U) = \mu(xU)\) for all \(x \in G\) and \(U \subseteq G\text{.}\) It follows that \(\mu(B) = \mu(xB)\) for all \(x \in G\) and all \(B \subseteq G\) Borel. Thus, \(\mu\) is a left Haar measure.
    🔗
    🔗
    Lemma 2.2.10 (with Theorem III.2.4.11) says that finding a left Haar measure on a locally compact Hausdorff group \(G\) is equivalent to finding a left invariant positive linear functional on \(C_c(G)\text{.}\) We will concentrate on finding such a linear functional. This will be a non-trivial process. We will first introduce a class of left invariant functions from \(G\) to \(\bR\text{.}\) These functions will not, unfortunately, be linear. However, we can use these functions to prove the existence of a left invariant linear functional.
    🔗

    Lemma 2.2.11.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group and let \(f,g\in C_c(G)\) be non-zero positive functions. Then there exists scalars \(c_1,c_2,\ldots,c_n \gt 0\) and \(x_1,\ldots, x_n\in G\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f \leq \sum_{i=1}^n c_i L_{x_i}g. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(K\) be the compact support of \(f\text{,}\) and let \(U = \{x \in G\colon g(x) \gt \|g\|_\infty/2\}.\) The set \(U\) is a non-empty open set. Note that \(\{xU\}_{x\in G}\) is an open cover of \(G\text{,}\) and hence \(K\text{.}\) Since \(K\) is compact, there are \(x_1,\ldots,x_n\in G\) such that \(\{x_iU\}_{i=1}^n\) is an open cover of \(K\text{.}\) If \(x \in K\text{,}\) there exists \(y\in U\) and \(x_j\) such that \(x=x_jy\text{.}\) Since, \(\frac{2g(y)}{\|g\|_\infty}\gt 1\text{,}\) it follows that
    \begin{align*} f(x) \amp \leq \|f\|_\infty\\ \amp \leq \frac{2\|f\|_\infty g(y)}{\|g\|_\infty} \\ \amp = \frac{2\|f\|_\infty}{\|g\|_\infty}L_{x_j}g(x)\\ \amp \leq \frac{2\|f\|_\infty}{\|g\|_\infty} \sum_{i=1}^n L_{x_i}g(x). \end{align*}
    If \(x \notin K\text{,}\) then \(f(x) =0\) and so the inequality also holds there.
    🔗
    🔗
    For positive, non-zero functions \(f,g \in C_c(G)\) we define
    \begin{equation*} [f\colon g] = \inf \left\{\sum_{i=1}^n c_i \colon c_1,\ldots,c_n\gt 0,\ \exists x_1,\ldots,x_n\in G,\ f \leq \sum_{i=1}^n c_i L_{x_i}g \right\} \end{equation*}
    Note that \([f \colon g] \lt \infty\text{,}\) by Lemma 2.2.11. If \(f \leq \sum_{i=1}^n c_i L_{x_i}g\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \|f\|_\infty \leq \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \|g\|_\infty. \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \geq \frac{\|f\|_\infty}{\|g\|_\infty}. \end{equation*}
    It follows that \([f\colon g] \geq \|f\|_\infty/\|g\|_\infty \gt 0.\)
    🔗
    Fix now a non-zero, positive \(f_0 \in C_c(G)\text{.}\) For each non-zero, positive \(g \in C_c(G)\) define a function \(I_g\) on the non-zero positive functions \(f \in C_c(G)\) by
    \begin{equation} I_g(f) = \frac{[f:g]}{[f_0:g]}.\tag{2.1} \end{equation}
    The functions \(I_g\) share a number of properties with left invariant linear functionals. They are, however, not necessarily linear.
    🔗

    Lemma 2.2.12.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group, \(f_1,f_2,g \in C_c(G)\) be non-zero positive functions. Then
    1. \(I_g(L_xf_1) = I_g(f_1) \) for all \(x\in G\text{;}\)
      🔗
    2. \(I_g(cf_1) = cI_g(f_1)\) for all scalars \(c \gt 0\text{;}\)
      🔗
    3. \(I_g(f_1 + f_2) \leq I_g(f_1) + I_g(f_2)\text{;}\)
      🔗
    4. \([f_0:f_1]^{-1} \leq I_g(f_1) \leq [f_1\colon f_0]\text{.}\)
      🔗
    5. if \(\varepsilon \gt 0\text{,}\) there exists an open neighborhood \(U\) of the identity such that
      \begin{equation*} I_g(f_1) + I_g(f_2) \leq I_g(f_1 + f_2) + \varepsilon \end{equation*}
      if the compact support of \(g\) is in \(U\text{;}\)
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Note that, for all \(x \in G\)
    \begin{equation*} f_1 \leq \sum_{i=1}^n c_i L_{x_i}g \end{equation*}
    if and only if
    \begin{equation*} L_xf_1 \leq \sum_{i=1}^n c_i L_{xx_i}g. \end{equation*}
    It follows that \([L_xf_1 \colon g] = [f_1 \colon g]\text{,}\) and hence \(I_g(L_xf_1) = I_g(f_1).\)
    🔗
    Part 2 and Part 3 are (near) immediate, so we will turn our attention to Part 4. If there are positive scalars \(c_1,\ldots,c_n,d_1,\ldots,d_m \lt 0\text{,}\) and \(x_1,\ldots,x_n,y_1,\ldots,y_m \in G\) such that
    \begin{equation*} f_1 \leq \sum_{i=1}^n c_i L_{x_i}f_0 \text{ and }f_0 \leq \sum_{j=1}^m d_j L_{y_j}g, \end{equation*}
    then
    \begin{equation*} f_1 \leq \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^m c_id_j L_{x_iy_j}g. \end{equation*}
    Thus \([f_1 \colon g] \leq [f_1 \colon f_0] [f_0\colon g],\) and so \(I_g(f) \leq [f_1\colon f_0].\) Similarly, \([f_0\colon g] \leq [f_0\colon f_1][f_1 \colon g],\) and so \(I_g(f_1) \geq [f_0\colon f_1]^{-1}\text{,}\) completing Part 4.
    🔗
    We will now prove Part 5 Let \(K\) be the compact support of \(f_1 + f_2\text{,}\) and let \(k\in C_c(G)\) be a positive function such that \(k(x) =1 \) for all \(x \in K.\) Note that such a \(k\) exists by Urysohn’s Lemma. Take \(\delta \gt 0\) and let \(h = f_1 + f_2 + \delta k.\) For \(i = 1,2\text{,}\) let \(h_i = f_i/h\text{.}\) By Proposition 2.1.12 \(h_1\) and \(h_2\) are right uniformly continuous. Thus, there is an open neighborhood \(U\) of the identity such that \(\|R_x h_i - h_i\|_\infty \lt \delta\) for all \(x \in U.\) Thus, if \(y^{-1}x \in U\) then \(|h_i(x) - h(y)|\lt\delta.\)
    🔗
    Assume the the compact support of \(g\) is in \(U\text{.}\) Take scalars \(c_1,\ldots,c_n \gt 0\) and \(x_1,\ldots,x_n \in G\) such that \(h \leq \sum_{j=1}^n c_j L_{x_j}g.\) Then, for \(i = 1,2\text{,}\) for \(x \in G\)
    \begin{equation*} f_i(x) = h(x)h_i(x) \leq \sum_{j=1}^n c_j g(x_j^{-1}x) h_i(x). \end{equation*}
    Note that, if \(g(x_j^{-1}x)\neq 0 \text{,}\) then \(x_j^{-1}x \in U\text{,}\) and hence \(|h_i(x_j)-h_i(x)|\lt \delta\text{,}\) for \(i = 1,2.\) Thus,
    \begin{align*} f_i(x) \amp \leq \sum_{j=1}^n c_j g(x_j^{-1}x) h_i(x) \\ \amp \sum_{j=1}^n c_j g(x_j^{-1}x)(h_i(x_j) + \delta). \end{align*}
    Hence \([f_i\colon g] \leq \sum_{j=1}^n c_j(h_i(x_j)+\delta)\text{,}\) for \(i=1,2\text{.}\) Since, \(h_1 + h_2 \leq 1\) it follows that
    \begin{equation*} [f_1\colon g] + [f_2\colon g] \leq \sum_{j=1}^n c_j(1 + 2 \delta). \end{equation*}
    It follows now that
    \begin{equation*} I_g(f_1) + I_g(f_2)\leq I_g(h)(1+2\delta) \leq (I_g(f_1+f_2) + \delta I_g(k))(1+2\delta), \end{equation*}
    where the last inequality is an application of Part 2 and Part 3. By Part 4, the result follows if
    \begin{equation*} 2\delta[f_1+f_2\colon f_0] + \delta(1+\delta)[k\colon f_0]\lt\varepsilon \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    By Lemma 2.2.12, as the support of \(g\) becomes a smaller and smaller neighborhood of the identity, \(I_g\) gets closer and closer to a left-invariant linear map on the positive functions in \(C_c(G)\text{.}\) It is possible to take a limit of \(I_g\) as the support of \(g\) gets smaller, this is what Cartan [4] does in his proof of the existence (and uniqueness) of the Haar measure. The details of this limit are rather complicated, and so we take another route to the proof.
    🔗
    Let \(\{X_\lambda\}_{\lambda \in \Lambda}\) a collection of subsets of \(\bR\text{.}\) Let \(X = \prod_{\lambda \in \Lambda}X_\lambda\) be the Cartesian product. Let \(f \colon \Lambda \rightarrow \bR\) be a function satisfying \(f(\lambda) \in X_\lambda\) for all \(\lambda \in \Lambda\text{.}\) Then \(f\) defines a unique element of \(X\text{:}\) \((f(\lambda))_\lambda\text{.}\) Conversely, if \(x=(x_\lambda)_\lambda \in X\text{,}\) then \(x\) determines a function \(f \colon \Lambda \rightarrow \bR\) by \(f(\lambda)= x_\lambda.\) Thus, the set \(X\) can be viewed as the collection of all function \(f \colon \Lambda \rightarrow \bR\) such that \(f(\lambda) \in X_\lambda\) for each \(\lambda \in \Lambda\text{.}\) This viewpoint of cartesian products will be necessary in the following proof.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.13.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group. Then there is a left Haar measure on \(G\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(C_c^+(G)\) be the collection of all non-zero, positive functions in \(C_c(G)\text{.}\) Let \(f_0\) be a fixed function in \(C_c^+(G)\) and let \(I_g\) be defined for each \(g \in C_c^+(G)\) as in (2.1). For each \(f \in C_c^+(G)\) let \(X_f \subseteq \bR\) be the closed interval
    \begin{equation*} X_f = \left[[f_0\colon f]^{-1}, [f\colon f_0] \right], \end{equation*}
    and let \(X = \prod_{f \in C_c^+(G)}X_f\) be the Cartesian product, endowed with the product topology. By Tychonoff’s Theorem, \(X\) is a compact space, and by Lemma 2.2.12 Part 4, \(I_g \in X\) for all \(g \in C_c^+(G).\)
    🔗
    For each open neighborhood \(U\) of the identity in \(G\) let \(K_U\) be the closure of the set \(\{I_g \colon \mathop{\mathrm{supp}}(g) \subseteq U\}\) in \(X\text{.}\) If \(U_1,\ldots,U_n\) are open neighborhoods of the identity, then
    \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{i=1}^n K_{U_i} \subseteq K_{\bigcap_{i=1}^n U_i}\neq \emptyset. \end{equation*}
    Hence, by Proposition III.2.3.1, \(\bigcap_{U} K_U \neq \emptyset\text{.}\) Take \(I \in \bigcap_{U} K_U \neq \emptyset.\) We will show that \(I\) can be extended to a left invariant linear functional on \(C_c(G).\)
    🔗
    Note that \(I \in K_U\) for each neighborhood \(U\) of the identity. Thus, given \(\varepsilon \gt 0\) and \(f_1,\ldots,f_n \in C_c^+(G)\text{,}\) there is an open neighborhood \(U\) of the identity and a \(g \in C_c^+(G)\) with \(\mathrm{supp}(g) \subseteq G\text{,}\) such that
    \begin{equation*} |I(f_i) - I_g(f_i)| \lt \varepsilon \end{equation*}
    for \(i = 1,\ldots,n.\) This is, essentially, all we need to complete the proof.
    🔗
    Take \(f \in C_c(G)\) and \(c \gt 0\text{.}\) For \(\varepsilon\gt 0\text{,}\) there is an \(I_g\) such that
    \begin{equation*} |I(cf) - I_g(cf)|\lt \varepsilon \text{ and }|I(f) - I_g(f)|\lt \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Hence, by part Lemma 2.2.12 Part 2,
    \begin{align*} |I(cf)-cI(f)| \amp\leq |I(cf) - I_g(cf)| + |cI_g(f) - cI_g(f)| \\ \amp \lt \varepsilon + c \varepsilon. \end{align*}
    As this holds for all \(\varepsilon \gt 0\text{,}\) it follows that \(I(cf) = cI(f).\) Similarly, if \(f \in C_c(G)\) and \(x \in G\text{,}\) for \(\varepsilon \gt 0\) there is an \(I_g\) such that
    \begin{equation*} |I(L_xf) - I_g(L_xf)|\lt \varepsilon \text{ and }|I(f) - I_g(f)|\lt \varepsilon, \end{equation*}
    and so, by Lemma 2.2.12 Part 1,
    \begin{equation*} |I(L_x f) - I(f)| \leq |I(L_x f) - I(L_x g)| + |I(f) - I(L_x g)| \lt 2 \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Hence, \(I(L_x f) = I(f).\)
    🔗
    Now take \(f_1,f_2 \in C_c^+(G)\) and \(\varepsilon \gt 0\text{.}\) By Lemma 2.2.12 Part 5, there is a neighborhood \(U\) of the identity such that if \(\mathrm{supp}(g) \subseteq U\) then \(I_g(f_1) + I_g(f_2) \leq I_g(f_1 + f_2) + \varepsilon.\) Choose \(g\) with \(\mathrm{supp}(g)\subseteq U\) such that
    \begin{align*} \amp|I(f_1) - I_g(f_1)|\lt\varepsilon,\ |I(f_2) - I_g(f_2)|\lt\varepsilon, \text{ and} \\ \amp |I(f_1+f_2) - I_g(f_1+f_2)|\lt\varepsilon. \end{align*}
    Note that, by Lemma 2.2.12 Part 3, and the choice of \(g\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} I_g(f_1 + f_2) \leq I_g(f_1)+I_g(f_2) \leq I_g(f_1+f_2) + \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Hence
    \begin{equation*} |I_g(f_1)+I_g(f_2)-I(f_1+f_2)|\leq \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Hence,
    \begin{align*} |I(f_1) +I(f_2) - I(f_1 + f_2)| \leq \amp |I(f_1)-I_g(f_1)| + |I(f_2)-I_g(f_2)| \\ \amp +|I_g(f_1) + I_g(f_2) - I(f_1+f_2)|\\ \leq \amp |I(f_1)-I_g(f_1)| + |I(f_2)-I_g(f_2)| \\ \amp +|I_g(f_1) + I_g(f_2) - I_g(f_1+f_2)| \\ \amp+ |I_g(f_1+f_2)- I(f_1+f_2)| \\ \lt\amp 4 \varepsilon. \end{align*}
    It follows that \(I(f_1 + f_2) = I(f_1) + I(f_2).\)
    🔗
    We extend \(I\) to all of \(C_c(G)\) by setting \(I(f) = I(f_+)-I(f_-)\) where \(f = f_+ - f_-\) is the decomposition of \(f\) into its positive and negative parts. That this extension is linear follows by the same reasoning as we saw in the proof of Theorem III.2.4.8. We have just shown that there is a positive left invariant linear functional on \(C_c(G)\text{.}\) By Lemma 2.2.10 and Theorem III.2.4.11 it follows that there is a left Haar measure on \(G\text{.}\)

This proof follows the ideas of [9]. For a general proof see, e.g., Chapter 11 of [7]. Note that, Example 2.1.3, Example 2.1.5, Example 2.1.6, and Example 2.1.7 are all \(\sigma\)-compact groups; the discrete groups in Example 2.1.2 are \(\sigma\)-compact when countable.

    Theorem 2.2.14.

    Let \(G\) be a locally compact Hausdorff group. If \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are two left Haar measures on \(G\text{,}\) then there is a constant \(c \gt 0\) such that \(\mu = c \nu.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof (of \(\sigma\)-compact case).

    Let \(G\) be a \(\sigma\)-compact locally compact Hausdorff group, and let \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) be two Haar measures on \(G\text{.}\) Since \(G\) is \(\sigma\)-compact and \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are Radon measures, both \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are \(\sigma\)-finite.
    🔗
    Assume that \(\mu \leq \nu\text{.}\) Then \(\mu \ll \nu\text{.}\) Hence, by the Radon-Nikodym Theorem, there is a non-negative Borel measurable function \(h\) such that
    \begin{equation*} \mu(E) = \int_E h\,d\nu \end{equation*}
    for all Borel sets \(E \subseteq G\text{.}\) Note that, since \(\mu\leq \nu\text{,}\) \(0\leq h \leq 1.\) Further, by Exercise II.7.5.3, if \(f\) is \(\mu\)-integrable, then
    \begin{equation*} \int_X f\,d\mu = \int_X fh\,d\nu. \end{equation*}
    Thus, for \(f \in C_c(G)\) and \(y \in G\text{,}\) since \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are left Haar measures,
    \begin{align*} \int_G fh\,d\nu \amp = \int_G f \,d\mu = \int_G f(y^{-1}x)\,d\mu(x)\\ \amp = \int_G f(y^{-1}x)h(x)\,d\nu(x)=\int_G f(x)h(y^{-1}x)\,d\nu(x). \end{align*}
    Thus, for all \(f \in C_x(G)\) and \(y\in G\text{,}\) \(\int_G f(x)[h(x)-h(z^{-1}x)]\,d\nu(x) = 0\text{,}\) and hence \(h-L_z h = 0\) almost everywhere.
    🔗
    Consider, now the function
    \begin{align*} H \colon G \times G \amp \rightarrow \bR \\ (x,y) \amp \mapsto |h(y^{-1}x)-h(x)|. \end{align*}
    Let \(\tilde{\mu}\) be the completion of \(\mu\text{,}\) as in Theorem II.2.2.3. By Tonelli’s Theorem
    \begin{align*} 0=\int_{G\times G} H \,d(\tilde{\mu}\times \tilde{\mu}) \amp= \int_G \int_G |h(y^{-1}x)-h(x)|\,d\tilde{\mu}(x)\,d\tilde{\mu}(y) \\ \amp= \int_G \int_G |h(y^{-1}x)-h(x)|\,d\tilde{\mu}(y)\,d\tilde{\mu}(x). \end{align*}
    Hence, for almost all \(x\in G\text{,}\) \(\int_G|h(y^{-1}x)-h(x)|\,d\tilde{\mu}(y)=0\text{.}\) Using again, that \(\mu\) is a left Haar measure, we have for almost all \(x \in G\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} 0=\int_G|h(y^{-1}x)-h(x)|\,d\tilde{\mu}(y)=\int_G|h(y^{-1})-h(x)|\,d\tilde{\mu}(y). \end{equation*}
    Hence, \(h\) is constant almost everywhere, and thus \(\mu = c \nu\) for a constant \(0 \lt c \leq 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now consider the general case, when we do not necessarily have \(\mu \leq \nu\text{.}\) Assume that \(\mu \neq \nu\text{.}\) Note that \(\mu + \nu\) is also a left Haar measure, and \(\mu \leq \mu + \nu.\) By the above argument, there is a constant \(0 \lt c \lt 1\text{,}\) such that \(\mu = c(\mu + \nu)\text{.}\) Hence,
    \begin{equation*} \mu = \frac{c}{1-c}\nu, \end{equation*}
    completing the proof.
