    Subsection 2.1 Open and closed sets in \(\mathbb{R}\)

    Definition 2.1.3.

    A subset \(A \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is open if for all \(x\in A\) there exists \(r>0\) such that \((x-r, x+r) \subseteq A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    A subset \(B \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is closed if its complement \(\mathbb{R}\backslash B\) is open.
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.4.

    We list here some examples of open set, closed sets, sets which are both open and closed, and sets which are neither.
    🔗
    1. \(\emptyset\) and \(\mathbb{R}\) are open and closed.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Open intervals are open, i.e. intervals of the form \((-\infty, b),\ (a,+\infty)\) and \((a,b)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. Closed intervals are closed, i.e. intervals of the form \((-\infty, b],\ [a,+\infty)\) and \([a,b]\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    4. Finite subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\) are closed.
      🔗
      🔗
    5. The rational numbers \(\mathbb{Q}\) are neither open nor closed.
      🔗
      🔗
    6. The intervals \((a,b]\) and \([a,b)\) are neither open nor closed.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.5.

    Let \(X \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) A set \(A \subseteq X\) is open in \(X\) if there is an open subset \(A' \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) such that \(A = A' \cap X\text{.}\) Similarly, a set \(B \subseteq X\) is closed in \(X\) if there is a closed subset \(B \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) such that \(B = B' \cap X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The key properties of open sets are:
    🔗
    1. \(\emptyset,\ \mathbb{R}\) are open;
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(\mathcal{F}\) is any collection of open sets then
      \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{F\in \mathcal{F}} F \text{ is open}; \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. if \(A_1,\ldots, A_n\) are open then
      \begin{equation*} \bigcap_{i=1}^n A_i \text{ is open.} \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗

    Remark 2.1.6.

    Using DeMorgan’s identities you can use (ii) to show that an arbitrary intersection of closed sets is closed; and using (iii) you can show a finite union of closed sets is closed.
    🔗
    🔗
    A useful characterization of closed sets in \(\mathbb{R}\) is the following.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.1.7.

    A subset \(B \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is closed if and only if given any convergent sequence \((x_n)_n\) of \(\mathbb{R}\) with \(x_n \in B\) for all \(n\text{,}\) we must have \(\lim x_n \in B\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.8.

    Let \(A\subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) The closure of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\overline{A}\) is the smallest closed set containing \(A\text{.}\) The interior of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(A^\circ\text{,}\) is the largest open set contained in \(A\text{.}\) A subset \(A\subseteq \mathbb{R}\) is dense in \(\mathbb{R}\) if \(\overline{A}=\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If \(X \subseteq \mathbb{R}\) and \(A \subseteq X\text{,}\) then the closure of \(A\) in \(X\) is the set \(\overline{A} \cap X\text{.}\) Similarly, the interior of \(A\) in \(X\) is the set \(A^\circ \cap X\text{.}\) A set \(A\subseteq X\) is dense in \(X\) if the closure of \(A\) in \(X\) is all of \(X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.1.9.

    The rational numbers \(\mathbb{Q}\) are dense in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) The irrational numbers \(\mathbb{R}\backslash \mathbb{Q}\) are also dense in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We will use the following property of open sets later in the course.
    🔗

    Proposition 2.1.10.

    Every non-empty open set in \(\mathbb{R}\) is the disjoint union of countably many open intervals.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(A\subseteq \mathbb{R}\) be non-empty open set. Take \(x\in A\text{.}\) Since \(A\) is open, there exists \(y>x\) such that \((x,y) \subseteq A\text{.}\) Similarly, there exists \(z<x\) with \((z,x) \subseteq A\text{.}\) Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} a_x &= \inf \{z \colon (z,x) \subseteq A\} \\ b_x &= \sup \{y \colon (x,y) \subseteq A\} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    (allowing for the possibility that \(a_x = -\infty\) and \(b_x = +\infty\)). Let \(I_x = (a_x, b_x)\text{.}\) We will show that \(I_x\subseteq A\) but \(a_x,b_x \notin A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take \(w\in I_x\text{.}\) Suppose \(x<w<b_x\text{.}\) By definition of \(b_x\) as the supremum, there exists \(y>w\) such that \((x,y) \subseteq A\text{.}\) Thus \(w\in A\text{.}\) Similarly, if \(w< x\) we get \(w\in A\text{.}\) Hence \(I_x \subseteq A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now assume \(b_x \in \mathbb{R}\) and \(b_x\in A\text{.}\) Then, since \(A\) is open , there is an \(r>0\) such that \((b_x-r, b_x+r) \subseteq A\text{.}\) But then \((x, b_x+r)\subseteq A\text{.}\) This contradicts the definition of \(b_x\) as a supremum, and hence \(b_x\notin A\text{.}\) Similarly, \(a_x\notin A\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take \(x,y \in A\text{.}\) Note that, either \(I_x = I_y\) or \(I_x \cap I_y= \emptyset\text{.}\) Hence the collection \(\{I_x \colon x\in A\}\) is a disjoint collection of open intervals. Further, since \(x\in I_x\) for all \(x\in A\text{,}\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \bigcup_{x\in A} I_x. \end{equation*}
    It remains to show that there are countably many distinct intervals \(I_x\text{.}\) Note that, since \(\mathbb{Q}\) is dense in \(\mathbb{R}\text{,}\) each open interval \(I_x\) contains rational numbers. Thus, the set \(\{I_x\colon x\in A\}\) can be indexed by a subset of \(\mathbb{Q}\text{.}\) Since \(\mathbb{Q}\) is countable this indexing set is countable, and we are done.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.1.11.

    A countable intersection of open sets if called a \(G_\delta\)-set. A countable union of closed sets is called an \(F_\sigma\)-set.
    🔗
    🔗

    Remark 2.1.12.

    The term \(G_\delta\) comes from German. The German for area is `Gebeit’, and the German for intersection is `Durschnitt’. Hence a \(G_\delta\) set comes from an intersection of areas.
    🔗
    The term \(F_\sigma\text{,}\) on the other hand, comes from another language altogether. In French the word for closed is `fermé’, and the word for sum is `somme’. Hence an \(F_\sigma\) set is a sum of closed sets.
    🔗
    🔗
    Recall that continuity of functions on \(\mathbb{R}\) can be defined solely in terms of open sets, with out making reference to the distance function on \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Proposition 2.1.13.

    Let \(A,\ B \subseteq \mathbb{R}\text{.}\) A function \(f \colon A \rightarrow B\) is continuous if and only \(f^{-1}(C)\) is open in \(A\) for all open sets \(C\) in \(B\text{.}\)
