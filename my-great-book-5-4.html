    Section 2.2 Properties of measures

    Proposition 2.2.1.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space. Then the following hold
    🔗
    1. (Monotonicity) If \(A, B \in \mathcal{M}\) with \(B \subseteq A\text{,}\) then \(\mu(B)\leq \mu(A)\text{;}\)\index{measure!monotonicity}
      🔗
      🔗
    2. (Excision) If \(A,B \in \mathcal{M}\) with \(B\subseteq A\) and \(\mu(A)&lt;\infty\text{,}\) then \(\mu(A \backslash B) = \mu(A) - \mu(B)\text{;}\)\index{measure!excision property}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. (Countable monotonicity) If \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of measurable sets, and \(E\) is a measurable set such that \(E \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k\text{,}\) then \(\mu(E) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k).\)\index{measure!countable monotonicity}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take \(A, B \in \mathcal{M}\) with \(B \subseteq A\text{.}\) Note we can write \(A\) as a disjoint union \($ A = B \cup (A \backslash B).\)$ Thus \begin{equation}\label{eq: excision} \mu(A) = \mu(B) + \mu(A \backslash B). \end{equation} Thus, \(\mu(B) \leq \mu(A)\text{.}\) If \(\mu(A)&lt;\infty\text{,}\) then it follows that \(\mu(B) &lt; \infty\) and \(\mu(A\backslash B) &lt; \infty\text{.}\) Hence, it follows from [cross-reference to target(s) "eq-excision" missing or not unique] that \($ \mu(A\backslash B) = \mu(A) - \mu(B).\footnote{That \)\mu(A)\(is finite is imporant here. Otherwise we could be looking at \)\infty - \infty\(, which is undefined.}\)$
    🔗
    Now let \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) be a countable collection of sets in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) Let \(A_1 = E_1\text{.}\) Let \(A_2 = E_2 \backslash A_1\text{.}\) Continuing in this way, we let \($ A_k = E_k \backslash \bigcup_{n=1}^{k-1} A_n.\)$ In this way, we get a countable family \(\{A_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) of disjoint measurable sets, which satisfies \($ \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k,\)$ and \(\mu(A_k) \leq \mu(E_k)\) for each \(k\text{.}\) Thus, if \(E \subseteq \bigcup_k E_k\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu(E) &\leq \mu\left( \bigcup_{k=1}^n E_k \right) \\ &= \mu\left( \bigcup_{k=1}^n A_k \right) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(A_k) \\ &\leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k).\qedhere \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    The next theorem is called \emph{the continuity of measure}.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.2.

    [Continuity of measure]\index{measure!continuity}\label{thm: continuity of measure} Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space.
    🔗
    1. If \(\{A_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of measurable sets satisfying \(A_k \subseteq A_{k+1}\) (i.e. \(\{A_k\}_k\) ascending measurable sets), then \($ \mu\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^\infty A_k \right) = \lim_{k\rightarrow \infty} \mu(A_k).\)$
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(\{B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of measurable sets satisfying \(B_{k+1} \subseteq B_{k}\) (i.e. \(\{B_k\}_k\) descending measurable sets) and \(\mu(B_1)&lt;\infty\text{,}\) then \($ \mu\left(\bigcap_{k=1}^\infty B_k \right) = \lim_{k\rightarrow \infty} \mu(B_k).\)$
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(\{A_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) be an countable collection of ascending measurable sets, and let \(A = \bigcup_k A_k\text{.}\) If \(\mu(A_{k_0}) = \infty\) for some index \(k_0\text{,}\) then \(\mu(A) = \infty\) since \(A_{k_0} \subseteq A\) (by the monotonicity of \(\mu\)). Hence \($ \infty = \mu(A) = \mu(A_{k_0}) = \lim_k \mu(A_k).\)$
    🔗
    Assume now that \(\mu(A_k) &lt; \infty\) for all \(k\text{.}\) Let \(B_1 = A_1\) and let \($B_k = A_k \backslash A_{k-1}.\)$ Then \(\{B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of disjoint measurable sets satisfying
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} A_k = \bigcup_{m=1}^k B_m. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(A\) is the disjoint union \(A = \bigcup_k B_k\text{.}\) Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \lim_{k} \mu(A_k) &= \lim_k \mu\left(\bigcup_{m=1}^k B_k\right) \\ &= \lim_k \sum_{m=1}^k \mu(B_m) \\ &= \sum_{m=1}^\infty \mu(B_m)\\ &= \mu(A). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus (i) holds.
    🔗
    Now assume \(\{B_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a descending collection of measurable sets, with \(\mu(B_1)&lt;\infty\text{.}\) For each \(k\text{,}\) let \(D_k = B_1 \backslash B_k\text{.}\) Then \(\{D_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is an ascending sequence of measurable sets. By part (i), we have that \begin{equation}\label{eq: cont meas 1} \mu\left( \bigcup_k D_k \right) = \lim_k \mu(D_k) = \lim_k [\mu(B_1) - \mu(B_k)] = \mu(B_1) - \lim_k \mu(B_k). \end{equation} Note that we have used the fact that \(\mu(B_1)&lt;\infty\) in order to use the excision property in [cross-reference to target(s) "eq-cont-meas-1" missing or not unique]. By De Morgan’s identities \($ \bigcup_k D_k = \bigcup_k (B_1 \backslash B_k) = B_1 \backslash \bigcap_k B_k. \)$ Hence \begin{equation}\label{eq: cont meas 2} \mu\left(\bigcup_k D_k\right) = \mu(B_1) - \mu\left(\bigcap_k B_k \right). \end{equation} Combining [cross-reference to target(s) "eq-cont-meas-1" missing or not unique] and [cross-reference to target(s) "eq-cont-meas-2" missing or not unique] we get \($ \mu \left(\bigcap_k B_k \right) = \lim_k \mu(B_k).\qedhere\)$
    🔗
    🔗
    Not all measure spaces that we encounter will be complete. Indeed, we will see that Borel measures need not be complete. We will see an important example of this in the real line later.
    🔗
    However, the next theorem shows that a measure space that is not complete can always be extended to be complete.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.2.3.

    \index{measure space!completion} Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space. Define \(\M_0\) to be the collection of subset of \(X\) \($ \M_0 = \{A \cup B \colon A \in \mathcal{M},\ B \subseteq C\text{ for some }C \in \mathcal{M} \text{ with }\mu(C) = 0\}.\)$ For \(E = A \cup B\) in \(\M_0\) where \(A \in \mathcal{M}\) and \(B \subseteq C\in \mathcal{M}\) with \(\mu(C)=0\text{,}\) define \(\mu_0(A\cup B) = \mu(A)\text{.}\) Then \(\M_0\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra and \((X,\M_0,\mu_0)\) is a complete measure space.\index{measure space!complete}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.2.4.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space, and let \((X,\M_0,\mu_0)\) be the complete measure space constructed from \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) in Theorem Theorem 2.2.3. Then \((X,\M_0,\mu_0)\) is the \emph{completion} of \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\text{.}\)\index{measure space!completion}
