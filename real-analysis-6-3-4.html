    Section 11.2 The dual of \(L^p\)-spaces

    In our study of \(L^p\)-spaces so far we have, mostly, looked at arbitrary measure spaces. When studying dual spaces, our strongest results will come when we work with \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces. Our first goal is to describe the linear functionals on \(L^p(X,\mu)\) when \(1\leq p<\infty.\) Here, Hölder’s inequality will play a significant role. When \(p=\infty\text{,}\) the study of the dual is more involved. We will discuss the dual of \(L^\infty\)-spaces at the end of this section.
    🔗
    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space, fix \(p\) with \(1\leq p<\infty\) and let \(q\) be conjugate to \(p\text{.}\) For \(g \in L^q(X,\mu)\text{,}\) we define a linear map \(T_g\) from \(L^p(X,\mu)\) to \(\mathbb{R}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T_g(f) = \int_X gf\, d\mu. \end{equation*}
    When \(p>1\text{,}\) that \(gf\) is integrable follows from Hölders inequality; when \(p=1\text{,}\) \(g \in L^{\infty}(X,\mu)\) and so \(gf\) is integrable. The linearity of \(T_g\) then follows from the linearity of integration. Further,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |T_g(f)| \leq \|g\|_q\|f\|_p \end{equation*}
    for all \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{,}\) and so \(T_g\) is continuous by . Thus the map \(g \mapsto T_g\) defines a map from \(L^q(X,\mu)\) to \((L^p(X,\mu))^*\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Lemma 11.2.1.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space, fix \(p\) with \(1< p<\infty\) and let \(q\) be conjugate to \(p\text{.}\) The map
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} T \colon & L^q(X,\mu) \rightarrow (L^p(X,\mu))^* \\ & g \mapsto T_g \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    is a linear isometric map (i.e. \(\|T_g\|_{\mathrm{op}} = \|g\|_q\) for all \(g \in L^q(X,\mu)\)).
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    The linearity of the map \(T\) follows from the linearity of integration. Suppose \(1<p<\infty\) and take \(g \in L^q(X,\mu).\) By Hölder’s inequality
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} |T_g(f)| \leq \|g\|_q\|f\|_p \end{equation*}
    for all \(f \in L^p(X,\mu).\) Hence \(\|T_\mathrm{op}\| \leq \|g\|_q.\) By , there is an \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\) with \(\|f\|_p=1\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T_g(f) = \|g\|_q, \end{equation*}
    and hence \(\|T_g\|_\mathrm{op}=\|g\|_q.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    When \(p=1\) we can prove similar, but we need some restrictions on our measure space.
    🔗

    Lemma 11.2.2.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a \(\sigma\)-finite measure space. The map
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} T \colon & L^\infty(X,\mu) \rightarrow (L^1(X,\mu))^* \\ & g \mapsto T_g \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    is a linear isometric map.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    We, again, have that \(T\) is linear, by the linearity of integration. Fix \(g \in L^{\infty}(X,\mu)\text{.}\) It is clear that \(\|T_g\|_\mathrm{op}\leq \|g\|_\infty.\) Fix \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A= \{ x\in X \colon |g(x)|> \|g\|_\infty - \varepsilon\}. \end{equation*}
    By the definition of \(\|g\|_\infty\text{,}\) \(A\) has non-zero measure. Since \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, there is a \(B \subseteq A\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 < \mu(B) < \infty. \end{equation*}
    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x) = \frac{1}{\mu(B)} \operatorname{sgn}(g(x))\chi_B(x). \end{equation*}
    Note
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_X |f|\,d\mu = \frac{1}{\mu(B)}\int_B 1\,d\mu = 1. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(f \in L^1(X,\mu)\) and \(\|f\|_1=1\text{.}\) Further
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \|T_g\|_\mathrm{op} &\geq |T_g(f)|\\ &= \int_X gf\,d\mu\\ &= \frac{1}{\mu(B)} \int_B |g|\,d\mu\\ &\geq \frac{(\|g\|_\infty - \varepsilon)\mu(B)}{\mu(B)}\\ &= \|g\|_\infty -\varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    As this holds for all \(\varepsilon >0\text{,}\) we have that \(\|T_g\|_\mathrm{op} = \|g\|_\infty.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We will show that, when \(1\leq p<\infty\text{,}\)
     1 
    In the \(p=1\) case we will work with a \(\sigma\)-finite space
    that the map \(T\) is bijective. I.e., we will show that if \(\varphi \in (L^p(X,\mu))\) then there is a \(g \in L^q(X,\mu)\) such that \(\varphi = T_g\text{.}\) We need the following lemma first.
    🔗

    Lemma 11.2.3.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space, let \(1\leq p<\infty\) and let \(q\) be conjugate to \(p\text{.}\) Suppose \(g\) is a measurable function on \(X\) such that \(\{x \in X \colon g(x) \neq 0\}\) is \(\sigma\)-finite. Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} M =\sup\left\{\left| \int_X gf\,d\mu \right| \colon f \in L^p(X,\mu) \text{ simple, and }\|f\|_p=1\right\}. \end{equation*}
    If \(M< \infty\text{,}\) then \(g \in L^q(X,\mu)\) and \(\|g\|_q =M.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    First assume \(p>1\text{,}\) so that \(q < \infty\text{.}\) Let \(\{E_n\}_n\) be an increasing sequence of finite measure sets, such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty E_n = \{x \in X \colon g(x) \neq 0 \}. \end{equation*}
    By the Simple Approximation Theorem, there is a sequence \((\phi_n)_n\) of simple functions converging pointwise to \(g\) with \(|\phi_n|\leq |g|\text{.}\) Let \(g_n = \phi_n \chi_{E_n}\text{.}\) For each \(n\text{,}\) \(g_n\) is a simple function which vanishes outside a set of finite measure. Thus, \(g_n \in L^q(X,\mu)\text{.}\) For each \(n\text{,}\) let \(f_n\) be the function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f_n = \|g_n\|_q^{1-q}\operatorname{sgn}(g_n)|g_n|^{q-1}. \end{equation*}
    As in the proof of , \(\|f_n\|_p = 1.\) Now, by Fatou’s Lemma, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \|g\|_q &\leq \liminf_{n\rightarrow \infty} \|g_n\|_q\\ &= \liminf_{n\rightarrow \infty} \int_X |g_n f_n| \, d\mu \\ & \leq \liminf_{n\rightarrow \infty} \int_X |g f_n| \, d\mu \\ & = \liminf_{n\rightarrow \infty} \int_X g f_n \, d\mu\\ & \leq M. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus \(g \in L^q(X,\mu)\) and \(\|g\|_q \leq M.\) By Hölder’s inequality, \(M \leq \|g\|_q\text{,}\) and so \(\|g\|_q = M.\)
    🔗
    Suppose now that \(p=1\) and \(q = \infty\text{.}\) We proceed in a way similar to the proof of . Fix \(\varepsilon>0\) and let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A = \{x \colon |g(x)| \geq M + \varepsilon \}. \end{equation*}
    If \(\mu(A)>0\text{,}\) then since \(\{x \in X \colon g(x) \neq 0 \}\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, there exists measurable \(B \subseteq A\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 0 < \mu(B) < \infty. \end{equation*}
    Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f = \frac{1}{\mu(B)} \operatorname{sgn}(g) \chi_B. \end{equation*}
    Then \(\|f\|_1 = 1\) and \(\int_X fg\,d\mu \geq M + \varepsilon.\) However, since \(f\) is a simple function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left|\int_X gf \,d\mu\right| \leq M. \end{equation*}
    This is a contradiction, hence \(\mu(A) = 0\text{.}\) Therefore \(g \in L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{.}\) That \(\|g\|_\infty = M\) is clear.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 11.2.4. Riesz Representation Theorem for \(L^p\)-spaces.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be a measure space, let \(1<p<\infty\) and let \(q\) be conjugate to \(p\text{.}\) The map
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} T \colon &L^q(X,\mu) \rightarrow (L^p(X,\mu))^*\\ &g \mapsto T_g \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    is an isometric, linear bijection.
    🔗
    If \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, then the map
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} T \colon &L^\infty(X,\mu) \rightarrow (L^1(X,\mu))^*\\ &g \mapsto T_g \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    is an isometric, linear bijection.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Let \(p\) and \(q\) be conjugate with \(1<p,q<\infty\text{.}\) That \(T\) is a linear isometric map is shown in . It remains to show that \(T\) is a surjective. In the proof we will first assume \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is a finite measure space; then a \(\sigma\)-finite measure space; finally we will just assume that \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is a measure space.
    🔗
    Assume \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is a finite measure space, and take \(\varphi \in (L^p(X,\mu))^*.\) Since \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is finite, \(\chi_E \in L^p(X,\mu)\) for all \(E \in \mathcal{M}.\) Define a map \(\nu\) on \(\mathcal{M}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) = \varphi(\chi_E). \end{equation*}
    Note that if \(\chi_E = 0\) almost everywhere, then \(\varphi(E) = 0\text{,}\) and so \(\nu(E) = 0.\) In particular, \(\nu(\emptyset) = 0.\) Let \(\{E_k\}_{k\geq 1}\) be a countable collection of disjoint measurable sets, and let \(E = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k.\) For each \(k\text{,}\) let \(B_n = \bigcup_{k=1}^n E_k.\) The sequence \((|\chi_{B_n}-\chi_E|^p)_n\) converges pointwise to \(0\text{.}\) Thus, by the Lebesgue Dominated Convergence theorem, \((\chi_{B_n})_n\) converges to \(\chi_{E}\) with respect to the \(p\)-norm. Thus, by the continuity of \(\varphi\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu(E_k) &= \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \sum_{k=1}^n \nu(E_k)\\ &= \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \varphi(\chi_{B_n})\\ &= \varphi(E)\\ &= \nu(E). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    We have shown that \(\nu\) is a signed-measure on \((X,\mathcal{M}).\) Clearly \(\nu \ll \mu\text{.}\) By the Radon-Nikodym Theorem for signed-measures, , there is a integrable function \(g\) on \(X\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) = \int_E g \,d\mu = \varphi(\chi_E) \end{equation*}
    for all \(E \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\) By the linearity of integration, if \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\) is a simple function, then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(f) = \int_X gf\,d\mu. \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sup \left\{\left|\int_X gf \,d\mu \right| \colon f \in L^p(X,\mu) \text{ simple, and }\|f\|_p=1\right\}\leq \|\varphi\|_\mathrm{op}. \end{equation*}
    Hence, by , \(g \in L^q(X,\mu).\) As \(T_g(f) = \varphi(f)\) for all simple functions \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{,}\) it follows from , that \(\varphi = T_g.\) Thus \(T\) is surjective.
    🔗
    Assume now that \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is \(\sigma\)-finite. Let \(\{E_n\}_n\) be an increasing sequence of finite measure sets, such that \(X = \bigcup_{n=1}^\infty E_n.\) We embed \(L^q(E_n,\mu)\) in \(L^q(X,\mu)\) be setting \(f(x) = 0\) for \(x \in X\backslash E_n\) for each \(f \in L^q(E_n,\mu)\text{.}\) Note that this embedding preserves the \(q\)-norm. Since \(\mu(E_n)<\infty\text{,}\) the above arguments shows that for each \(n\) there is a \(g_n \in L^q(E_n,\mu)\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \varphi|_{L^q(E_n,\mu)} = T_{g_n}. \end{equation*}
    Thus, \(\|g_n\|_q \leq \|\varphi\|_{\mathrm{op}}\) for all \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    By the uniqueness of the Radon-Nikodym derivative in , if \(n\geq m\) then \(g_n|_{E_m} = g_m\) almost everywhere. Thus, as elements of \(L^q(X,\mu)\) we have \(g_n|_{E_m} = g_m\) when \(n \geq m.\) We can thus define a function \(g\) almost everywhere by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} g(x) = g_n(x) \text{ if } n \in E_n. \end{equation*}
    Thus, the sequence \((|g_n|^q)_n\) is an increasing sequence, converging pointwise a.e. to \(|g|^q\text{.}\) Hence, by the Monotone Convergence Theorem,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}\int_X |g|^q\,d\mu = \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \int_X |g_n|^q\,d\mu \leq \|\varphi\|^q_{\mathrm{op}}. \end{equation*}
    Hence, \(g \in L^q(X,\mu)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take any \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{.}\) The sequence \((f\chi_{E_n})_n\) converges pointwise to \(f\text{.}\) By Hölders inequality, \(fg_n\) is integrable for all \(n\text{,}\) and \(fg\) is integrable. It follows from the Lebesgue Dominated Convergence Theorem, that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow n} \int_X fg_n\,d\mu = \int_X fg \,d\mu. \end{equation*}
    Also, again by the Lebesgue Dominated Convergence Theorem,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}\|f - f\chi_{E_n}\|_p = \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty}\left( \int_X |f-f\chi_{E_n}|^p \right)^{1/p} = 0. \end{equation*}
    Hence, by the continuity of \(\varphi\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \varphi(f) &= \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \varphi(f \chi_{E_n}) \\ &= \lim_{n\rightarrow \infty} \int_X fg_n \,d\mu\\ &= \int_X fg \,d\mu. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Therefore \(\varphi = T_g\text{,}\) completing the proof for the \(\sigma\)-finite case.
    🔗
    Now let \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) be an arbitrary measure space. Again, if \(E \subseteq X\) is measurable we embed \(L^q(E,\mu)\) into \(L^q(X,\mu)\) by setting functions to be \(0\) on \(X \backslash E\text{.}\) By the above argument, if \(E \subseteq X\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, then there is a \(g_E\in L^q(E,\mu)\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \varphi(f) = \int_{E_n} fg_E\,d\mu \end{equation*}
    for all \(f \in L^p(E,\mu).\) Note that, by the uniqueness of \(g_E\text{,}\) if \(E\) and \(F\) are \(\sigma\)-finite with \(E \subseteq F\) then \(g_F|_E = g_E\) almost everywhere. Thus \(\|g_F\|_q \geq \|g_E\|_q.\) Let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} M = \sup \{ \|g_E\|_q \colon E\subseteq X\ \sigma\text{-finite}\}\leq \|\varphi\|_\mathrm{op}. \end{equation*}
    Take a sequence \(\{E_n\}_n\) of \(\sigma\)-finite sets, such that \(\|g_{E_n}\|_q\|\) converges to \(M\text{,}\) and let \(F = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k.\) Then \(F\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, and \(\|g_F\|_q \geq \|g_{E_n}\|_q\) for all \(n\text{.}\)\ Thus \(\|g_F\|_q = M.\) Suppose now that \(A\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, with \(F \subseteq A\text{.}\) Then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \int_X |g_A|^q\,d\mu = \int_X |g_F|^q\,d\mu + \int_X |g|^q_{A\backslash F}\,d\mu \leq M^q = \int_X |g_F|^q. \end{equation*}
    Thus \(\|g_{A\backslash F}\|_q = 0,\) and so \(g_A = g_F\) almost everywhere. If \(f \in L^p(X,\mu)\text{,}\) then by , the set
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} B = \{x \in X \colon f(x) \neq 0\} \end{equation*}
    is \(\sigma\)-finite. Thus, the set \(A = B \cup F\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \varphi(f) &= \int_X f g_A \,d\mu \\ &= \int_X f g_F\,d\mu. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\varphi = T_g\text{,}\) completing the proof for \(1<p<\infty.\)
    🔗
    When \(p=1\) and \(q = \infty\) the proof follows a similar vein of reasoning (when we assume \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is \(\sigma\)-finite). The details are left as an exercise, Exericse~\ref{exer: riesz rep p=1}.

    Remark 11.2.5.

    shows that in order for the dual of \(L^1(X,\mu)\) to be (isometrically isomorphic to) \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{,}\) it is sufficient that \(\mu\) be \(\sigma\)-finite. But is it necessary that \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite? The proof relies on two key steps: first we show in , that if \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, then the map \(g \mapsto T_g\) is a norm preserving map from \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\) to \((L^1(X,\mu))^*\text{;}\) then in we show that the map is surjective.
    🔗
    You will notice, that the proof of does not fully rely on the fact that \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite. Indeed, the property we use is that if \(\mu(A)>0\text{,}\) then there is a \(B \subseteq A\) with \(0<\mu(B)<\infty\text{.}\) Measure spaces with this property are called semi-finite. Whilst all \(\sigma\)-finite measure spaces are semi-finite, not all semi-finite measure spaces are \(\sigma\)-finite. E.g., if \(X\) is an uncountable set and \(c\) is the counting measure on all the subsets of \(X\text{,}\) then \((X,P(X),c)\) is semi-finite but not \(\sigma\)-finite.
    🔗
    The proof of surjectivity in also does not tell the whole story. In order for the map \(g \mapsto T_g\) to be surjective, a necessary and sufficient condition is that the measure space is localizable. Other than to say that being localizable is a stronger condition than being semi-finite, but a weaker condition than being \(\sigma\)-finite, we will say no more on the topic. We refer the interested reader to the treatise of Fremlin [7].
    🔗

In , an example is given of a measure space which is not \(\sigma\)-finite, but the dual of \(L^1(X,\mu)\) is \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{.}\) In , an example is given where the dual of \(L^1(X,\mu)\) is not \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{.}\)

    Subsection 11.2.1 The dual of \(\ell^\infty\)

    Notably absent from our discussions so far is the dual of \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\text{.}\) The dual of \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\) can be described, but the description is not as neat as for the dual of \(L^p(X,\mu)\) when \(1<p<\infty\text{.}\) In this subsection we will briefly discuss the dual of \(\ell^\infty\text{.}\) We leave the general theory of the dual of \(L^\infty(X,\mu)\) to a series of exercises: , , and .
    🔗

    Example 11.2.6.

    Let \(c_0\) be the collection of all sequences of real numbers that converge to \(0\text{.}\) In we establish that \(c_0\) is a separable Banach subspace for \(\ell^\infty\text{.}\) For \(a = (a_n)_n \in \ell^1\text{,}\) we define a linear functional \(T_a\) on \(c_0\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} T_a((b_n)_n) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty a_nb_n. \end{equation*}
    shows that the map
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} T \colon & \ell^1 \rightarrow (c_0)^* \\ & a \mapsto T_a \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    is an isometric bijection. That is, \((c_0)^* \simeq \ell^1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    In we show that if \(\varphi \in (\ell^\infty)^*\) then there is an \(a \in \ell^1\) and a \(\psi \in (\ell^\infty)^*\) with \(\psi(b) = 0\) for all \(b \in c_0\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \varphi = T_a + \psi. \end{equation*}
    It is reasonable to believe that there are non-zero elements \(\psi \in (\ell^\infty)^*\) with \(\psi|_{c_0} = 0\text{.}\) After all, \(\ell^\infty\) is a not separable by , and \(c_0\) is a separable by . That \(\ell^\infty\) is larger than \(c_0\) suggests that \((\ell^\infty)^*\) should be larger than \((c_0)^*\text{.}\) Exhibiting \(\psi\) with this property is between difficult and impossible. However, with the Hahn-Banach theorem, a significant theorem in the field of Functional Analysis, one can show that such \(\psi\) exist. Thus \((\ell^\infty)^*\) is not the same as \(\ell^1\) under the standard isomorphism \(T\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    In fact \((\ell^\infty)^*\) is much larger than \(\ell^1\text{.}\) We know that \(\ell^1\) is separable and that \(\ell^\infty\) is not separable. That \(\ell^\infty\) is not separable guarantees that \((\ell^\infty)^*\) is also not separable, and so \((\ell^\infty)^*\) cannot be the same as \(\ell^1\) under any map. There are a few ways to show that \((\ell^\infty)^*\) is not separable, but all fall outside the remit of this course. We quickly outline the details of two ways in the following remarks, one using topology and one using functional analysis.

    These details are included for your edification and to whet your appetite for further study. Don’t stress too much if the details are confusing.

    Remark 11.2.7.

    Consider the natural numbers \(\mathbb{N}\) with the discrete topology, i.e. the topology where every subsets of \(\mathbb{N}\) is open. Using the Stone-\u{C}ech compactification from topology, it can be shown that there is a compact Hausdorff space \(\beta\mathbb{N}\) containing a homeomorphic copy of \(\mathbb{N}\text{,}\) such that every bounded function on \(\mathbb{N}\) extends uniquely to a continuous bounded function on \(\beta\mathbb{N}\text{.}\) The bounded functions on \(\mathbb{N}\) are simply \(\ell^\infty\text{.}\) Thus, the Stone-\u{C}ech compactification shows that \(\ell^\infty \simeq C_b(\beta\mathbb{N})\text{.}\) For each \(x \in \beta\mathbb{N}\) we can define a linear functional \(\phi_x\) on \(C_b(\beta\mathbb{N})\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \phi_x(f) = f(x). \end{equation*}
    One can show that, if \(x\neq y\text{,}\) then \(\|\phi_x - \phi_y\|_\mathrm{op} = 1.\) The construction of \(\beta\mathbb{N}\) guarantees that \(\beta\mathbb{N}\) is uncountable. Using an argument similar to the proof of , we can use the uncountable collection \(\{\phi_x\}_{x\in\beta\mathbb{N}}\) to show that \((\ell^\infty)^*\) is not separable.

    Remark 11.2.8.

    With functional analysis tools, we can say more. One can show that if \(X\) is a Banach space which is not separable, then the dual space \(X^*\) is also not separable. Proving that the dual of a non-separable space is non-separable relies on the Hahn-Banach theorem.
    🔗
    Thus, since \(\ell^\infty\) is not separable, we cannot have that \(\ell^1\) is isomorphic to \((\ell^\infty)^*\text{.}\) We also know that \(L^\infty([0,1],m)\) is not separable by , and thus \((L^\infty([0,1],m)^*\) is not separable. As \(L^1([0,1],m)\) is separable, by , we must have that \(L^1([0,1],m)\not\simeq (L^\infty([0,1],m))^*.\)
