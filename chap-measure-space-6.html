    Exercises 2.4 Exercises

    1.

    Let \(X\) be an uncountable set. A set \(A \subseteq X\) is cocountable if its complement \(A^c\) is countable.
    🔗
    1. Show that the collection \(\mathcal{M}\) of all subsets of \(X\) which are either countable or cocountable forms a \(\sigma\)-algebra.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Define a function \(\mu \colon \mathcal{M} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\) by \(\mu(A) = 0\) if \(A\) is countable, and \(\mu(A) = 1\) if \(A\) is cocountable. Show that \(\mu\) is a measure.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Prove the claims in Example 2.1.14. That is, show that the counting measure \(c\) on a non-empty set \(X\) defines a complete measure.
    🔗
    Show that \((X,\mathcal{P}(X),c)\) is \(\sigma\)-finite if and only if \(X\) is countable. Show that \((X,\mathcal{P}(X),c)\) is a finite measure space if and only if \(X\) is finite.
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set with the discrete topology. Let \(c\) be the counting measure on \((X,\mathcal{P}(X))\text{.}\) Show that \(c\) is a Borel measure.
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{F})\) be a measurable space. Fix \(x \in X\text{,}\) and let \(\delta_x\) be the Dirac measure defined in Example 2.1.15. Show that \(\delta_x\) is a measure.
    🔗
    Suppose \(X\) is a topological space, and \(\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{B}(X)\) is the Borel sets. Show that \(\delta_x\) is a Borel measure.
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    Let \(\overline{\mathbb{R}} = \mathbb{R}\cup \{-\infty, \infty\}\) be the extended real line. Let \(\overline{\mathcal{B}}\) be the collection of sets in \(\overline{\mathbb{R}}\) given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \overline{\mathcal{B}} = \{ A \subseteq \overline{\mathbb{R}} \colon A \cap \mathbb{R} \in \mathcal{B}\}, \end{equation*}
    where \(\mathcal{B}\) is the collection of Borel sets in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) Show that \(\overline{\mathcal{B}}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra.
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Let \(\overline{\mathbb{R}} = \{-\infty, \infty\}\) be the extended real line. For \(U \subseteq \overline{\mathbb{R}}\) we say \(U\) is open in \(\overline{\mathbb{R}}\) if it satisfies the following conditions
    🔗
    1. \(U \cap \mathbb{R}\) is open in \(\mathbb{R}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(\infty \in U\text{,}\) then there exists \(n \in \mathbb{R}\) such that \((n,\infty) \subseteq U\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. \(-\infty \in U\text{,}\) then there exists \(n \in \mathbb{R}\) such that \((-\infty,n) \subseteq U\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Show that this defines a topology on \(\overline{\mathbb{R}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Let \(\overline{\mathbb{R}} = \{-\infty, \infty\}\text{.}\) Let \(\overline{\mathcal{B}}\) be the \(\sigma\)-algebra from Exercise 2.4.5. Endow \(\overline{\mathbb{R}}\) with the topology from Exercise 2.4.6. Show that the \(\sigma\)-algebra of Borel sets in \(\overline{\mathbb{R}}\text{,}\) \(\mathcal{B}(\overline{\mathbb{R}})\text{,}\) conincides with \(\overline{\mathcal{B}}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    8.

    Prove Theorem 2.2.3.
    🔗
    🔗

    9.

    Let \((Y,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) be a measure space, and let \((Z,\mathcal{G})\) be a measurable space. Let \(f \colon Y \rightarrow Z\) be a function satisfying \(f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}\) for all \(B \in \mathcal{G}\text{.}\) Define a function \(f_*\mu\) on \(\mathcal{G}\) by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f_*\mu(A) = \mu(f^{-1}(A)). \end{equation*}
    1. Show that \(f_*\mu\) is a measure on \((Z,\mathcal{G})\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. Show that \(\mu(Y) = f_*\mu(Y)\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    10.

    Prove the claim in Example 2.3.4: Let \(X\) be an infinite set, and let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the collection of all finite subsets of \(X\text{.}\) Let \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty)\) be the counting function. Let \(\mu^*\) be the outer measure induced by \(\mu\text{.}\) Show that \(\mu^*=c\text{,}\) the counting measure on \(X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    11.

    Prove the claim in Example 2.3.5: Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the collection of all open intervals in the real line. Let \(\mu\) be the length function on \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) i.e. \(\mu(I) = \ell(I)\) for all \(I \in \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Let \(\mu^*\) be the outer measure induced by \(\mu\text{.}\) Show that \(\mu^*(I) = \ell(I)\) for all intervals \(I\) in \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    12.

    Let \(\mu^*\) be an outer measure on a non-empty set \(X\text{.}\) Show that if \(\mu^*(A) = 0\) then \(A\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable. (Note that this completes the proof of Theorem 2.3.7).
    🔗
    🔗

    13.

    Show that the collections \(\mathcal{I}_1\text{,}\) \(\mathcal{I}_2\) and \(\mathcal{I}_3\) from Example 2.3.11 form a semirings of subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\) That is show that the collection \(\mathcal{I}_1\) of all intervals in \(\mathbb{R}\text{;}\) the colletion \(\mathcal{I}_2\) of all intervals of finite length; and \(\mathcal{I}_3\) of all intervals of the form \((a,b]\text{,}\) \(a\leq b\text{,}\) for semirings.
    🔗
    🔗

    14.

    Show that the collection \(\mathcal{S}\) from Example 2.3.12 forms a semiring. That is, if \((X,\mathcal{M}, \mu)\) and \((Y, \mathcal{N}, \nu)\) are two measure spaces and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{S} = \{ A \times B \colon A \in \mathcal{M}, \ B \in \mathcal{N}\}, \end{equation*}
    show that \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring of subsets of \(X \times Y\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    15.

    Let \(X\) be an uncountable set and let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the collection of all countable subsets of \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Define a function \(\mu\) on \(\mathcal{S}\) by \(\mu(A) = 0\) for all \(A \subseteq \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Let \(\mu^*\) be the outer measure induced by \(\mu\text{.}\) Determine \(\mu^*\) for each \(A \subseteq X\text{.}\) Determine which sets are \(\mu^*\)-measurable. Note that the \(0\) measure \(\nu(A) = 0\) for all \(A \subseteq X\) is also an extension of \(\mu\text{.}\) Why does this not contradict the Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem (Theorem 2.3.16)?
    🔗
    🔗

    16.

    Let \(\mathcal{I}_1\text{,}\) \(\mathcal{I}_2\) and \(\mathcal{I}_3\) be the semirings of intervals from Exercise 2.4.13. Show that the length function \(\ell\) when restricted to each \(\mathcal{I}_i\) is a premeasure of \(\mathcal{I}_i\text{,}\) for \(i=1,2,3\text{.}\)
    🔗
