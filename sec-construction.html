    Section 2.3 Constructing measures

    The reason for the dearth of examples of measures thus far is simple: measures are complicated. Let \((X,\mathcal{M}, \mu)\) be a measure space. Given \(A \subseteq X\) one may ask
    🔗
    1. Is \(A\) measurable? That is, is \(A \in \mathcal{M}\text{?}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(A\) is measurable, what is the value of \(\mu(A)\text{?}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    In general, neither of these questions are easy to answer. However, as we will see, we can make do without having a full answer to these questions. More commonly, we will want \(\mu\) to take some prescribed values on a subset \(\mathcal{S}\) of \(\mathcal{P}(X)\text{,}\) where \(\mathcal{S}\) is not necessarily a \(\sigma\)-algebra. In this section we will see how to construct a measure by defining \(\mu\) on \(\mathcal{S}\) (when \(\mu\) and \(\mathcal{S}\) are sufficiently well behaved).
    🔗
    The process for constructing measures will involve several steps. Starting with a function \(\mu\) on a set of sets \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) we will construct a function \(\mu^*\text{,}\) called an outer measure, on all of \(\mathcal{P}(X)\text{.}\) We will then restrict \(\mu^*\) two a smaller \(\sigma\)-algebra on which it is a measure.
    🔗
    We begin with the definition of an outer measure.
    🔗

    Definition 2.3.1.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set. A function
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^* \colon \mathcal{P}(X) \rightarrow [0,\infty] \end{equation*}
    is an outer measure on \(X\) if
    🔗
    1. \(\mu^*(\emptyset) = 0\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of subsets of \(X\text{,}\) and \(E \subseteq \bigcup_k E_k\) then
      \begin{equation*} \mu^*(E) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(E_k). \end{equation*}
      I.e. \(\mu^*\) is countably monotone.
      🔗
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Outer measures are not as well behaved as measures. However, they have two significant advantages: they are defined on all subsets of \(X\text{,}\) and they are easy to construct.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.2.

    Let \(X\) be a nonempty set, let \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) be a non-empty collection of subsets of \(X\text{,}\) and let \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) be any function. Define a function \(\mu^* \colon \mathcal{P}(X) \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) by
    🔗
    1. \(\mu^*(\emptyset) = 0\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. if \(\emptyset\neq A\text{,}\) and there exists a countable collection \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\) such that \(A \subseteq \bigcup_k E_k\text{,}\) then
      \begin{equation*} \mu^*(A) = \inf \left\{ \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k) \colon \{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty \subseteq \mathcal{S},\ A \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k \right\}; \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    3. if \(\emptyset \neq A\) and \(A\) can not be covered by a countably infinite family in \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) then \(\mu^*(A) = \infty.\)
      🔗
      🔗
    Then \(\mu^*\) is an outer measure on \(X\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    By definition, \(\mu^*(\emptyset) = 0\text{,}\) so we only need to show that \(\mu^*\) is countably monotone. Take subsets \(A\text{,}\) and \(\{A_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) of \(X\) with \(A \subseteq \bigcup_k A_k\text{.}\) If \(\mu^*(A_{k_0}) = \infty\) for some \(k_0\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(A) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(A_k) = \infty. \end{equation*}
    Suppose now that \(\mu^*(A_k)<\infty\) for all \(k\text{.}\) Fix \(\varepsilon >0\text{.}\) For each \(k\text{,}\) choose a countable collection \(\{E_{k,m}\}_{m=1}^\infty\) of subsets of \(\mathcal{S}\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(A_k) > \sum_{m=1}^\infty \mu(E_{k,m}) - \frac{\varepsilon}{2^k}. \end{equation*}
    Since \(A \subseteq \bigcup_k A_k\text{,}\) the collection \(\{E_{k,m}\}_{k,m=1}^\infty\) covers \(A\text{.}\) Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu^*(A) &\leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \sum_{m=1}^\infty \mu(E_{k,m}) \\ &< \sum_{k=1}^\infty [\mu^*(A_k) + \frac{\varepsilon}{2^k}]\\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(A_k) + \varepsilon. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\mu^*(A) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(A_k)\text{,}\) and so \(\mu^*\) is countably monotone.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 2.3.3.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set, and let \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) be a non-empty collection of subsets. Let \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) be a function and let \(\mu^*\) be the outer measure constructed in Theorem Theorem 2.3.2. We call \(\mu^*\) the outer measure induced by \(\mu\).
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(\mu^*\) be the outer measure induced from a set function \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty].\) In general, there is no reason to expect \(\mu^*\) to be well-behaved, beyond being an outer measure. We may not even have \(\mu^*(A) = \mu(A)\) for \(A \in \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) We will see that whether or not \(\mu^*\) preserves nice properties of \(\mu\) (if there are any) will depend on both \(\mu\) and \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) The following two examples give instances when the induced outer measure does end up being useful.
    🔗

    Example 2.3.4.

    Let \(X\) be an infinite set, and let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the collection of all finite subsets of \(X\text{.}\) Let \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty)\) be the counting function. I.e. for \(A \subseteq \mathcal{S}\text{,}\) \(\mu(A)\) is the (necessarily finite) number of elements of \(A\text{.}\) If \(\mu^*\) is the outer measure induced by \(\mu\text{,}\) then \(\mu^*=c\) is the counting measure (Exercise Checkpoint 2.4.10).
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.5.

    Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the collection of all open intervals in the real line. Let \(\mu\) be the length function on \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) i.e. \(\mu(I) = \ell(I)\) for all \(I \in \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Let \(\mu^*\) be the outer measure induced by \(\mu\text{.}\) Then \(\mu^*(I) = \ell(I)\) for all intervals in \(\mathbb{R}\) (Exercised Checkpoint 2.4.11). The outer measure \(\mu^*\) is called the Lebesgue outer measure on \(\mathbb{R}\). We will see (much!) more on this later.
    🔗
    🔗
    Having constructed an outer measure from an arbitrary (set) function, we will now restrict outer measures to measures.
    🔗

    Definition 2.3.6.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set and let \(\mu^*\) be an outer measure on \(X\text{.}\) A set \(E \subseteq X\) is measurable with respect to \(\mu^*\) if
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(A) = \mu^*(A\cap E) + \mu^*(A\cap E^c), \end{equation*}
    for all \(A \subseteq X.\)
    🔗
    The term \(\mu^*\)-measurable is also used for sets which are measurable with respect to \(\mu^*\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.7.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set, let \(\mu^*\) be an outer measure on \(X\text{,}\) and let \(\mathcal{M}\) be the collection of all subsets of \(X\) which are measurable with respect to \(\mu^*\text{.}\) Then \(\mathcal{M}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra and the restriction of \(\mu^*\) to \(\mathcal{M}\) is a measure. That is, \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is a measure space, where \(\mu = \mu^*|_\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) Further, \((X,\mathcal{M},\mu)\) is a complete measure space.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Clearly \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{M}\text{.}\) Also, if \(E\) is measurable with respect to \(\mu^*\text{,}\) then \(E^c\) is also in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) To show that \(\mathcal{M}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra it remains to show that \(\mathcal{M}\) is closed under countable unions. We will do this in tandem with showing that \(\mu^*|_\mathcal{M}\) is a measure.
    🔗
    Let \(E_1\) and \(E_2\) be two \(\mu^*\)-measurable sets. Take any \(A \subseteq X\text{.}\) Then, since \(E_1\) and \(E_2\) are measurable, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu^*(A) = &\mu^*(A \cap E_1) + \mu^*(A \cap E_1^c) \\ = & \mu^*(A \cap E_1) + \mu^*((A \cap E_1^c) \cap E_2) + \mu^*((A \cap E_1^c) \cap E_2^c). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Note that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} [A\cap E_1] \cup [(A\cap E_1^c)\cap E_2] = A\cap (E_1 \cup E_2), \end{equation*}
    and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A \cap E_1^c \cap E_2^c = A \cap (E_1 \cup E_2)^c. \end{equation*}
    Hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu^*(A) = &\mu^*(A \cap E_1) + \mu^*(A \cap E_1^c) \\ = & \mu^*(A \cap E_1) + \mu^*((A \cap E_1^c) \cap E_2) + \mu^*((A \cap E_1^c) \cap E_2^c)\\ \geq & \mu^*(A\cap (E_1 \cup E_2)) + \mu^*(A \cap (E_1 \cup E_2)^c). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Therefore, \(E_1\cup E_2\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable. It follows inductively that if \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^n\) is a finite collection of measurable sets, then \(\bigcup_{k=1}^n E_k\) is measurable.
    🔗
    Let \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^n\) be a finite collection of disjoint measurable sets. Take any set \(A \subseteq X\text{.}\) Since the sets \(\{E_k\}_k\) are disjoint and measurable, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \label{eq: fin count} \begin{split} \mu^*(A) &= \mu^*(A\cap E_1) + \mu^*(A \cap E_1^c)\\ &= \mu^*((A\cap E_1) + \mu^*(A \cap E_2) + \mu^*(A \cap (E_1 \cup E_2)^c) \\ &\vdots \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^n \mu^*(A \cap E_k) + \mu^*\left( A \cap \left(\bigcup_{k=1}^n E_k\right)^c\right). \end{split} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    In particular,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^n E_k\right) = \sum_{k=1}^n \mu^*(E_k). \end{equation*}
    Thus far, we have shown that \(\mathcal{M}\) is closed under finite unions, and \(\mu^*\) is additive on finite collections of disjoint sets in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) As \(\mathcal{M}\) is closed under taking complements, it follows that \(\mathcal{M}\) is also closed under finite intersections. We need to extend finite to infinite in both instances. Let \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) be a countable collection of measurable sets and let \(E = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k.\) We will use our favorite trick to ``disjointize" this collection. Though we have used this method before, we will go through the steps carefully to make sure that at each step we remain with a set in \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) Let \(F_1 = E_1\text{,}\) and \(F_2 = E_2 \backslash F_1\text{.}\) Note that \(E_2 \backslash F_1= E_2 \cap F_1^c \in \mathcal{M}.\) Continuing in this way, for each \(n\) let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} F_n = E_n \backslash \bigcup_{k=1}^{n-1}F_k. \end{equation*}
    Again, we have that \(F_n \in \mathcal{M}\) for each \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) Thus \(\{F_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is a countable collection of disjoint measurable sets. Further \(\bigcup_{k=1}^n F_k = \bigcup_{k=1}^n E_k\) for \(n \in \mathbb{N} \cup\{\infty\}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Fix \(n\text{,}\) and take any \(A \subseteq X\text{.}\) Let \(B = \bigcup_{k=1}^n E_k\text{.}\) Then, by the measurability of \(B\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu^*(A) = & \mu^*(A \cap B) + \mu^*(A\cap B^c)\\ =& \sum_{k=1}^n \mu^*(A\cap F_k) + \mu^*(A\cap B^c)\\ \geq& \sum_{k=1}^n \mu^*(A\cap F_k) + \mu^*(A\cap E^c), \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    where the second inequality comes from [cross-reference to target(s) "eq-fin-count" missing or not unique] and the last inequality holds since \(B \subseteq E\text{.}\) As this holds for all \(n\text{,}\) the countable monotonicity of the outer measure \(\mu^*\) gives
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu^*(A) \geq & \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(A\cap F_k) + \mu^*(A\cap E^c)\\ \geq & \mu^*(A\cap E) + \mu^*(A \cap E^c). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    It follows that \(E\) is measurable.
    🔗
    We have thus shown that \(\mathcal{M}\) is a \(\sigma\)-algebra. It remains to complete the proof that \(\mu^*\) is a measure when restricted to \(\mathcal{M}\text{.}\) It only remains to show that \(\mu^*\) is countably additive on disjoint measurable sets. Let \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) be a disjoint collection of measurable sets and let \(E = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k\text{.}\) If \(\mu^*(E) = \infty\text{,}\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(E) \geq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(E_k), \end{equation*}
    and so \(\mu^*(E) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(E_k)\text{.}\) Suppose now that \(\mu^*(E) < \infty\text{.}\) For every \(n\) we have, by the measurability of \(E\) and the finite additivity of \(\mu^*\) on disjoint sets,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu^*(E) = \sum_{k=1}^n \mu^*(E_k) + \mu^*\left(E \backslash \bigcup_{k=n+1}^\infty E_k \right) \\ \geq \sum_{k=1}^n \mu^*(E_k). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    As this holds for all \(n\text{,}\) we have that \(\mu^*(E) \geq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(E_k)\text{.}\) Hence \(\mu^*(E) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu^*(E_k)\text{.}\) Thus \((X, \mathcal{M}, \mu^*|_\mathcal{M})\) is a measure space. That this measure space is complete is left as an exercise (Exercise Checkpoint 2.4.12).
    🔗
    🔗
    Starting we a function \(\mu_0\) on some set \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) we can now construct a measure.
    🔗
    1. Start with \(\mu_0\) on \(\mathcal{S}\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. let \(\mu^*\) be the outer-measure induced by \(\mu_0\) (Theorem Theorem 2.3.2);
      🔗
      🔗
    3. let \(\mu\) be the measure we get by restricting \(\mu^*\) to the \(\mu^*\)-measurable sets (Theorem Theorem 2.3.7).
      🔗
      🔗
    We call \(\mu\) the Carathéodory measure induced by \(\mu_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now that we can construct the Carathéodory measure \(\mu\) induced by a set function \(\mu_0 \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty]\text{,}\) we can ask how \(\mu_0\) and \(\mu\) relate. In particular, when are we guaranteed that each set \(A \in \mathcal{S}\) is measurable with respect to \(\mu\text{?}\) And if the sets in \(\mathcal{S}\) are measurable, is \(\mu|_{\mathcal{S}} = \mu_0\text{?}\) As stated earlier, the answers to these questions rely both on \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(\mu_0\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 2.3.8.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set and let \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) be a non-empty collection of subsets of \(X\text{.}\) A function \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) is a premeasure if
    🔗
    1. \(\mu\) is finitely additive, i.e. if \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^n\) is a disjoint collection of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\) whose union is also in \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) then \(\mu\left(\bigcup_{k=1}^n E_k\right) = \sum_{k=1}^n \mu(E_k)\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. \(\mu\) is countably monotone, i.e. if \(E,\ \{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are in \(\mathcal{S}\) and \(E \subseteq \bigcup_k E_k\text{,}\) then \(\mu(E) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k)\text{;}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    3. if \(\emptyset \in S\text{,}\) then \(\mu(\emptyset) =0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Let \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty]\text{.}\) As any measure is a premeasure, in necessary condition for the Carathéodory measure induced by \(\mu\) to agree with \(\mu\) on \(\mathcal{S}\) is for \(\mu\) to be a premeasure. With some additional structure on \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) we can show that the Carathéodory measure is an extension of a premeasure. When this happens, we call the Carathéodory measure a Carathéodory extension.
     1 
    It may be the case that there are more measures than the Carathéodory extension which extend a premeasure \(\mu\text{.}\) However, we will see in Theorem Theorem 2.3.9 that Carathéodory is sometimes unique.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.9.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set. Let \(\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(X)\) be a non-empty collection of subseteq which is closed under relative complements. That is, if \(A,B \in \mathcal{S}\text{,}\) then \(A \backslash B \in \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Let \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) be a premeasure, and let \(\overline{\mu}\) be the Carathéodory measure induced by \(\mu\text{.}\) Then each \(A \in \mathcal{S}\) is \(\overline{\mu}\)-measurable, and \(\overline{\mu}(A) = \mu(A)\text{.}\) That is, \(\overline{\mu}\) is the Carathéodory extenion on \(\mu\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Take \(A \in \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) We will show that \(A\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable, where \(\mu^*\) is the outer measure induced by \(\mu\text{.}\) Take any \(E \in X\text{.}\) We need to show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(E) = \mu^*(E\cap A) + \mu^*(E \cap A^c). \end{equation*}
    If \(\mu^*(E) = \infty\) then this equality clearly holds. Thus we will suppose that \(\mu^*(E) < \infty.\) It suffices to show that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(E) = \mu^*(E\cap A) + \mu^*(E \cap A^c). \end{equation*}
    Fix a \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) By the definition of \(\mu^*\text{,}\) and since \(\mu^*(E)<\infty\text{,}\) there exists a countable collection \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) such that \(E \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(E) + \varepsilon \geq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k). \end{equation*}
    Note that, \(E_k \cap A = E_k \backslash [E_k\backslash A]\text{,}\) and hence \(E_k \cap A \in \mathcal{S}\) for each \(k\text{.}\) Further, \(E_k \cap A^c = E_k \backslash A\text{,}\) and so \(E \cap A^c \in \mathcal{S}\) for each \(k\text{.}\) By the countable additivity of \(\mu\) we thus have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu(E_k) = \mu(E_k \cap A) + \mu(E_k \cap A^c). \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k) =\sum_{k=1}^\infty (\mu(E_k \cap A) + \mu(E_k \cap A^c)). \end{equation*}
    As \(\{E_k \cap A\}_{k=1}^\infty\) covers \(E \cap A\) and \(\{E_k \cap A^c\}_{k=1}^\infty\) covers \(E \cap A^c\) we have that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k\cap A) &\geq \mu^*(E\cap A) \text{ and}\\ \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k\cap A^c) &\geq \mu^*(E\cap A^c). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Thus
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu^*(E) + \varepsilon &\geq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k \cap A) + \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k \cap A^c) \\ &\geq \mu^*(E\cap A) + \mu^*(E \cap A^c). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    As this holds for all \(\varepsilon >0\text{,}\) it follows that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(E) = \mu^*(E\cap A) + \mu^*(E \cap A^c). \end{equation*}
    Thus \(A\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable.
    🔗
    That \(\mu^*(E)=\mu(E)\) for all \(E \in \mathcal{S}\) follows since \(\mu\) is countably monotone. Thus the Carathéodory measure induced by \(\mu\) is an extension of \(\mu\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    The conditions on \(\mathcal{S}\) in Theorem Theorem 2.3.9 are not quite what we will have practical use for. We instead will look at semirings of subsets.
    🔗

    Definition 2.3.10.

    Let \(X\) be a non-empty set and let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a non-empty collection of subsets of \(X\text{.}\) The collection \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring (of subsets of \(X\)) if for each \(A,B \in \mathcal{S}\)
    🔗
    1. \(A \cap B \in \mathcal{S}\text{;}\) and
      🔗
      🔗
    2. there exists a finite disjoint collection \(\{C_k\}_{k=1}^n\) of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\) such that
      \begin{equation*} A \backslash B = \bigcup_{k=1}^n C_k. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    Note that if \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring, then \(\emptyset \in \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Indeed, if \(A \in \mathcal{S}\) then \(\emptyset = A \backslash A\) can be written as a disjoint union of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) These sets are necessarily the empty set.
    🔗

    Example 2.3.11.

    Let \(\mathcal{I}_1\) be the collection of all intervals in \(\mathbb{R}\text{;}\) let \(\mathcal{I}_2\) be the collection of all intervals of finite length in \(\mathbb{R}\text{;}\) and let \(\mathcal{I}_3\) be the collection of intervals of the form
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{I}_3 = \{ (a,b] \colon a \leq b\}. \end{equation*}
    Then each of \(\mathcal{I}_1\text{,}\) \(\mathcal{I}_2\text{,}\) and \(\mathcal{I}_3\) are semirings of subsets of \(\mathbb{R}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.3.12.

    Let \((X,\mathcal{M}, \mu)\) and \((Y, \mathcal{N}, \nu)\) be two measure spaces. Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be the collection of subsets of \(X \times Y\) given by
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{S} = \{ A \times B \colon A \in \mathcal{M}, \ B \in \mathcal{N}\}. \end{equation*}
    Then \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring. The elements of \(\mathcal{S}\) are called measurable rectangles. More on this later, when we look at product measures.
    🔗
    🔗

    Lemma 2.3.13.

    Let \(\mathcal{S}\) be a semiring of subsets of a non-empty subset \(X\) and let
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mathcal{S}' = \left\{ \bigcup_{k=1}^n C_k : \{C_k\}_{k=1}^n \text{ finite disjoint collection in }\mathcal{S}\right\}. \end{equation*}
    Then \(\mathcal{S}'\) is closed under relative complements. Further, if \(\mu\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}\) then \(\mu\) extends uniquely to a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Note that \(\mathcal{S}\) is closed under finite intersections. It follows that \(\mathcal{S}'\) is also closed under finite intersections. Now take \(A, B \in \mathcal{S}\text{.}\) We can write \(A \cup B\) as the disjoint union
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} A \cup B = (A \backslash B) \cup (A\cap B) \cup (B\backslash A). \end{equation*}
    As each of the three terms can be written as disjoint unions of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) it follows that \(A \cup B \in \mathcal{S}'\text{.}\) Inductively, it can be shown that \(\mathcal{S}'\) is closed under finite unions.
    🔗
    Let \(\{A_i\}_{i=1}^m\) and \(\{B_j\}_{j=1}^n\) be finite disjoint collections of sets from \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) We have that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \left[ \bigcup_{i=1}^m A_i \right] \backslash \left[ \bigcup_{j=1}^n B_j \right] = \bigcup_{j=1}^m \left[ \bigcap_{i=1}^n (A_i \backslash B_j) \right]. \end{equation*}
    Thus, relative complements of sets in \(\mathcal{S}'\) can be written as finite unions of finite intersections of sets in \(\mathcal{S}'\text{.}\) As \(\mathcal{S}'\) is closed under finite unions and finite intersections, it follows that \(\mathcal{S}'\) is closed under relative complements.
    🔗
    Suppose now that \(\mu\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) For disjoint sets \(\{A_i\}_{i=1}^m\) in \(\mathcal{S}\) define
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu'\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^m A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^m \mu(A_i). \end{equation*}
    We must first show that \(\mu'\) is well-defined on \(\mathcal{S}'\text{.}\) To this end, suppose \(\{A_i\}_{i=1}^m\) and \(\{B_j\}_{j=1}^n\) are finite disjoint collections in \(\mathcal{S}\) with \(\bigcup_{i=1}^m A_i = \bigcup_{j=1}^n B_j\text{.}\) Note that, for each \(j\text{,}\) \(B_j = \bigcup_{i=1}^m (B_j \cap A_i)\) is a disjoint union of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) By the finite additivity of \(\mu\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu(B_j) = \sum_{i=1}^m \mu(B_j \cap A_i). \end{equation*}
    Similarly, for each \(i\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu(A_i) = \sum_{j=1}^n (B_j \cap A_i). \end{equation*}
    Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \sum_{j=1}^n \mu(B_j) &= \sum_{j=1}^n \sum_{i=1}^m \mu(B_j \cap A_i) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^m \sum_{j=1}^n \mu(B_j \cap A_i) \\ &= \sum_{i=1}^m \mu(A_i). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\mu'\) is a well-defined function on \(\mathcal{S}'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    That \(\mu'\) is finitely additive is clear. To show that \(\mu'\) is a premeasure, it remains to show countable monotonicity. To this end, take a countable collection \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) in \(\mathcal{S}'\text{,}\) and \(E \in \mathcal{S}'\) such that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} E \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k. \end{equation*}
    Note that, since \(\mathcal{S}'\) is closed under finite unions and relative complements, we can assume that the collection \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is disjoint. As sets in \(\mathcal{S}'\) are disjoint unions of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) can further assume that the sets \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are in \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Let \(\{A_i\}_{i=1}^m\) be disjoint sets in \(\mathcal{S}\) such that \(E = \bigcup_{i=1}^m A_i\text{.}\) By the countable monotonicity of \(\mu\) we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu(A_i) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(A_i \cap E_k) \end{equation*}
    for each \(1\leq i \leq m\text{.}\) Hence
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu'(E) & = \sum_{i=1}^m \mu(A_i) \\ &\leq \sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(A_i \cap E_k)\\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \sum_{i=1}^m \mu(A_i \cap E_k)\\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E \cap E_k) \\ &\leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu'(E_k). \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    Hence \(\mu'\) is a premeasure on \(\mathcal{S}'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The uniqueness of \(\mu'\) follows from the definition of \(\mathcal{S}'\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Let \(\mu_0\) be a function on a collection of subsets \(\mathcal{S}\) of \(X\text{.}\) Denote by \(\mathcal{S}_\sigma\) the collection of all countable unions of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\text{;}\) and denote by \(\mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) the collection of all countable intersections of sets in \(\mathcal{S}_\sigma\text{.}\) Note that, if the sets in \(\mathcal{S}\) are measurable with respect to the Carathéodory measure \(\mu\) induced by \(\mu_0\) (in particular, if \(\mu\) is an extension of \(\mu_0\)), then the sets in \(\mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) are also measurable. The following proposition tells us that the sets in \(\mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) can often be used to approximate the outer measure \(\mu^*\) (and indeed the measure \(\mu\)).
    🔗

    Remark 2.3.14.

    The \(\sigma\delta\)-notation used above is supposed to be reminiscent of \(G_\sigma\) and \(F_\delta\) notation used in topology: \(G_\sigma\)-sets are countable unions of open sets; \(F_\delta\)-sets are countable intersections of closed sets.
     2 
    `G’ for `open’ comes from the German word geöffnet; `F’ for `closed’ comes from the French word fermé.
    When working with measures on topological spaces we will often use \(G_\delta\) and \(F_\sigma\) sets in the way \(S_{\sigma\delta}\) sets are used in the following proposition.
    🔗
    🔗

    Proposition 2.3.15.

    Let \(\mu\) be a set function on a collection \(\mathcal{S}\) of subsets of \(X\text{,}\) and let \(\mu^*\) be the induced outer measure, and let \(\mu\) be the induced Carathéodory measure.
    🔗
    If \(E \subseteq X\) satisfies \(\mu^*(E)<\infty\) then there exists an \(A \in \mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) such that \(E \subseteq A\) and \(\mu^*(E) = \mu^*(A)\text{.}\) If \(E\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable and each set in \(\mathcal{S}\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable, then \(A\) is \(\mu^*\)-measurable and \(\mu(A \backslash E) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    Suppose \(\mu^*(E) < \infty\text{.}\) By the definition of \(\mu^*\text{,}\) for each \(n\) there exists a countable collection \(\{E_{n,k}\}_{k=1}^\infty\) such that \(E \subseteq \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_{n,k}\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(E) + \frac{1}{n} > \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_{n,k}). \end{equation*}
    Let \(A_n = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_{n,k}\text{.}\) Then \(A_n \in \mathcal{S}_\sigma\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu^*(A_n) \leq \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E_k) < \mu^*(E) + \frac{1}{n}. \end{equation*}
    Now let \(A = \bigcap_{n=1}^\infty A_n\text{.}\) Then \(A \in \mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\text{,}\) and \(E \subseteq A\text{.}\) Further, for each \(n\text{,}\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \mu^*(E) &\leq \mu^*(A) \\ &\leq \mu^*(A_n) \\ &< \mu^*(E) + \frac{1}{n}. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    As this holds for all \(n\text{,}\) it follows that \(\mu^*(E) = \mu^*(A)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, if \(\mu\) extends \(\mu_0\) then every set in \(\mathcal{S}\) is measurable, and thus every set in \(\mathcal{S}_\sigma\) and \(\mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) is measurable. If \(E\) is also measurable, then by the excision property
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \mu(E\backslash A) = \mu(E) - \mu(A) = 0. \end{equation*}
    \qedhere
    🔗
    🔗
    We are now ready for the main theorem of this section: our main tool for constructing measures. The uniqueness in the following theorem is important and will be used frequently. Before stating the theorem we need one more definition. If \(\mathcal{S}\) is a collection of subsets of \(X\text{,}\) and \(\mu \colon \mathcal{S} \rightarrow [0,\infty]\) is a function, we say that \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finiteif there is a countable collection \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\) with \(\mu(E_k)< \infty\) for all \(k\text{,}\) and \(X = \bigcup_{k=1}^\infty E_k\text{.}\) Note that this definition is consistent with the definition of a \(\sigma\)-finite measure.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.3.16. Carathéodory-Hahn Theorem.

    Let \(\mu_0\) be a premeasure on a semiring \(\mathcal{S}\) of subsets of a non-empty set \(X\text{.}\) Then the Carathéodory measure \(\mu\) induced by \(\mu_0\) is an extension of \(\mu_0\text{.}\) Furthermore, if \(\mu_0\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, then \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite and \(\mu\) is the unique measure on the set of \(\mu^*\)-measurable subsets extending \(\mu_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    That \(\mu\) extends \(\mu_0\) follows from Theorem Theorem 2.3.9 and Lemma Lemma 2.3.13. If \(\mu_0\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, then \(\mu\) is \(\sigma\)-finite since \(\mu\) extends \(\mu_0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    It remains to show that \(\mu\) is unique when \(\mu_0\) is \(\sigma\)-finite. Suppose that \(\nu\) is another measure on the \(\mu\)-measurable sets which extends \(u_0\text{.}\) Suppose \(E \in X\) satisfies \(\mu^*(E) = 0\text{.}\) By the definition of the out measure \(\mu^*\text{,}\) if we fix \(\varepsilon>0\) there exists \(\{E_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) in \(\mathcal{S}\) such that \(E \subseteq \bigcup_k E_k\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu_0(E_k) < \varepsilon. \end{equation*}
    Noting that \(\mu_0(E_k) = \nu(E_k)\) for each \(k\text{,}\) the countable monotonicity of \(\nu\) implies that \(\nu(E) = 0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Take any \(A \in \mathcal{S}_\sigma\) (i.e. \(A\) is a countable union of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\)). As \(\mathcal{S}\) is a semiring, we can write \(A\) as a disjoint countable union of sets in \(\mathcal{S}\text{.}\) Since \(\mu\) and \(\nu\) are countably additive and agree on \(\mathcal{S}\text{,}\) it follows that \(\mu(A) = \nu(A)\text{.}\) If \(A \in \mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) (i.e. \(A\) is a countable intersection of sets in \(\mathcal{S}_\sigma\)), and \(\mu(A)< \infty\text{,}\) then by the continuity of measure, for both \(\mu\) and \(\nu\text{,}\) we have that \(\mu(A) = \nu(A)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Now take \(E_0 \in \mathcal{S}\) with \(\mu_0(E_0)<\infty\text{.}\) Take any \(E \subseteq E_0\text{.}\) We will show that \(\mu(E) = \nu(E)\text{.}\) By Proposition Proposition 2.3.15, there is an \(A \in \mathcal{S}_{\sigma\delta}\) such that \(E\subseteq A\) and \(\mu(A\backslash E) = 0\text{.}\) We can further assume that \(A \subseteq E_0\text{.}\) By the excision property
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \nu(E) = \nu(A) - \nu(A\backslash E). \end{equation*}
    We have already shown that \(\nu(A) = \mu(A)\) and \(\nu(A \backslash E) = \mu(A\backslash E)\text{.}\) And hence \(\nu(E) = \mu(A)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Finally, if \(\mu_0\) is \(\sigma\)-finite, there are sets \(\{X_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) in \(\mathcal{S}\) which cover all of \(X\) and satisfy \(\mu_0(X_k)<\infty\text{.}\) We can further assume that the sets \(\{X_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) are disjoint. Thus, if \(E \subseteq X\) is measurable then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \nu(E) &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \nu(E \cap X_k) \\ &= \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mu(E \cap X_k) \\ &= \mu(E), \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
    thus proving the uniqueness of \(\mu\text{.}\)
